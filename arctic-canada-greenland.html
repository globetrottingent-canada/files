Cuisine Guide

Arctic Canada & Greenland

Inuit, First Nations, Nunavut, Yukon, NWT, and Kalaallit Nunaat — 5,000 years of ecological knowledge built on marine mammals, caribou, and fish at the edge of human survival.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Arctic Canada and Greenland share the oldest and most environmentally extreme food culture in the Americas — a tradition developed over 5,000 years by the Inuit peoples (Kalaallit in Greenland, Inuit across Canada's Nunavut, Northwest Territories, and Yukon) in conditions so harsh that every aspect of food practice is a direct response to survival at minus 50 degrees Celsius in a landscape with no agriculture. The food culture of the circumpolar Arctic is an extraordinarily sophisticated ecological knowledge system built on hunting marine mammals, caribou, and fish, and the techniques of preservation and fermentation that extract maximum nutrition from these resources.

The Inuit food tradition is among the most nutritionally complete in the world despite its apparent monotony to outside eyes — raw and fermented animal foods, marine mammal blubber, and organ meats provide all essential vitamins where no vegetables grow through the long polar night. The fur trade era from the 17th century introduced European foods — bannock, tea, sugar, and flour — that were rapidly adopted and transformed into new hybrid traditions. Bannock, the Scottish flatbread introduced by fur traders, is now so embedded in Indigenous Canadian food culture it's considered traditional. Greenland adds a Danish colonial layer — Danish rule from 1721 brought smørrebrød, pastry, and coffee culture alongside traditional Kalaallit food in modern Nuuk.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Arctic food is defined by fat, fermentation, and the cold itself as a preservation and cooking medium. Marine mammal fat — the blubber of seal, beluga, and bowhead whale — is the caloric foundation of the traditional Arctic diet. Muktuk — frozen raw whale skin and blubber — is eaten with the appreciation a European brings to cheese: different whales produce different flavours and textures, and the quality of muktuk is a matter of considered preference. Arctic char, the cold-water salmonid that thrives in glacial lakes, is eaten raw, frozen, dried, or smoked and considered one of the finest-tasting fish in the world.

Bannock is the most visible bridge between traditional and introduced food cultures — made with flour, fat, and water, fried in a pan or baked over a fire, it is now the comfort food of the Arctic and subarctic. Akutaq, sometimes called Eskimo ice cream, is a traditional preparation of whipped fat mixed with berries, fish, or snow, providing calories and vitamin C in a dessert that could only have been invented in the Arctic. The short summer brings cloudberries, crowberries, and wild herbs — a seasonal counterpoint to the winter diet of preserved meat and fish.

03

At the Table

  • Country food — the Inuit term for traditionally hunted and gathered food — is not merely sustenance but identity, medicine, and culture. Seal, caribou, Arctic char, and beluga muktuk are shared within communities according to customary protocols.
  • Eating is communal and sharing is obligatory — hoarding food while others go hungry is a profound social violation. Successful hunters distribute their catch to the entire community, particularly elders.
  • Raw and frozen food are not emergency measures but preferences — raw frozen caribou, fish eaten like popsicles, and kiviak (fermented birds) are delicacies whose flavour is completely different from cooked equivalents.
  • Bannock, introduced by Scottish fur traders in the 18th century, is now considered a First Nations and Inuit food — a story of cultural absorption running the opposite direction from most colonial food history.
  • Greenland adds Danish layers — colonial presence from the 18th century brought smørrebrød, pastries, and coffee culture alongside traditional Greenlandic food in modern Nuuk.
  • The seasons define everything — short summer brings berries and fish runs; long winter means preserved and frozen meat, seal hunting at breathing holes, and the fat that protects against extreme cold.

Did You Know?

  • Muktuk — the frozen skin and blubber of the beluga or bowhead whale — is one of the most nutritionally dense foods on earth, containing exceptionally high levels of Vitamin C, Vitamin D, and essential fatty acids, essential for preventing scurvy where no plant foods grow for most of the year.
  • Greenland's population of approximately 57,000 people — the world's largest island with one of the smallest populations — has maintained a food culture almost entirely dependent on marine mammals and fish for thousands of years, a diet associated with remarkably low rates of cardiovascular disease.
  • Kiviak — a Greenlandic delicacy made by stuffing a seal carcass with 400–500 little auks, sewing it shut, and fermenting it under rocks for 18 months — is one of the most extreme preserved foods in the world, eaten whole, bones and all, at celebrations.
  • The fur trade from the 17th century onwards introduced bannock, tea, sugar, and flour, which were rapidly adopted and woven into existing food cultures. Bannock became so thoroughly Indigenous in preparation and meaning that most people today consider it a traditional food.
