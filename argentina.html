Cuisine Guide

Argentina

A country that reinvented itself through immigration — Italian and Spanish waves layered onto Pampas cattle culture, asado ritual, and dulce de leche.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Argentine cuisine is the product of a country that largely reinvented itself through immigration. The indigenous Quechua, Guaraní, and Mapuche peoples contributed corn, potatoes, squash, quinoa, and the mate tradition, but it was the extraordinary wave of European immigration between 1880 and 1930 — primarily from Italy and Spain, with significant numbers from Germany, France, and the Middle East — that shaped the Argentina on the plate today. Italian immigrants outnumbered all others, and their culinary legacy is embedded in everyday Argentine life: pasta, pizza, and gnocchi are as native as asado and empanadas.

The Spanish colonial period established the cattle ranching tradition on the vast Pampas grasslands, which would define Argentine identity more than any other single factor. The grass-fed cattle of the Pampas — raised on some of the world's finest pasture — produced a beef of exceptional quality, and the asado culture that developed around it became the central ritual of Argentine social life. Lebanese and Syrian immigrants contributed empanadas' stuffed pastry cousins, visible in the stuffed bread and baked pastry traditions found across the country.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Argentine food is European in technique, South American in ingredient, and utterly its own in spirit. Beef is the obsession and the art — cuts unfamiliar elsewhere, cooked low and slow over wood or charcoal, served simply with chimichurri and salmuera. The asado is not just a cooking method but a ceremony: the asador controls the fire, the timing, and the sequence of cuts with total authority. Offal — mollejas, chinchulines, riñones — is eaten with pleasure as part of the parrillada.

Dulce de leche is the defining sweet flavor of Argentina — a slow-cooked caramel made from sweetened milk that appears in alfajores, medialunas, cakes, and is spread on everything from toast to pancakes. Italian influence shows in the quality of the helado, the ubiquity of pizza, and the Sunday pasta lunch that rivals the asado as a family institution. Malbec from Mendoza is the national wine, and mate is the national drink — a bitter, grassy herb tea shared communally throughout the day.

03

At the Table

  • The asado is the heart of Argentine social life — a Sunday barbecue ritual that can last all afternoon, presided over by the asador with religious seriousness. Showing up hungry is essential.
  • Dinner is eaten late — rarely before 9pm, and often starting at 10pm or later. Restaurants in Buenos Aires don't fill until after 10pm. Lunch is the lighter meal.
  • Mate is not just a drink but a social ritual — shared from a single gourd passed around a group, sipped through a bombilla straw. Offering mate is an act of friendship; refusing it, mildly offensive.
  • Beef is not merely food in Argentina — it is identity. The quality of the grass-fed Pampas cattle is a point of national pride, and overcooking a steak is considered close to a crime.
  • Dulce de leche appears at every meal context — spread on toast, inside medialunas, in alfajores, on pancakes, and eaten directly from the jar. It is Argentina's defining sweet flavor.
  • Italian immigration in the late 19th and early 20th centuries transformed Argentine food — pasta, pizza, and gelato are as embedded in the culture as asado and empanadas.

Did You Know?

  • Argentina consumes more beef per capita than almost any other country on earth — though consumption has declined from a peak of 100kg per person per year, it still hovers around 50kg, nearly double the global average.
  • The empanada varies dramatically by region — Salta uses potatoes and hard-boiled eggs, Tucumán uses only beef and spices, Mendoza adds olives, and each province considers its version superior. The fold pattern on the edge traditionally indicated the filling, so illiterate workers could tell them apart.
  • Malbec — now Argentina's signature grape — is a French variety from Cahors that nearly died out in France after a devastating frost in 1956. Argentine winemakers in Mendoza had been cultivating it since the 1850s and preserved what France almost lost.
  • Yerba mate, the bitter herb used to make the national drink, is consumed in quantities that dwarf coffee in Argentina — the average Argentine drinks 6–8kg of yerba per year, compared to roughly 1kg of coffee.
Food & Drink Reference

The dishes, sweets, and drinks that define the everyday table.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. A ceremonial barbecue of mixed cuts and offal, slow-grilled over wood or charcoal by the asador.
  2. Hand-folded stuffed pastries filled with beef, chicken, or vegetables, varying dramatically by region.
  3. A breaded, fried beef or chicken cutlet, Argentina's everyday comfort food.
  4. A thick stew of corn, beans, and squash, a national dish especially eaten on independence holidays.
  5. Grilled steak served with the tangy, herby chimichurri sauce of parsley, garlic, and vinegar.

More Mains

  1. A grilled chorizo sausage split and served in crusty bread, Argentina's classic asado starter.
  2. A stew of beef, squash, corn, and sometimes fruit like peaches, showing Argentina's sweet-savory range.
  3. Flank steak topped with tomato sauce, cheese, and oregano like a pizza, then grilled or baked.
  4. A sweet-savory corn paste steamed inside its own corn husk, an Andean-influenced dish.
  5. Whole lamb slow-roasted over an open fire, a specialty of Argentina's Patagonian south.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Sweets & Pastries

  1. Two soft cookies sandwiching dulce de leche, often coated in chocolate or powdered sugar.
  2. Thin pancakes rolled around dulce de leche, a beloved everyday dessert.
  3. Small sweet breakfast pastries sold by weight at any Argentine bakery.
  4. A simple dessert of sliced cheese paired with sweet quince paste.

More Sweets

  1. A dense, dark fruit cake spiced and often soaked in liquor.
  2. Creamy rice pudding sweetened and dusted with cinnamon.
  3. A showstopper layered cake of sponge, dulce de leche, meringue, and cream, from the town of Balcarce.
  4. Flaky, savory-sweet biscuits made with lard, a rustic Argentine baking tradition.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Bakery & Bar

  1. Small, sweet glazed croissants, the standard Argentine breakfast pastry.
  2. A grilled chorizo sausage split and served in crusty bread, Argentina's classic asado starter.
  3. A thick slab of provolone cheese grilled until molten and lightly charred.
  4. Toasted bread spread thickly with dulce de leche, a common breakfast.

More Snacks

  1. A shared platter of cured meats, cheeses, and olives, eaten before or alongside drinks.
  2. Fried potatoes tossed or served with tangy chimichurri sauce.
  3. Small, thin crustless sandwiches layered with ham, cheese, or vegetables, a party staple.
  4. An assortment of small sweet pastries sold at any neighbourhood bakery.
