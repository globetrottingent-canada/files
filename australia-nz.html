Australia & New Zealand

BBQ, hāngi, flat white, bush tucker, and pavlova — British settler foundations built over tens of thousands of years of Aboriginal and Māori food knowledge, remade again by waves of immigration.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Australian and New Zealand cuisine share the common roots of British settler-colonial societies built on top of rich indigenous food traditions, shaped by waves of immigration that transformed what were once purely Anglo food cultures into some of the most diverse and exciting in the Southern Hemisphere. Australia was colonised by Britain from 1788, New Zealand from 1840, and both countries inherited the British food tradition of meat, potatoes, and bread. The indigenous peoples of both countries had been eating sophisticated diets for tens of thousands of years before European arrival: Aboriginal Australians had the world's oldest continuous food culture, built on bush tucker — kangaroo, emu, witchetty grubs, quandongs, wattleseed, lemon myrtle — while the Māori of New Zealand developed the hāngi feast tradition and deep knowledge of the Pacific seafood environment.

The 20th century transformed both food cultures through immigration. The post-WWII migration boom brought Italian, Greek, Lebanese, and Chinese communities to Australia's cities, shifting the food culture away from its Anglo-Irish base. The 1970s removal of the White Australia Policy opened both countries to Asian immigration, and Vietnamese, Thai, Chinese, and Indian restaurant traditions transformed everyday eating in Sydney, Melbourne, and Auckland. The 1990s and 2000s saw both countries develop serious fine dining scenes — Melbourne became regarded as one of the world's best restaurant cities. The flat white, developed in both countries simultaneously, became the global coffee standard.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Australian food is casual, outdoor, and produce-driven — a culture that takes its extraordinary natural ingredients (barramundi, mud crab, Tasmanian salmon, Wagyu beef, Margaret River wine) seriously but eats them without ceremony. The BBQ — a flat hotplate grill, not an American charcoal smoker — is the social centre of Australian weekend life. The café culture of Melbourne and Sydney is among the world's finest, built on espresso technique imported by Italian immigrants and refined into the flat white and the long black. Vegemite, the intensely savoury yeast extract, is the taste that most defines Australian identity to outsiders.

New Zealand food has a quieter confidence — a country of 5 million people producing world-class lamb, dairy, seafood, and wine that largely exports its finest produce while eating well at home. The Māori hāngi tradition remains a living food ceremony rather than a tourist attraction, and Māori ingredients — huhu grubs, kawakawa, pāua (abalone), kina (sea urchin), and the kumara sweet potato — are increasingly finding their way into mainstream New Zealand restaurants. The rivalry between Australia and New Zealand over pavlova, the flat white, and Phar Lap is conducted with genuine competitive spirit.

03

At the Table

  • The Australian BBQ — a flat metal hotplate on a gas grill, not charcoal — is the social centrepiece of the Australian weekend. Sausages, lamb chops, and prawns are cooked communally, eaten with bread and tomato sauce (never ketchup).
  • The Māori hāngi is New Zealand's most significant food tradition — a feast cooked by lowering baskets of meat, fish, vegetables, and kumara into an earth pit lined with heated stones, covered and left to steam for hours. It is ceremony as much as cooking.
  • Flat white culture defines both countries' café identity — developed independently in Sydney and Auckland in the late 1980s, it is Australia and New Zealand's contribution to global coffee culture.
  • Vegemite is a genuine cultural marker in Australia — the dark, intensely savoury yeast extract spread on toast is eaten by most Australians from childhood and almost universally rejected by everyone else. It is applied thinly, not thickly.
  • The pavlova dispute — both Australia and New Zealand claim to have invented the meringue dessert named after ballerina Anna Pavlova — is one of the most passionately argued food origin debates in the Southern Hemisphere. Evidence slightly favours New Zealand.
  • Seafood defines the coastal eating culture — Sydney rock oysters, Moreton Bay bugs, Tasmanian salmon, Bluff oysters, and the green-lipped mussel are all world-class products eaten with directness.

Did You Know?

  • Australia produces some of the world's finest wine from Barossa Valley, McLaren Vale, Coonawarra, and Margaret River — Penfolds Grange is consistently rated among the world's greatest red wines. Australian winemakers were among the first to embrace screwcaps over corks, a decision initially mocked internationally and now widely adopted as superior.
  • The meat pie is the closest thing Australia has to a national dish — a hand-sized pastry filled with minced meat and gravy, eaten at football matches and from servo pie warmers. The pie floater — a meat pie floating in a bowl of pea soup — is a South Australian institution.
  • New Zealand lamb is considered among the finest in the world — grass-fed pastoral farming and careful breeding produce exceptional quality exported globally. New Zealand is also the world's largest exporter of dairy products per capita, and its Anchor butter has been a British household staple since the 1880s.
  • Tim Tams — the Australian chocolate biscuit — are consumed at a rate of 35 million packets per year in Australia alone, and the Tim Tam Slam (biting opposite corners and using the biscuit as a straw to suck hot coffee before the chocolate melts) is a ritual practised by virtually every Australian.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Australia

  1. A hand-sized pastry case filled with minced meat and gravy, eaten at football matches and from servo pie warmers across the country.
  2. Meat, fish, vegetables, and kumara lowered in baskets into a heated stone-lined earth pit and steamed for hours — ceremony as much as cooking.
  3. A prized native Australian fish with firm, flaky white flesh, typically grilled or pan-fried.
  4. Slow-roasted leg of lamb served with roast vegetables and mint sauce, the classic Sunday family meal.
  5. Whole prawns grilled on the flat hotplate BBQ, eaten communally with bread at weekend gatherings.

New Zealand

  1. A crisp-shelled, marshmallow-soft meringue dessert topped with whipped cream and fresh fruit, at the centre of a trans-Tasman origin dispute.
  2. Battered fried fish served with thick-cut chips, wrapped in paper and eaten by the water.
  3. Tiny whole whitebait fish bound in egg batter and fried into a delicate patty, a New Zealand seasonal delicacy.
  4. Slow-roasted lamb served with a sharp mint sauce, showcasing New Zealand's famous grass-fed lamb.
  5. Mashed avocado on toasted sourdough, a café brunch dish that spread globally from Australian and New Zealand cafés.

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. A crisp-shelled, marshmallow-soft meringue dessert topped with whipped cream and fresh fruit, at the centre of a trans-Tasman origin dispute.
  2. Squares of sponge cake dipped in chocolate icing and rolled in desiccated coconut.
  3. A chocolate-coated biscuit sandwich filled with chocolate cream, Australia's best-selling biscuit.
  4. A dense, chewy oat and golden syrup biscuit originally sent to WWI soldiers, still baked every ANZAC Day.

More Sweets

  1. A traybake version of the ANZAC biscuit, pressed into a pan and cut into bars.
  2. Vanilla ice cream studded with crunchy honeycomb toffee pieces, a New Zealand favourite flavour.
  3. Soft dumplings poached in a sweet golden syrup sauce, a classic Australian comfort dessert.
  4. A no-bake traybake made from crushed Weet-Bix cereal, chocolate, and butter.

Snacks & Street Food

Classics

  1. A hot meat pie topped with a squeeze of tomato sauce, the everyday Australian snack.
  2. A grilled sausage on a slice of white bread with onions and sauce, sold at hardware stores and community fundraisers.
  3. Toast spread thinly with butter and the intensely savoury yeast extract Vegemite.
  4. White bread spread with butter and covered in rainbow sprinkles, a staple of Australian children's parties.

More Snacks

  1. A meat pie served floating upside-down in a bowl of thick pea soup, a South Australian specialty.
  2. A small, thick pancake served with butter and jam, similar to a Scotch pancake.
  3. A deep-fried pastry roll filled with cabbage, barley, and meat, an Australian take on the spring roll.
  4. Toast spread with Marmite yeast extract and topped with melted cheese.
