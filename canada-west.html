Cuisine Guide

Western Canada

Vancouver, Okanagan, Alberta beef, Prairie Ukrainian, and Pacific salmon — Indigenous foundations transformed by Pacific Rim immigration and European prairie settlement.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

The food cultures of British Columbia, Alberta, and the Prairie provinces span some of the most dramatic contrasts in Canadian eating — from the Pacific Coast's world-class seafood and Asian-influenced Vancouver restaurant scene to the cattle ranches of the Alberta foothills and the Ukrainian-inflected comfort food of the Saskatchewan and Manitoba plains. What connects them is the extraordinary quality of their primary ingredients: BC Pacific salmon, Alberta beef, prairie bison and grain, and the Okanagan Valley's wine and fruit. The Indigenous food traditions of the region — from the salmon ceremonies of the Coast Salish and Haida peoples to the bison hunting culture of the Blackfoot and Cree — form the deepest layer of a culinary story that European settlement transformed but did not erase.

British Columbia's food culture is the most cosmopolitan in Canada outside Toronto — Vancouver's proximity to Asia and its large Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and South Asian communities have made it one of the great food cities of the Pacific Rim. The Richmond district's Cantonese dim sum is considered world-class, and Japanese-Canadian food culture produced the California roll. Alberta's food identity is built on beef — Hereford and Black Angus cattle raised on the foothills grasslands produce meat of exceptional quality — and on the Ukrainian diaspora heritage of the prairies, where perogies, cabbage rolls, and borscht are as embedded in the culture as the wheat fields surrounding them.

02

Character of the Cuisine

BC cooking is defined by the Pacific — wild salmon, Dungeness crab, BC spot prawns, Fanny Bay oysters, and extraordinary sea vegetables are treated with the respectful simplicity their quality demands. Cedar-planked salmon — a First Nations technique of slow-cooking salmon on cedar wood over an open fire — is the most distinctly BC preparation. The Okanagan wine country has matured into a world-class wine region in three decades, making the valley a food destination on the level of Napa — but with Riesling and Pinot Noir rather than Cabernet.

Alberta's beef culture is expressed simply and with conviction: a well-aged Alberta ribeye over high heat, served with minimal distraction, is the pinnacle of the provincial table. The prairie heritage of Ukrainian, German, and Scandinavian settlers is still edible every weekend — perogies with sour cream and bacon, borscht with fresh dill, and cabbage rolls baked in tomato sauce. The saskatoon berry — the small purple prairie fruit with its distinctive almond undertone — appears in pies, jams, and sauces in a way no imported berry can replicate.

03

At the Table

  • Alberta beef is eaten with pride and without apology — a 16oz ribeye from a Black Angus or Wagyu-cross steer raised on the Alberta foothills is one of the finest pieces of beef in the world, and Albertans know it.
  • BC's Richmond district has the greatest concentration of Cantonese restaurants outside Hong Kong — the dim sum culture of Richmond's Golden Village is world-class.
  • The Ukrainian prairie heritage is edible every weekend — perogies, borscht, and cabbage rolls are comfort food across Saskatchewan and Manitoba, eaten at church hall suppers and family gatherings.
  • Saskatoon berries — the purple-black prairie fruit that tastes of almond and wild cherry — are the defining prairie flavour, used in pies and jams that no imported berry can replicate.
  • Vancouver's food truck scene and Saturday farmers' markets are serious institutions, as ingredient-obsessed as any food culture in North America.
  • Okanagan Valley wine has emerged as genuinely world-class — Pinot Noir, Riesling, and Merlot from the hot, dry valley now compete with the finest from California and Europe.

Did You Know?

  • The California roll — the inside-out sushi roll with avocado, cucumber, and crab — was almost certainly invented in Vancouver in the late 1960s or early 1970s by Japanese chef Hidekazu Tojo, to appeal to Canadian diners unfamiliar with raw fish.
  • Alberta is home to one of the world's largest Ukrainian diaspora communities — over 1.2 million Canadians claim Ukrainian heritage, concentrated in Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba. Vegreville, Alberta, hosts the world's largest Easter egg, a monument to the community's contribution.
  • Pacific salmon — Chinook, sockeye, coho, pink, and chum — have been the foundational food of BC's First Nations for thousands of years, and the salmon ceremony traditions represent one of the world's oldest sustained relationships between a human culture and a single food species.
  • Butter tarts — small pastry shells filled with a runny butter, sugar, egg, and syrup filling — are considered Canada's most distinctly national baked good. The debate over runny versus firm filling, and raisins versus pecans, is conducted with genuine feeling.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

BC & Coast

  1. A well-aged Alberta ribeye grilled simply over high heat, served with minimal distraction.
  2. Wild Pacific salmon slow-cooked on cedar wood over an open fire, a First Nations technique.
  3. Boiled potato and cheese dumplings topped with sour cream and fried onion, a prairie staple.
  4. A lean, richly flavoured bison patty grilled and served as a burger.
  5. A creamy risotto built on foraged BC wild mushrooms.

Alberta & Prairies

  1. A prairie take on the creamy tomato-based Indian butter chicken, popular across Western Canada.
  2. Cabbage leaves stuffed with rice and meat, baked in tomato sauce, a Ukrainian-prairie staple.
  3. Sweet Dungeness crab steamed and cracked simply at the table.
  4. A tangy beet soup finished with sour cream and fresh dill, a Ukrainian-prairie comfort classic.
  5. Wild Pacific salmon cold-smoked for a rich, delicate flavour.

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. A small pastry tart filled with a gooey butter, sugar, and syrup filling, Canada's most argued-over dessert.
  2. A double-crust pie filled with saskatoon berries, tasting of almond and wild cherry.
  3. A no-bake bar of crumb base, custard filling, and chocolate topping, named for the BC city.
  4. A flat, fried pastry dough dusted with cinnamon sugar, a Canadian fairground classic.

More Sweets

  1. Baked Okanagan apples under a buttery oat crumble topping.
  2. A pie mixing several berries and sometimes rhubarb, named for its jumbled combination.
  3. A moist, lightly spiced cake sweetened with prairie honey.
  4. A meringue dessert topped with cream and foraged wild prairie berries.
