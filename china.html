China

Peking duck, mala Sichuan fire, and Cantonese dim sum — a 5,000-year tradition so vast that 'Chinese cuisine' is really a family of distinct regional cuisines.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Chinese cuisine is among the oldest, most complex, and most geographically diverse food cultures on earth — a 5,000-year tradition that has shaped the eating habits of half the world's population and exported its techniques, ingredients, and dishes to every corner of the globe. The sheer scale of China — its climatic, topographical, and cultural variation from the frozen northeast to the tropical south, from the Sichuan basin to the Cantonese coast — means that 'Chinese cuisine' is not a single tradition but a vast family of distinct regional cuisines, each with its own philosophy, techniques, and signature flavors.

The eight great regional traditions — Cantonese, Sichuan, Hunan, Shandong, Jiangsu, Zhejiang, Fujian, and Anhui — are each fully developed culinary systems. Cantonese cooking prizes freshness and subtlety, using minimal seasoning to showcase pristine ingredients; Sichuan cooking is bold and numbing, built around chili and the unique Sichuan peppercorn; Hunan is fiercely spicy and sour; Shandong's seafood traditions shaped much of what became the imperial court cuisine of Beijing. Trade routes brought new ingredients including chili peppers, tomatoes, and peanuts that transformed the cuisine profoundly after the 16th century.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Chinese cooking is governed by a philosophy of balance — between flavors, textures, temperatures, and the five tastes of sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami. The wok, a uniquely Chinese invention, enabled the high-heat technique of wok hei — the 'breath of the wok,' a smoky, slightly charred flavor produced by tossing ingredients in an intensely hot iron pan — that is the defining technique of Chinese stir-frying and cannot be fully replicated at home. Fermented ingredients — doubanjiang, black bean paste, soy sauce, vinegars — provide layers of complexity that distinguish Chinese cooking from simpler traditions.

The cuisine encompasses extraordinary variety: the delicate steamed dim sum of Canton; the numbing fire of Sichuan mala hotpot; the lacquered magnificence of Peking duck; the soup dumpling engineering of xiaolongbao; the slow-braised comfort of red-cooked pork. Noodles, rice, and bread all serve as staples depending on region — wheat dominates the north, rice the south. Tea culture is ancient, complex, and inseparable from the social fabric of Chinese life.

03

At the Table

  • Meals are shared communally — dishes are placed in the center and eaten from together, not plated individually. Ordering one dish per person and eating it alone is considered antisocial.
  • The host serves guests first, and filling someone's bowl or glass without being asked is an act of care and hospitality. Letting a guest's glass go empty is considered neglectful.
  • Tea is served throughout the meal — a tap of two fingers on the table is the polite way to thank someone for pouring, a gesture said to originate from Qing dynasty court etiquette.
  • Chinese New Year is the most food-significant event of the year — whole fish for prosperity, dumplings for wealth, long noodles for longevity, and glutinous rice cakes for advancement.
  • Regional variation is so extreme that Cantonese, Sichuan, Shanghainese, and Peking cuisines are effectively different cooking traditions, sharing little beyond chopsticks and rice.
  • Dim sum is a Cantonese tradition of small dishes eaten with tea during morning or midday — yum cha literally means 'drink tea,' and the food is secondary to the social occasion.

Did You Know?

  • China has been making noodles for at least 4,000 years — the oldest noodles ever found were excavated at Lajia in northwest China in 2005, made from millet and dating to around 2000 BCE, predating Italian pasta by millennia.
  • Tofu was invented in China around 200 BCE, reputedly by Prince Liu An of the Han dynasty, and spread through East and Southeast Asia via Buddhist monks. It is now one of the world's most consumed protein sources.
  • Sichuan peppercorn creates a unique numbing, tingling sensation on the tongue called málà — it is not heat like chili but a different neurological sensation caused by the compound hydroxy-alpha-sanshool. Nothing else in the world produces quite this effect.
  • China is the world's largest tea producer and has the oldest continuous tea culture, stretching back over 3,000 years. The word 'tea' in most European languages derives from the Hokkien Chinese 'te,' carried along Dutch trade routes; 'chai' derives from the Mandarin 'chá.'

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Duck roasted until the skin lacquers deep mahogany, sliced and wrapped in thin pancakes with scallion and sauce.
  2. Silken tofu in a fiery, numbing sauce of doubanjiang and Sichuan peppercorn, studded with minced beef.
  3. Diced chicken stir-fried with peanuts, dried chilies, and a tangy sweet-savory sauce.
  4. Delicate steamed dumplings engineered to hold a pocket of hot savory broth inside.
  5. Battered fried pork in a bright, tangy-sweet sauce, a Cantonese classic known worldwide.

More Mains

  1. Pork marinated and roasted until sticky and caramelized with a honey-red glaze.
  2. A simmering pot of broth at the table into which diners cook meat, seafood, and vegetables communally.
  3. Noodles tossed in a spicy sauce of chili oil, sesame paste, and preserved vegetable.
  4. Crispy fried chicken tossed in a sweet, tangy, mildly spicy glaze, an American-Chinese classic.
  5. Oversized, tender pork meatballs braised slowly in broth with cabbage.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Festival Sweets

  1. Chewy glutinous rice balls filled with sesame or red bean, served in warm syrup.
  2. A dense pastry filled with lotus seed paste or red bean, eaten during the Mid-Autumn Festival.
  3. A chilled, silky mango-flavored custard, a Cantonese dessert staple.
  4. A flaky pastry shell filled with silky egg custard, a Cantonese and Macanese favourite.

More Sweets

  1. A chewy, sticky rice cake eaten sweet or savory, symbolic of advancement at New Year.
  2. Fried glutinous rice balls coated in sesame seeds with a sweet paste filling.
  3. A warm, sweet soup of slow-cooked red beans, often served at the end of a meal.
  4. A buttery shortbread pastry filled with tangy pineapple jam, a Taiwanese specialty.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Dumplings & Pastries

  1. Pork or vegetable filled dumplings, boiled, steamed, or pan-fried until crisp on the bottom.
  2. Thin pastry rolled around vegetables or meat and deep-fried until crisp.
  3. A flaky, layered pan-fried flatbread laced with scallion.
  4. A soft steamed bun filled with sweet honey-glazed barbecue pork.

Street Food

  1. Glutinous rice and filling wrapped in bamboo leaves and steamed, eaten at the Dragon Boat Festival.
  2. Skewered meat or vegetables grilled over charcoal and dusted with cumin and chili.
  3. A thin savory crepe wrapped around egg, a popular Chinese breakfast food.
  4. Delicate dumplings filled with pork or shrimp, served in broth or fried.
