Cuisine Guide

Colombia

Bandeja paisa, ajiaco, and sancocho — a geographically vast cuisine spanning Andean highlands, Caribbean and Pacific coasts, Amazon, and Llanos plains.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Colombian cuisine is a layered synthesis of indigenous, Spanish, African, and Middle Eastern traditions, shaped by one of the most geographically diverse countries on earth — a nation that encompasses Pacific and Caribbean coastlines, Andean highlands, Amazon rainforest, and the vast Llanos plains, each with its own distinct ecology, crops, and cooking traditions. The indigenous peoples of the region — Muisca, Zenú, Tairona, Quimbaya, and dozens of others — cultivated corn, yuca, potato, beans, and an extraordinary variety of tropical fruits for millennia before Spanish contact. The arepa, the corn cake that is Colombia's daily bread, is their most enduring culinary gift.

The Spanish conquest from the early 16th century introduced cattle, pigs, wheat, dairy, and the sofrito tradition of building flavour from onion, tomato, and garlic — the hogao sauce that appears in Colombian cooking today is a direct descendant. Enslaved West Africans brought to the Caribbean and Pacific coasts contributed coconut milk cookery, rice traditions, and the flavour principles that define coastal Colombian food. Lebanese and Syrian immigrants in the early 20th century settled particularly in Barranquilla and the Atlantic coast, leaving traces in kibbeh-influenced Colombian dishes. The result is a cuisine of genuine regional diversity united by a few common threads: the arepa, the sancocho, the bandeja, and coffee.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Colombian cooking is abundant, warming, and deeply regional — what is eaten on the Pacific coast bears little resemblance to the Andean paisa tradition of Medellín and Antioquia, which in turn differs from the coastal Caribbean cooking of Cartagena, the llanero beef culture of the Llanos, or the indigenous-rooted cooking of the Amazon. The paisa tradition of Antioquia is perhaps the most internationally recognised: the bandeja paisa, a platter of extraordinary generosity containing rice, beans, ground beef, chicharrón, fried egg, arepa, avocado, and plantain, is the emblem of paisa abundance and the default image of Colombian food abroad.

Ajiaco bogotano — the potato and chicken soup of Bogotá, made with three distinct varieties of Colombian potato and the guascas herb found nowhere else — is one of the most distinctive soups in Latin America. Sancocho, the slow-cooked broth of meat, corn, yuca, and plantain, is the great democratic dish eaten at every level of society. The Pacific coast tradition of fresh seafood in coconut milk represents a completely different Colombia — lighter, fragrant, and thoroughly West African in its flavour logic. Colombia's extraordinary fruit diversity — over 200 native varieties — gives the beverage and dessert traditions a richness found nowhere else on the continent.

03

At the Table

  • The bandeja paisa is less a dish than a test of appetite — a tray bearing rice, beans, ground meat, chicharrón, fried egg, arepa, avocado, and plantain, eaten at lunchtime in Antioquia.
  • Arepas are the daily bread of Colombia — thick corn cakes griddled or grilled, eaten at every meal, stuffed with cheese, egg, or meat, or eaten plain alongside everything else.
  • Lunch is the main meal — Colombians eat a substantial midday comida corriente of soup, rice, protein, salad, and juice. Dinner is lighter.
  • Fresh fruit juice is the national drink of everyday life — jugos naturales of lulo, maracuyá, guanábana, and tomate de árbol are drunk daily.
  • Sancocho is the great restorative soup — a slow-cooked broth of whatever protein is available with corn, yuca, plantain, and potato, eaten as both everyday meal and hangover cure.
  • Colombian hospitality is warm and generous — a guest arriving at mealtime must be offered food. The phrase 'ya comió?' (have you eaten?) is both a greeting and an invitation.

Did You Know?

  • Colombia is the world's third-largest coffee producer and arguably its most celebrated for quality — the Coffee Cultural Landscape of Antioquia, Caldas, Quindío, and Risaralda was granted UNESCO World Heritage status in 2011. Juan Valdez and his mule became one of the most recognisable advertising icons of the 20th century.
  • Colombia is one of the world's most biodiverse countries, possessing more species of birds, orchids, and freshwater fish than any other country on earth. This extends to food — Colombia has over 200 native fruit varieties, many unknown outside the country.
  • The arepa predates the Spanish conquest by thousands of years — indigenous peoples across what is now Colombia and Venezuela were making corn cakes long before European contact. The word derives from a Cumanagoto indigenous word for corn.
  • Aguardiente — Colombia's anise-flavoured sugarcane spirit — is produced in distinct regional styles with fiercely loyal followings. Antioqueño, Nariño's version, and the coffee region's aguardientes each have their own devoted drinkers.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. A tray of rice, beans, ground beef, chicharrón, fried egg, arepa, avocado, and plantain, Antioquia's signature dish.
  2. A potato and chicken soup made with three potato varieties and the guascas herb, unique to Bogotá.
  3. A slow-cooked broth of meat, corn, yuca, and plantain, Colombia's democratic comfort dish.
  4. Rice cooked in coconut milk until lightly caramelized, a Caribbean coast staple.
  5. A rich seafood stew simmered in coconut milk, a Pacific coast specialty.

More Mains

  1. A whole pig stuffed with rice, peas, and spices, then slow-roasted, a Tolima festival centerpiece.
  2. Corn masa filled with meat and vegetables, wrapped in banana leaves and steamed.
  3. An assortment of grilled and fried meats served together, a festive shared plate.
  4. Beef slow-braised in a dark, sweet sauce of panela and spices, a Cartagena specialty.
  5. A creamy soup of yam and melted cheese, a Caribbean coast comfort dish.

Puddings & Sweets

Sweets & Pastries

  1. A rich custard made from the skimmed skin of scalded milk, sweetened and spiced.
  2. A thick, caramelized milk spread, Colombia's version of dulce de leche.
  3. Deep-fried balls of cheese-enriched dough, a Christmas season staple.
  4. A soft, sliceable cinnamon-spiced corn pudding, traditional at Christmas.

More Sweets

  1. Poached figs filled or topped with arequipe caramel.
  2. A dense, chewy coconut candy popular on the Caribbean coast.
  3. A moist cake made from fresh sweet corn.
  4. A thick, sweet milk custard similar to arequipe but often firmer.

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. A sweet corn arepa griddled and filled with melted cheese.
  2. Crisp fried corn-dough pastries filled with meat and potato.
  3. A chewy bread roll made from cassava starch and cheese.
  4. A warm milk broth with a poached egg and scallion, a Bogotá breakfast tradition.

More Snacks

  1. Thin wafer discs sandwiched together with arequipe caramel.
  2. Fresh passion fruit blended into a tart, fragrant juice.
  3. Small, soft cheese bread rolls, often eaten with hot chocolate.
  4. Ripe plantain slices battered around cheese and deep-fried.
