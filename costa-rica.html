Cuisine Guide

Costa Rica

Gallo pinto, Salsa Lizano, and comida sencilla — a mild, nourishing Mesoamerican and Spanish colonial tradition built around rice, beans, and world-class coffee.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Costa Rican cuisine is a Mesoamerican and Spanish colonial tradition, shaped by the country's position as a narrow land bridge between North and South America and the biodiversity of its tropical landscape. The indigenous peoples — Chorotega, Bribri, Boruca, and others — cultivated corn, beans, squash, yuca, and tropical fruits for thousands of years before Spanish colonization in the 16th century. These indigenous staples remain the foundation of the diet today: the daily combination of rice and beans is a direct continuation of the pre-Columbian agricultural tradition, adapted through centuries of mestizo culture.

The Spanish colonial period brought cattle, pigs, wheat, and dairy — and the slow-cooked stew tradition that produced olla de carne and sopa negra. Unlike other Central American countries, Costa Rica did not develop a large plantation economy based on enslaved labor, which meant African culinary influence is less pronounced than in neighboring countries, though present in the Caribbean coastal region around Limón. Coffee cultivation, introduced in the early 19th century, transformed the country economically and socially. Later waves of Jamaican, Chinese, and Italian immigration left culinary traces, particularly in the Caribbean province.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Costa Rican food is mild, nourishing, and built around simplicity — Ticos themselves describe their cuisine as comida sencilla, simple food. The flavor is not spicy-hot but warmly aromatic, built on the sofrito base of onion, garlic, sweet pepper, and culantro coyote (a more pungent cousin of cilantro). Salsa Lizano — a thin, slightly sweet, mildly spiced bottled sauce — is the most distinctive flavoring in the national kitchen, appearing on eggs, rice, beans, and meat with the frequency of ketchup elsewhere.

Gallo pinto — rice and beans fried together with Salsa Lizano — is the national breakfast, eaten daily across all social classes. The casado lunch plate anchors midday eating, while tamales made in banana leaves appear at Christmas with near-religious devotion. The Caribbean coast offers a distinctly different tradition: rice and beans cooked in coconut milk, jerk-spiced chicken, and tropical fruit desserts reflect the Jamaican heritage of the region. Costa Rica's exceptional coffee and tropical fruits give the beverage tradition a richness that compensates for the modesty of the savory cooking.

03

At the Table

  • Gallo pinto is eaten for breakfast every day — rice and beans fried together with Salsa Lizano, served with eggs, natilla, and fried plantains. It is the most consumed meal in the country.
  • Casado is the classic lunch plate — rice, beans, salad, fried plantain, and a protein. Every soda serves one; it is the democratic lunchtime institution of Costa Rica.
  • Sodas are the heart of Costa Rican food culture — small family-run restaurants serving home cooking at low prices. They are not tourist restaurants; they are where Ticos eat.
  • Mealtime is family time — large lunches shared with extended family on Sundays are the social anchor of Costa Rican life.
  • Coffee is taken seriously — Costa Rica produces world-class arabica from high-altitude farms in the Central Valley, Tarrazú, and Monteverde. A cup of chorreado filtered coffee is the morning ritual.
  • Salsa Lizano is to Costa Rica what Worcestershire sauce is to England — a thin, slightly sweet, mildly spiced condiment that goes on virtually everything savory.

Did You Know?

  • Costa Rica produces some of the world's finest arabica coffee, grown at high altitudes in volcanic soil. The country banned robusta coffee by law in 1988 to maintain quality standards — the only country in the world with such legislation.
  • Gallo pinto is the subject of a friendly dispute with Nicaragua, which also claims the dish as its own national food. The Costa Rican version is distinctively seasoned with Salsa Lizano and often includes a splash of Worcestershire sauce.
  • Costa Rica was the first country in Central America to commercially cultivate coffee, beginning in the early 19th century. Coffee funded the construction of the National Theatre in San José when coffee barons paid a tax on each bag exported.
  • Palmito — heart of palm — is a Costa Rican delicacy harvested from peach palm trees. Costa Rica is one of the world's largest producers and exporters of canned heart of palm, and fresh palmito appears in salads, empanadas, and pasteles.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Rice and beans fried together with Salsa Lizano, the national daily breakfast.
  2. A lunch plate of rice, beans, salad, fried plantain, and a protein, Costa Rica's everyday meal.
  3. A hearty beef stew with corn, yuca, plantain, and root vegetables.
  4. Chicken cooked together with seasoned rice and vegetables.
  5. A black bean broth soup traditionally served with a poached or hard-boiled egg.

More Mains

  1. Layered rice, beans, and crispy fried pork over tortilla chips with salsa.
  2. Corn masa filled with meat and vegetables, wrapped in banana leaf and steamed, a Christmas tradition.
  3. Fresh fish cured in lime juice with cilantro, onion, and sweet pepper.
  4. Twice-fried green plantain discs served alongside beans.
  5. A minced hash of arracacha root and ground beef, a nostalgic traditional dish.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Creamy rice pudding sweetened and dusted with cinnamon.
  2. A sponge cake soaked in three kinds of milk until meltingly moist.
  3. A silky caramel custard flavored with coconut.
  4. A dense, chewy fudge made from coconut milk and sugar.

More Sweets

  1. A stringy squash candied in sugar syrup and spices, a traditional preserve.
  2. A sweet, thick pudding made from corn.
  3. A dense, lightly sweet pound cake, a coffee-time staple.
  4. Small ring-shaped cookies of corn flour and cheese, slightly savory-sweet.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. Corn dough turnovers filled with cheese, meat, or beans, fried or baked.
  2. Green plantain slices fried twice until crisp, served as a snack or side.
  3. Small bread rolls stuffed with meat, cheese, and vegetables.
  4. Soft corn tortillas folded around a savory filling like picadillo.

More Snacks

  1. Simple handmade corn tortillas, the base of many Costa Rican snacks.
  2. Thick, sweet pancakes made from fresh corn, often served with natilla.
  3. Corn on the cob loaded with mayonnaise, cheese, and sauces.
  4. Fresh heart of palm dressed simply with lime juice.
