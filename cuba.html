Cuba

Ropa vieja, mojo, and café cubano — a creole fusion of Spanish, West African, and Taíno traditions, shaped further by revolution, rationing, and resourcefulness.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Cuban cuisine is a creole fusion of Spanish colonial cooking, West African culinary traditions brought by enslaved people, and indigenous Taíno ingredients — layered over four centuries into something wholly distinctive. The Spanish brought olive oil, garlic, onions, rice, and pork; the Taíno contributed cassava (yuca), corn, sweet potatoes, and tropical fruits; and the African diaspora introduced cooking techniques, spicing philosophies, and dishes that form the soul of Cuban food. All three threads are still traceable in a single plate of ropa vieja.

Sugar and tobacco shaped the island's colonial economy and, indirectly, its food culture — the plantation system that produced both crops relied on enslaved African labor, whose culinary knowledge transformed Cuban cooking from the inside. Chinese indentured workers arrived in the mid-19th century and added yet another layer, particularly in Havana's Barrio Chino. The 1959 revolution and subsequent US embargo reshaped food availability dramatically — scarcity and the state rationing system (la libreta) became defining forces in how Cubans cook and eat, producing a creative, resourceful kitchen that makes exceptional food from limited ingredients.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Cuban food is warm, comforting, and built around a handful of core elements: rice, black beans, pork, and plantains appear daily in some form. The flavor foundation is sofrito — a slow-cooked base of garlic, onion, peppers, tomato, and cumin — that underpins most savory dishes. Mojo, a sauce of sour orange juice, garlic, and cumin, is used to marinate meats and dress boiled vegetables. The cooking is not spicy-hot but richly seasoned, with bright acidity from citrus and depth from slow braising.

Tropical fruits and root vegetables give Cuban cooking its Caribbean character: plantains appear in multiple forms at the same meal (tostones, maduros), yuca is boiled and dressed with mojo, and batidos (milkshakes) made from mamey, mango, or guava are everyday refreshments. The Cubano sandwich — layered with roast pork, ham, Swiss cheese, pickles, and mustard on pressed Cuban bread — has become one of the most recognized Latin American food exports globally.

03

At the Table

  • The main meal (almuerzo) is eaten at midday — rice and black beans appear at virtually every table, every day. They are not a side dish but the foundation around which everything else is arranged.
  • Food is communal and generous — Cuban hospitality means a guest is fed, regardless of how little the household has. Refusing food is considered impolite.
  • The bodega system (state ration stores) has shaped Cuban cooking profoundly since 1962 — scarcity encouraged creativity, stretching ingredients and relying on what is available.
  • Coffee (café cubano) is a ritual — a tiny, intensely sweet espresso shot drunk multiple times daily. Sharing a cafecito is a social gesture as much as a caffeine delivery.
  • Street food and paladares (private family restaurants) have become central to Cuban food culture, especially since private enterprise was partially liberalized in the 1990s and 2000s.
  • Pork is the celebratory protein — a whole roast pig (lechón asado) cooked over an open fire is the centerpiece of Christmas Eve (Nochebuena), weddings, and any major celebration.

Did You Know?

  • Cuba's signature cocktail, the Mojito, was reputedly a favorite of Ernest Hemingway, who lived in Cuba for 20 years and drank at La Bodeguita del Medio in Havana, which still displays his handwritten note on the wall.
  • The Cuba Libre (rum and Coca-Cola with lime) was born around 1900, shortly after Coca-Cola arrived in Cuba following the Spanish-American War, when soldiers mixed it with local rum and toasted 'Por Cuba Libre!'
  • Cuban cigars are considered the finest in the world, and tobacco — alongside sugar and rum — formed the three pillars of Cuba's colonial economy. The Vuelta Abajo region produces tobacco leaves considered unmatched in quality.
  • Cuba was the world's largest sugar producer for much of the 19th and early 20th centuries — the sugar industry drove the slave trade, shaped the landscape, and left a lasting sweetness in Cuban cooking and rum production.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Shredded beef slow-braised in a savory tomato sauce until fork-tender.
  2. Black beans and white rice cooked together until the rice takes on a deep, savory color.
  3. A whole pig slow-roasted over an open fire, the centerpiece of Cuban celebrations.
  4. Marinated shredded beef pan-fried until crisp at the edges.
  5. Ground beef simmered with tomato, olives, and raisins, a sweet-savory Cuban staple.

More Mains

  1. A hearty stew of root vegetables and mixed meats, Cuba's answer to the Latin American sancocho.
  2. Chicken pieces braised in a savory tomato-based sauce.
  3. A beef pot roast stuffed with chorizo sausage, sliced to reveal the filling.
  4. Boiled cassava dressed with a garlicky sour orange mojo sauce.
  5. Corn masa filled with seasoned pork, wrapped and steamed in corn husks.

Puddings & Sweets

Custards & Classics

  1. A silky caramel custard, Cuba's most beloved classic dessert.
  2. Creamy rice pudding sweetened and dusted with cinnamon.
  3. A soft, spoonable vanilla custard set with egg.
  4. Fried dough shapes soaked in anise-scented syrup, a Christmas tradition.

More Sweets

  1. A sweet, dense coconut custard dessert.
  2. A rustic sweet of milk curdled and cooked down with sugar.
  3. Light, crisp baked meringue cookies.
  4. Crispy fried dough strips dusted in sugar, a traditional Cuban celebration snack.

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food & Sandwiches

  1. Green plantain slices fried twice until crisp and flattened.
  2. Ripe plantain slices fried until caramelized and sweet.
  3. Roast pork, ham, Swiss cheese, pickles, and mustard pressed on Cuban bread.
  4. Creamy ham-studded béchamel, breaded and fried until golden.

Sides & Snacks
