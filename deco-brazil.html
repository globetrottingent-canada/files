SOUTH AMERICA / BRAZILIAN

Décor & Party Guide

Brazil

Pure exuberance — Carnaval feathers and sequins, the boom of the samba drums, tropical colour on every surface, and a caipirinha in hand.

01

The Visual Spirit

A Brazilian party is pure exuberance — colour, movement, and rhythm turned all the way up. The flagship is Carnaval: feathers and sequins, samba drums, streamers everywhere, and the loose, joyful energy of a street-party bloco. Nothing is understated; brightness and warmth are the whole point, and glitter is always welcome.

But Brazil is enormous and gloriously diverse, so it's worth reaching past the Rio-Carnival postcard. There's the rustic countryside Festa Junina, the deep Afro-Brazilian culture of Bahia (capoeira, candomblé, the berimbau), and the cool of bossa nova and the beach. The palette spans the flag's green-yellow-blue and the full tropical rainbow, mixed with real foliage, bright fabric, paper bunting, and drums.

One country, many parties

Carnaval — the flagship. Feathers, sequins, samba, glitter, and street-party blocos. Big, loud, and euphoric.

Festa Junina (June) — the countryside festival: checkered shirts and straw hats, triangular bunting (bandeirinhas), bonfires, forró and quadrilha square-dancing, and corn treats.

Bahia & beyond — Afro-Brazilian Bahia (capoeira, berimbau, the white-lace baiana dress), plus the cool of bossa nova and the beach. It's all Brazil — mix to taste.

02

Setting the Scene

For a Carnaval party, go maximal. Drape streamers and metallic fringe, pile on feathers (penachos), sequins, and colourful paper garlands, and bring the tropics indoors with palm, monstera, and heliconia. Set up a percussion corner — a surdo bass drum, tamborins, and shakers — because the batucada drumming is the heartbeat of the night. Colour on every surface, glitter encouraged.

For a countryside or all-rounder Brazilian party, string bandeirinhas (triangular bunting) across the ceiling, add paper lanterns, hay bales, and a checkered-cloth table of corn treats, with forró playing — the Festa Junina look. For a Bahia accent, tie on Senhor do Bonfim wish-ribbons (fitas), stand a berimbau in the corner, and add white-and-blue lace touches.

03

Dressing the Room

  • Go big on colour — streamers, metallic fringe curtains, and rainbow paper garlands on every surface. This is a Carnaval, not a minimalist affair.
  • String bandeirinhas — triangular fabric or paper bunting overhead — the signature of a Festa Junina and a great all-purpose Brazilian backdrop.
  • Add feathers and sequins — plumes (penachos), sparkle, and glitter for full Carnaval glamour.
  • Bring the tropics in — palm leaves, monstera, heliconia, and bright flowers massed together.
  • Build a percussion corner — surdo, tamborim, pandeiro, and shakers — the batucada that drives samba.
  • (Festa Junina) lay checkered cloths, add straw hats, hay bales, paper lanterns, and a "bonfire" (fogueira) centrepiece.
  • (Bahia) tie on Senhor do Bonfim fitas (wish-ribbons), stand a berimbau in the corner, and add white lace accents.
  • Fly the colours — the green, yellow, and blue of the flag, plus the full tropical rainbow.

05

Did You Know?

  • Carnaval is powered by escolas de samba (samba schools) — huge community organisations that spend most of the year building costumes and floats for the Sambadrome parade. The driving sound is batucada, led by the booming surdo bass drums.
  • Brazil is so vast that Festa Junina — the June "São João" festivals honouring Saints Anthony, John, and Peter — rivals Carnaval across much of the Northeast, with bonfires, forró, and quadrilha square dancing.
  • The colourful fitas of Senhor do Bonfim in Salvador are tied around the wrist with three knots — one wish per knot — and worn until they fall off on their own, when the wishes are said to come true.
  • Bossa nova — the cool, hushed sound of "The Girl from Ipanema" — grew out of samba on the beaches of Rio in the late 1950s and became one of Brazil's biggest cultural exports.
Décor & Ambiance Reference

One big, colourful culture — lean into Carnaval, or branch out.

06

Room & Table Décor

Carnaval

  1. Big feather plumes, sequinned fabric, and sparkle everywhere — the glamorous, over-the-top core of the Carnaval look.
  2. Paper streamers, metallic fringe curtains, and rainbow garlands draped from ceilings and doorways — instant party maximalism.
  3. Palm fronds, monstera leaves, heliconia, and bright tropical flowers to bring the lushness of Brazil indoors.
  4. A surdo bass drum, tamborins, pandeiro, and shakers set up in a corner — both décor and the engine of the samba sound.

Festa Junina & Beyond

  1. Rows of colourful triangular flag bunting strung overhead — the defining look of a Festa Junina "arraiá," and cheerful for any Brazilian party.
  2. Red-checkered tablecloths, straw hats, hay bales, and paper lanterns for the rustic caipira (countryside) feel of São João.
  3. A bonfire (fogueira) is central to Festa Junina — a safe faux version plus warm paper lanterns capture it indoors.
  4. Senhor do Bonfim wish-ribbons tied around wrists and railings, a berimbau in the corner, and white lace — the Afro-Brazilian Bahia touch.
07

What to Wear

Carnaval

  1. A tall feather headdress and plumes — the unmistakable crown of a samba-parade costume (fantasia).
  2. Bright sequinned bodysuits, bikinis, or shirts in vivid colour — the more it catches the light, the better.
  3. Body glitter and stick-on face gems — the easy, universal way to look Carnaval-ready with almost no effort.
  4. Flower crowns and bright blooms for a street-party bloco look that's fun and low-commitment.

Festa Junina & Bahia

  1. The Festa Junina "country" costume: a checkered (xadrez) shirt, straw hat, and patched trousers, often with painted freckles — a warm, playful tribute to rural life.
  2. A full, floral-print chita skirt with pigtails and ribbons — the women's Festa Junina look, great for the quadrilha.
  3. The flowing white lace dress and turban of Bahia's baianas — elegant, dignified, and central to Afro-Brazilian tradition.
  4. No costume? Green-and-yellow, a football shirt, and Havaianas flip-flops read Brazilian and keep things easy.
08

Music & Sound

Carnaval & Party

  1. The definitive Brazilian rhythm — irresistible, danceable, and the sound of Carnaval. Put it on and the party starts itself.
  2. The pounding percussion of a samba-school bateria — surdos, tamborins, and repiniques. Live drums turn any room into a parade.
  3. The high-energy pop sound of Salvador's Carnaval — made for the trio elétrico sound trucks and mass dancing.
  4. The frantic, brass-driven Carnaval music of Pernambuco, danced with a little frevo umbrella — colourful and joyous.

Bossa & Regional

  1. Understated, jazzy, and effortlessly cool — Jobim and "The Girl from Ipanema." Perfect for the mellow, beachy end of the evening.
  2. Accordion-led dance music from the Northeast — the sound of Festa Junina, made for close, swaying partner dancing.
  3. Música Popular Brasileira — the rich songbook of Gil, Veloso, and more for a warm, sophisticated background.
  4. Relaxed, communal samba played around a table — the intimate, everyday cousin of the big Carnaval sound.
