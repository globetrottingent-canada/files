OCEANIA / HAWAIIAN

Décor & Party Guide

Hawaii

The real islands, not the tiki bar — aloha and ohana, fresh lei, ti leaves and lauhala, koa-wood calabash, and the sound of slack-key and the ukulele.

01

The Visual Spirit

Authentic Hawaii is quieter, warmer, and richer than the party-store version. The guiding feeling is aloha — generosity, welcome, and the sense of ohana, family — and the highest expression of it is the luau: a communal feast built around the kalua pig from the imu, with everyone sharing from common bowls. The look that carries it is drawn from the land and the ocean: fresh flower lei, glossy ti leaves, woven lauhala, koa-wood calabash bowls, and the deep greens and volcanic blacks of the islands, lit warm and low.

Materials matter more than bright color here. Real leaves, real flowers, natural fiber, and carved wood do the work — accented by the vivid punctuation of plumeria, hibiscus, and lehua. Handled with a little care and respect (lei and hula carry real meaning, so they're honored rather than worn as gags), a Hawaiian room feels less like a themed bar and more like a genuine island celebration.

Which direction?

This page is the authentic route — Native Hawaiian craft, lei, hula, and slack-key music.

Want carved tiki idols, bamboo bars, and Mai Tais? That's "Polynesian Pop," a mid-century American invention rather than real Hawaiian culture — save it for the Pacific Islands party.

Just want a bright, breezy backyard luau? That's a perfectly fun modern take — big floral prints, string lights, and paper lanterns. Keep it light on the plastic and it still nods to the real thing.

02

Setting the Scene

Think low and communal. A long table close to the ground, or a generous buffet, dressed with a runner of overlapping ti leaves and set on lauhala mats. Serve from carved koa or kou-wood calabash bowls and gourds rather than themed plastic. Keep the palette natural — greens, wood tones, the earth browns of kapa barkcloth — and let real flowers provide the color: plumeria and orchids floating in shallow bowls of water, hibiscus tucked here and there, ferns trailing off the table.

Greet guests at the door with a lei — the single most meaningful Hawaiian gesture you can make. Light the room warmly with candles and hurricane lanterns (skip the flaming tiki torches, which belong to the retro bar look), and give the music real space: a corner for a ukulele or slack-key player, or a good playlist kept present but soft. The imu-roasted pig, or a stand-in of kalua pork, is the centerpiece the whole evening gathers around.

03

Dressing the Room

  • Run a ti-leaf runner down the length of the table — overlapping glossy green leaves — and use extra leaves as natural placemats or to line serving platters.
  • Lay down lauhala mats (woven pandanus) and serve from koa-wood calabash bowls and gourds (ipu) for an authentic, natural table.
  • Greet every guest with a fresh lei — plumeria, orchid, ti, or kukui nut. Giving a lei is the heart of Hawaiian welcome (see the etiquette note below).
  • Fill the room with real island flowers — plumeria and orchids floating in bowls of water, hibiscus, heliconia, bird of paradise, and ferns — instead of plastic garlands.
  • Add kapa (barkcloth) patterns as runners or wall hangings — earth-tone geometric prints that read distinctly Hawaiian rather than generic tropical.
  • Light it warm and low — candles, hurricane lanterns, and kukui-nut lamps. Save the flaming tiki torches for the retro-bar look.
  • Keep it natural, not plastic — ti, lauhala, wood, stone, water, and flowers. Skip the inflatable palms, grass-skirt table skirts, and neon leis.

05

Did You Know?

  • There's an etiquette to the lei. Accept one with both hands and never remove it in front of the giver; it's disrespectful to throw a lei away — traditionally you return it to the earth or dry it as a keepsake. The touristy cheek-kiss is a WWII-era import; the older gesture is the hā, a shared breath, cheek to cheek.
  • The ti leaf (kī) is considered sacred and protective — used to wrap food for the imu, to make lei and hula skirts, and hung for good luck. Its deep green is the workhorse of authentic Hawaiian décor.
  • May 1 is Lei Day — "May Day is Lei Day in Hawaii" — with lei-making contests, hula, and lauhala weaving. Each of the eight main islands has its own signature lei flower, like the red ʻōhiʻa lehua of the Big Island and the pink lokelani of Maui.
  • Kapa — Hawaiian barkcloth beaten from wauke (paper mulberry) and stamped with natural dyes in geometric earth-tone patterns — is a pre-contact island textile, quite different from the bright floral prints most people picture.

06

Room & Table Décor

Natural Foundations

  1. Big glossy sacred leaves used as table runners, placemats, and platter liners. The most authentic, versatile, and affordable Hawaiian décor element there is.
  2. Handwoven mats and placemats made from hala (pandanus) leaves — a living Hawaiian craft that grounds the table in natural fiber.
  3. Turned wooden serving bowls, traditionally from koa or kou wood — used for poi and shared food. Warm, handsome, and central to communal Hawaiian dining.
  4. Dried gourds used as containers and as the hula rhythm instrument (ipu heke). Clustered on a table, they read instantly and authentically island.

Flowers & Finishing

  1. The garland of welcome, given to each guest. Plumeria and orchid for beauty, ti-leaf or kukui-nut lei for a more traditional, protective feel.
  2. Hibiscus, plumeria, orchid, heliconia, and bird of paradise — floated in water bowls or arranged low so people can see and talk across the table.
  3. Earth-tone geometric barkcloth prints as runners or wall hangings — a distinctly Hawaiian textile that sets the room apart from generic tropical decor.
  4. Shallow bowls of water with floating blossoms and candles, plus hurricane lanterns and kukui-nut (candlenut) lamps for warm, authentic light.
07

What to Wear

For Her

  1. The loose, comfortable Hawaiian dress — or the more formal holokū — in a nature-inspired print. Breezy, graceful, and genuinely island rather than costume.
  2. A wrapped fabric hula skirt — the real thing, not the plastic grass version. Pair with a simple top for a dancer's look.
  3. A braided flower crown for the head, plus a blossom tucked behind the ear. Small, fresh, and far more elegant than a novelty headband.
  4. A fragrant flower lei, or a kukui-nut or shell lei for something longer-lasting. Choose real materials over the plastic party-store strands.

For Him

  1. The real aloha shirt — muted, reverse-print, or with motifs of native plants like kukui, breadfruit, and taro. A world away from the neon tourist version.
  2. Light linen or palaka trousers, sleeves rolled, and rubber slippers ("slippahs") or bare feet. Comfort and ease are the whole point.
  3. A cloth sash worn over one shoulder — the traditional, more ceremonial look for a host or for hula, often paired with a ti-leaf or maile lei.
  4. An open-ended maile-vine lei or a twisted ti-leaf lei — the traditional lei for men, worn draped rather than closed in a loop.
08

Music & Sound

Instruments & Voices

  1. Hawaii's signature instrument, adapted from a Portuguese braguinha. Bright and easy — perfect for a singalong corner and endlessly recognizable.
  2. A uniquely Hawaiian fingerstyle guitar in loosened "slack" tunings — warm, gentle, and the definitive sound of a relaxed island evening.
  3. The sliding, singing lap steel guitar — a Hawaiian invention that spread worldwide. Its shimmer instantly says "islands."
  4. The soaring Hawaiian falsetto and the older tradition of oli (chant) — a hush-the-room moment that carries real cultural weight.

Artists & Styles

  1. The beloved voice of modern Hawaii — his ukulele medley is the easiest way to set an authentic, warm-hearted tone for any gathering.
  2. Gabby Pahinui, Keola Beamer, and other slack-key greats make a perfect, unobtrusive background playlist that's genuinely of the islands.
  3. Ancient hula kahiko is chanted to ipu and pahu drums; modern hula ʻauana flows with ukulele and guitar. Even a short performance or lesson makes the night.
  4. The modern island sound — a laid-back blend of reggae and local music. A good, contemporary option if you want something younger and upbeat.
