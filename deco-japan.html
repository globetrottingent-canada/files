EAST ASIA / JAPANESE

Décor & Party Guide

Japan

Restraint and season — the calm of wabi-sabi, paper-lantern glow and a single branch of blossom, tatami and ikebana, with sake poured and taiko in the distance.

01

The Visual Spirit

Traditional Japanese style is an art of restraint. It's built on natural materials, seasonal awareness, and ma — the deliberate use of empty space — so a little does a great deal: one hanging scroll, one branch of blossom, the soft glow of a single paper lantern. The guiding sensibility is wabi-sabi, a love of the simple, imperfect, and impermanent. The result feels calm, elegant, and quietly intentional rather than busy.

Season is everything: a traditional room is re-dressed through the year — sakura and soft pinks in spring, indigo and koi in summer, momiji maple in autumn, monochrome sumi-e in winter. Work in natural materials (wood, washi paper, bamboo, tatami, ceramic, indigo cloth) and a palette of indigo, sumi black, vermilion red, gold, natural wood, and sakura pink, and the room will read unmistakably Japanese without ever feeling crowded.

Two eras, both truly Japan

Traditional (wa) — this page. Wabi-sabi calm, tatami and shoji, ikebana and tea, festival lanterns and blossom. Timeless, elegant, and seasonal.

Modern-pop Tokyo — neon signs, kawaii pastels, anime, city pop and karaoke, Harajuku colour. Just as authentically Japanese — simply a different era. Reach for it if you want bright, playful, and loud rather than serene; it's a genuine choice, not a lesser one.

02

Setting the Scene

For a traditional evening, start by clearing space — the emptiness is the point. Set a low table with floor cushions on tatami-style matting, and create one focal alcove in the spirit of a tokonoma: a hanging scroll, a single ikebana arrangement, perhaps an incense burner. Keep the light soft and diffused — shoji-style washi lamps and paper lanterns — hang a noren in a doorway, and bring in a branch of the season. Serve on ceramic and lacquerware, with a sake set and a little matcha and wagashi.

For a festival or hanami mood, turn up the warmth: string red-and-white chōchin lanterns, hand out happi coats, and lay a picnic beneath real or faux cherry blossom, with taiko drumming in the background and yatai-style food and sake. It's livelier and more celebratory, but still grounded in the real thing rather than novelty.

03

Dressing the Room

  • Embrace ma (empty space) — resist filling every surface. Let a few beautiful things breathe; the restraint is the aesthetic, not a lack of effort.
  • Create a focal alcove — a hanging scroll (kakejiku), a single ikebana arrangement, and perhaps incense. The tokonoma idea works scaled down to any shelf or wall.
  • Diffuse the light — shoji-style washi paper lamps and chōchin lanterns for a soft, warm glow. Never harsh or bright.
  • Hang a noren — a short fabric curtain in a doorway to divide space gently and carry a seasonal motif.
  • Bring in the season — sakura branches in spring, indigo textiles and koi in summer, momiji maple in autumn, sumi-e monochrome in winter.
  • Set the table low — floor cushions, ceramic and lacquerware, chopsticks on rests (hashioki), and a sake set (tokkuri and ochoko).
  • Favour natural materials — bamboo, unfinished wood, stone, a bonsai or ikebana — arranged organically and a little asymmetrically.
  • (Festival) string red-and-white chōchin, add happi coats and a taiko soundtrack for a matsuri or hanami feel.

05

Did You Know?

  • Wabi-sabi — finding beauty in the simple, imperfect, and impermanent — underlies the whole aesthetic. A cracked tea bowl mended with gold (kintsugi) is treasured for its flaws, not in spite of them.
  • Ma (間), the deliberate use of empty space, matters as much as what fills it. Restraint and negative space are the design, so resist the urge to add "just one more" thing.
  • Traditional rooms change with the season — the scroll and flowers in the tokonoma were swapped roughly four times a year, and shō-chiku-bai (pine, bamboo, and plum) appears at New Year as an auspicious trio.
  • Chōchin paper lanterns are tied to festivals and izakaya, and the calligraphy on them usually names a shop or dish — a lantern reading 祭 (matsuri) marks a festival.

06

Room & Table Décor

The Room

  1. Wood-framed paper screens and washi paper lamps give the warm, diffused glow that defines a Japanese room. Isamu Noguchi's Akari lamps are the classic modern take.
  2. A short split fabric curtain hung in a doorway to divide space softly and carry a motif. Indigo or a simple seasonal pattern is the most versatile.
  3. Collapsible bamboo-and-paper lanterns — white and minimal for calm, or red-and-white with calligraphy for a matsuri festival feel.
  4. A single focal display — a hanging scroll (kakejiku), one flower arrangement, and perhaps an incense burner. The heart of a traditional room, scaled to any wall.

The Table

  1. Handmade ceramic bowls (celadon, blue-and-white, or rustic raku) and black-and-red lacquer trays — beautiful, tactile, and intentionally a little imperfect.
  2. A tokkuri flask and little ochoko cups (or a wooden masu box) for pouring sake warm or cold — a centrepiece and an activity in one.
  3. A restrained, asymmetric ikebana arrangement or a single bonsai — living sculpture that carries the season into the room.
  4. Furoshiki wrapping cloths as placemats or gift wrap, plus bamboo trays and utensils — natural texture that ties the table together.
07

What to Wear

For Her

  1. A light cotton yukata with an obi sash — comfortable, elegant, and the go-to for summer festivals and relaxed gatherings.
  2. A silk kimono with a haori jacket for a formal occasion — layered, seasonal in colour, and worn with tabi socks and zōri sandals.
  3. A decorative kanzashi hairpin — often seasonal flowers — completes the look with a delicate finishing touch.
  4. A sensu folding fan (or flat uchiwa) — practical in summer heat and a graceful accessory in its own right.

For Him

  1. A matching jinbei top and shorts in cotton or linen — the easiest, most comfortable summer option, ideal for a relaxed festival mood.
  2. A men's cotton yukata with a simple obi — understated and just right for a summer evening or hanami.
  3. A kimono with a haori jacket and wide hakama trousers — the formal, ceremonial men's look.
  4. A short, boldly printed happi coat — the classic matsuri festival jacket, great for a lively, celebratory theme.
08

Music & Sound

Traditional

  1. The 13-string koto — serene and rippling, the quintessential sound of a calm, traditional Japanese setting.
  2. The banjo-like shamisen — bright and percussive, from delicate classical pieces to the driving Tsugaru style.
  3. The breathy bamboo shakuhachi flute — meditative and spare, perfect background for a quiet, wabi-sabi evening.
  4. Thunderous taiko drumming — the pulse of a festival. Nothing signals a matsuri or a big celebration faster.

Modern

  1. Glossy retro city pop (Mariya Takeuchi's "Plastic Love," Tatsuro Yamashita) — hugely back in fashion and perfect for a stylish modern party.
  2. Contemporary J-pop for energy and fun — the everyday soundtrack of modern Japan.
  3. Studio Ghibli scores for something gentle, or high-energy anime openings for a modern-pop theme.
  4. Karaoke ("empty orchestra") is a national pastime — set up a mic and it becomes the party rather than the background.
