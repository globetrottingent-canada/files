NORTH AMERICA / MEXICAN

Décor & Party Guide

Mexico

Two celebrations, two moods — the colour and clatter of a Cinco de Mayo fiesta, and the candlelit remembrance of Día de los Muertos, both built from Mexico's bold, handmade folk art.

01

The Visual Spirit

Mexico's design language is bold, handmade, and full of life — papel picado strung overhead, folk pottery, bright paper flowers, and colour used with joyful abandon. But the two celebrations on this page pull that same toolkit in very different directions. One is a party; the other is a tribute.

Cinco de Mayo is a lively fiesta — vivid, noisy, and celebratory. Día de los Muertos is a warm but reverent family remembrance, built around an ofrenda (altar) that honours loved ones who have died. Both are gorgeous and colourful, but the first fills a room with music and piñatas, while the second glows with candlelight, marigolds, and photographs. Decide which you're throwing before you start — they don't really mix.

Two occasions, two moods

Cinco de Mayo (5 May) — the fiesta. It marks Mexico's 1862 victory over France at the Battle of Puebla; it is not Mexican Independence Day (that's 16 September), and in Mexico it's mostly a Puebla affair — the big party is largely a Mexican-American tradition. Lean on real folk art and skip the giant novelty sombrero and fake moustache, which read as caricature.

Día de los Muertos (1–2 Nov) — a loving remembrance of those who've passed, centred on the ofrenda. It is not "Mexican Halloween." Treat it as the heartfelt tribute it is and it will be genuinely beautiful.

02

Setting the Scene

For a Cinco de Mayo fiesta, go loud with colour. String rows of papel picado across the ceiling, lay the table in bright serape stripes, and cover it with Talavera pottery (fittingly from Puebla), clay dishes, and jars of bold paper flowers. Add cactus and agave, tin (hojalata) ornaments, and a star piñata hung where everyone can see it. Mariachi in the background, agua frescas and a margarita bar, and the whole room should feel like a Mexican mercado in full swing.

For Día de los Muertos, build an ofrenda. Set a tiered altar with framed photographs of the people you're honouring at its heart, then layer on the elements: marigolds (cempasúchil) and petal pathways, candles, copal incense, pan de muerto and sugar skulls, salt and a glass of water, and the departed's favourite foods and drinks. Trim it with papel picado, keep the light warm and low, and let it feel personal and unhurried rather than spooky.

03

Dressing the Room

  • Both: string up papel picado — punched tissue-paper banners. For a fiesta go full rainbow; on an ofrenda the colours carry meaning (orange/yellow for the sun, purple for mourning, white for hope) and the movement represents wind.
  • Fiesta: dress the table in serape stripes and Talavera pottery, with clay dishes and terracotta for a warm, market-stall base.
  • Fiesta: crowd it with bright paper flowers, cactus and agave, tin ornaments, and a hanging star piñata.
  • Ofrenda: build a tiered altar and put framed photos of your loved ones at the centre — the whole thing is for and about them.
  • Ofrenda: bring in marigolds (cempasúchil) everywhere — whole blooms and loose petals laid as a pathway to guide the spirits in.
  • Ofrenda: light candles and copal incense — traditionally a candle for each person remembered — and keep the glow warm and low.
  • Ofrenda: set out pan de muerto, sugar skulls (calaveras), salt, a glass of water, and the departed's favourite foods and drinks.
  • Both: add handmade folk touches — barro clay pottery, tin milagros, and a La Catrina figure — that suit either the party or the altar.

05

Did You Know?

  • Cinco de Mayo isn't Independence Day. It marks Mexico's 1862 victory over the French army at the Battle of Puebla; Independence Day is 16 September. In Mexico the fifth is mostly observed in Puebla — the big celebration grew up in the United States as a Mexican-American tradition.
  • Marigolds guide the dead home. The cempasúchil — the "flower of the dead" — leads spirits to the ofrenda with its blazing orange and strong scent, and petals are scattered as a path from the door to the altar. Its Nahuatl name means "twenty flower."
  • Papel picado isn't just pretty. On an ofrenda it stands for air — one of the four elements honoured, along with water (a glass to drink), earth (food), and fire (candles) — and its colours each carry meaning.
  • La Catrina is modern. The elegant, top-hatted skeleton now synonymous with Día de los Muertos comes from the early-1900s cartoonist José Guadalupe Posada, later popularised by the muralist Diego Rivera.

06

Room & Table Décor

Cinco de Mayo · the Fiesta

  1. Rows of colourful punched tissue-paper banners strung overhead — the fastest way to turn any room into a Mexican fiesta.
  2. Bright striped serape blankets as runners, and Talavera pottery — the blue-and-multicolour tin-glazed ceramics of Puebla, fittingly the Cinco de Mayo city.
  3. Big, bold crepe-paper flowers in every colour, massed in jugs and along the table — a folk craft that carries the whole look.
  4. A classic seven-point star piñata, hung where everyone can admire it (and later swing at it). Part decoration, part main event.

Día de los Muertos · the Ofrenda

  1. A tiered altar with framed photographs of loved ones at its heart, dressed with cloths, papel picado, and the elements below. The centre of the whole celebration.
  2. Vivid orange marigolds — whole blooms on the altar and loose petals laid as a pathway to guide the spirits home. The essential Día de los Muertos flower.
  3. Candles (traditionally one per soul remembered) for guiding light, and copal incense whose aroma cleanses the space and welcomes the spirits.
  4. Sweet "bread of the dead" and decorated sugar skulls (often named for the departed), set out with salt, water, and the loved one's favourite food and drink.
07

What to Wear

Cinco de Mayo

  1. A hand-embroidered floral blouse (Otomí or Oaxacan style) — colourful, authentic, and comfortable. The everyday-festive Mexican look.
  2. The traditional dress of Puebla — an embroidered, sequined skirt and blouse. Especially fitting for Cinco de Mayo, given the day's Puebla roots.
  3. The elegant charro suit — fitted trousers, short jacket, and silver buttons — worn by mariachis and Mexican horsemen. The dignified alternative to a novelty sombrero.
  4. A woven rebozo shawl in bold colour, thrown over the shoulders — practical, beautiful, and very Mexican. Skip the fake moustache and oversized hat.

Día de los Muertos

  1. Dress as La Catrina (or the male Catrín): a refined, almost Victorian outfit — a long dress or dark suit — worn with a big flowered hat. Elegant, never gruesome.
  2. The ornate skull face — flowers, filigree, and colour around the eyes. Done with care it's a beautiful tribute; keep it artful rather than a scary Halloween mask.
  3. A crown of marigolds and bright blooms — the finishing touch for a Catrina look, and lovely on its own.
  4. A floral embroidered dress or shirt in the orange, purple, and black of the season — dressed-up and heartfelt rather than costume-y.
08

Music & Sound

Fiesta Sounds

  1. Trumpets, violins, guitarrón, and vihuela — the joyous, instantly recognisable heart of a Mexican celebration. Live if you can, on a playlist if you can't.
  2. Big, heartfelt classics (think "Cielito Lindo" — everyone knows the "ay, ay, ay, ay" chorus) that get the whole room singing along.
  3. Booming brass banda and accordion-led norteño — driving, danceable regional styles that keep the energy high.
  4. Irresistibly danceable cumbia — the tempo that fills a floor and keeps a fiesta moving well into the night.

For the Ofrenda

  1. The mournful, beautiful ballad long associated with Día de los Muertos — a fittingly reflective centrepiece for the evening.
  2. The gentle, resonant marimba — warm and unhurried, right for a candlelit remembrance rather than a party.
  3. Regional sones and quiet guitar or bolero — the softer, traditional music heard at vigils and around the ofrenda.
  4. Día de los Muertos is personal — playing the music the departed loved is one of the warmest tributes you can offer.
