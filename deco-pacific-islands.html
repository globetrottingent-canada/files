OCEANIA / PACIFIC

Décor & Party Guide

The Pacific Islands

Two very different parties share this map: the retro tiki bar — a mid-century American fantasy — and the real islands of Polynesia, Melanesia, and Micronesia. Here's how to throw either.

01

The Visual Spirit

This page carries two looks that happen to share a corner of the map but come from completely different places. The first is Tiki — the amber-lit bar of carved idols, bamboo, rum, and lounge music. It's enormous fun, but it's important to know it's a fantasy: "Polynesian Pop," dreamed up in 1930s California, an American daydream of the South Seas rather than any real island culture.

The second is the authentic Pacific — the living cultures of Fiji, Samoa, Tonga, Tahiti, and Papua New Guinea. That look is built from the earth-oven feast (umu or lovo), tapa barkcloth, woven pandanus mats, the ceremonial kava bowl, coconut and banana leaf, and open, generous communal hospitality. Both are here. Pick the one that fits your night — or borrow tastefully from each, as long as you know which is which.

Two ways to do it

Tiki / Polynesian Pop — the retro cocktail-bar fantasy invented by Donn Beach and Trader Vic. Gloriously kitschy, rum-soaked, and escapist. Best for a fun grown-up cocktail party.

Authentic Pacific — the real cultures of the islands, centred on the communal feast and the kava circle. Best for honouring the places themselves. Treat sacred elements (kava, ceremony, dance) with respect rather than as gags.

Looking for the specifically Hawaiian version? That has its own page — see Hawaii.

02

Setting the Scene

For a tiki night, build a bar. Line a corner with bamboo and rattan, add a thatch valance, and drop the lights to a warm amber glow — pufferfish lamps, glass float lights, a string or two of little lanterns. Populate it with carved tiki idols and a shelf of collectable tiki mugs, hang fishing floats and netting, and make rum the star. A loop of exotica in the background completes the illusion.

For an authentic island feast, think low and communal. Lay banana or ti leaves straight down the centre of the table, set everything on woven pandanus mats, and hang or drape tapa barkcloth for pattern. Serve from coconut shells and wooden bowls, and give the table a real centrepiece — a carved tanoa kava bowl, tropical flowers (hibiscus, frangipani, heliconia), and torch or firelight. The earth oven, or a stand-in of slow-roasted pork and taro, is what the whole gathering orbits.

03

Dressing the Room

  • Set the light warm and low — tiki: amber bulbs, pufferfish and float lamps, tiki torches. Authentic: torch and firelight ringing the feast.
  • Bring in natural structure — tiki: bamboo, rattan, and a thatch valance for the bar. Authentic: woven pandanus mats and baskets on and around the table.
  • Choose your wall pieces — tiki: carved idols, masks, and a shelf of tiki mugs. Authentic: tapa barkcloth (masi, ngatu, or siapo) hung as art or run down the table.
  • Add the props — tiki: fishing floats, nets, and driftwood. Authentic: coconut shells, carved wooden bowls, and a tanoa kava bowl as centrepiece.
  • Flowers work for both — hibiscus and frangipani, loose in bowls, tucked into arrangements, or floated in water.
  • Pile on real greenery — banana leaves, monstera, palm fronds, and ti — as runners, plates, and backdrop for either look.
  • Dress the table — tiki: bold vintage barkcloth-print cloths and mugs. Authentic: banana-leaf place settings and shared communal bowls.

05

Did You Know?

  • Tiki is American, not Pacific. "Polynesian Pop" was invented in 1930s Los Angeles by Donn Beach (Don the Beachcomber) and Victor "Trader Vic" Bergeron — a stylised fantasy of the South Seas. The Mai Tai, the Zombie, and the ceramic tiki mug all come from this bar scene, not from any island tradition.
  • Exotica — tiki's lounge soundtrack, pioneered by Martin Denny ("Quiet Village") and Les Baxter — is equally a studio creation, layering jazz with bird calls and vibraphone to conjure an imagined jungle paradise.
  • Real Pacific barkcloth (tapa) goes by different names across the islands — ngatu in Tonga, siapo in Samoa, masi in Fiji, kapa in Hawaii — beaten from mulberry bark and still made by hand for weddings, funerals, and gifts of honour.
  • Kava, drunk from a carved wooden tanoa bowl, is a genuine ceremonial centrepiece across Fiji, Samoa, Tonga, and Vanuatu, governed by real protocol that signals welcome and respect — worlds away from a novelty cocktail.

06

Room & Table Décor

The Tiki Bar

  1. Bamboo poles, rattan furniture, and a thatch valance are the backbone of the tiki look — instant "little grass shack" atmosphere for a bar corner.
  2. Grinning carved tiki idols and a shelf of collectable ceramic tiki mugs — the signature (and knowingly kitschy) icon of the whole aesthetic.
  3. Dried pufferfish lanterns and glass fishing-float lights cast the low amber glow that defines a classic tiki room.
  4. Thatch matting, draped fishing nets, glass floats, and driftwood — the "scrounged from the waterfront" clutter that Donn Beach built the original look from.

The Island Feast · Authentic

  1. Hand-beaten barkcloth in geometric earth tones — hung as a wall piece or run down the table. The single most authentic Pacific textile you can use.
  2. Handwoven mats and baskets from pandanus leaf — laid on the table or the floor for a genuine communal-feast setting.
  3. Big glossy leaves as table runners, platters, and plates — exactly how food is wrapped and served from the earth oven.
  4. A carved wooden tanoa (kava bowl) as centrepiece, with coconut-shell cups and bowls — the heart of Pacific hospitality. Handle with respect.
07

What to Wear

Tiki / Retro

  1. A bold 1950s-style rayon aloha shirt is the tiki uniform — the louder and more retro, the better.
  2. A halter or wiggle dress in a big tropical barkcloth print — think cocktail-hour pin-up, cat-eye sunglasses optional.
  3. A single hibiscus behind the ear is the low-effort finishing touch that reads instantly tropical.
  4. Honestly, the most important tiki accessory is a well-garnished mug — paper umbrella, mint, and a wedge of pineapple.

Island / Authentic

  1. The wraparound skirt worn across the Pacific by men and women — sulu in Fiji, lavalava in Samoa, pareu in Tahiti. Comfortable, respectful, and genuinely island.
  2. A flowing floral dress in an island print — modest, breezy, and worn everywhere from church to celebration.
  3. A fresh flower or leaf garland — salusalu in Fiji, ei in the Cook Islands — given and worn as a mark of welcome and honour.
  4. A fragrant tiare (Tahitian gardenia) or frangipani flower, often behind the ear, plus bare feet — simple and true to the islands.
08

Music & Sound

Tiki Lounge

  1. Martin Denny's "Quiet Village" and Les Baxter's "Ritual of the Savage" are the quintessential tiki soundtrack — lush, kitschy, and perfect for cocktail hour.
  2. Reverb-drenched surf guitar from the same California scene — a livelier, up-tempo option to keep a tiki party moving.
  3. Old Hawaiian-themed songs with English lyrics and sliding steel guitar — the "Hawaiian standards" that play in every classic Trader Vic's.
  4. Bongos, vibraphone, and piped-in bird and jungle sounds — the little audio flourishes that complete the tiki illusion.

Pacific Sound · Authentic

  1. Hollowed wooden slit drums drive Pacific rhythm — the toʻere of Tahiti, the lali of Fiji and Tonga, the garamut of Papua New Guinea.
  2. The fast, hip-shaking drum dance of Tahiti — thrilling to watch and the highlight of the Heiva festival.
  3. The Fijian meke (chant-and-dance storytelling) and the graceful Samoan siva — living dance traditions rather than floor-show spectacle.
  4. Warm ukulele-and-guitar string bands and contemporary island reggae — the everyday, feel-good music of the modern Pacific.

Party Touches & Activities

The Tiki Bar

  1. Rum: Mai Tai, Zombie, Painkiller
  2. Serve in tiki mugs
  3. One flaming drink (carefully!)
  4. Exotica on the turntable
  5. Limbo contest
  6. Cocktail-glam dress code

The Island Feast

  1. Umu / lovo earth-oven feast
  2. Kava circle (with respect)
  3. Torch-lit & fireside
  4. Learn a meke or ʻōteʻa
  5. Coconut everything
  6. Toast — "Bula!" / "Manuia!"

Regional Flavours

  1. Fijian — kava, meke, masi
  2. Samoan / Tongan — umu, siva, ngatu
  3. Tahitian — Heiva, ʻōteʻa, tiare
  4. Melanesian / PNG — garamut, sing-sing
  5. Cook Islands — ei, ika mata
