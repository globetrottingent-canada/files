BRITISH ISLES / HIGHLAND

Décor & Party Guide

Scotland

Tartan and firelight — a Burns Supper or a ceilidh done right: candlelit tables, clan crests and the Saltire, a dram in hand, and the skirl of the pipes.

01

The Visual Spirit

A Scottish gathering is built from two things above all: tartan and firelight. Whether it's a Burns Supper, a ceilidh, or a Hogmanay, the feeling is warm, convivial, and a little bit grand — candlelit tables laid with tartan and pewter, a dram of whisky in hand, and the skirl of the pipes cutting through the room. The palette comes straight from the Highlands: heather purple, thistle, stag-brown tweed, saltire blue, and whisky amber, set against dark wood, stone, and candle glow. Think baronial hall meets cosy hearth.

The through-line is clan and craft — the tartan sett, the clan crest, the thistle and the Saltire, the two-handled quaich passed round for a toast. Lean on real materials (wool, tweed, pewter, antler, candle, and heather) and the room reads unmistakably Scottish and genuinely warm, rather than like a costume shop.

Two registers

Authentic Highland (this page) — tartan and clan, the Burns Supper, the ceilidh, whisky and the quaich. Handsome, warm, and grounded in real tradition.

Tartan-tourist kitsch — "See You Jimmy" wigs, inflatable bagpipes, Nessie everything, and novelty everything-in-tartan. It's a laugh, and a wink or two is fine — just remember a little goes a long way.

02

Setting the Scene

Dress the table in tartan — a full cloth or a runner in a clan sett, or a universal one like Black Watch or Royal Stewart — and light it with candles in silver or pewter. Add sprigs of thistle and heather, a centrepiece of whisky and a quaich, and darker accents of antler, tweed, and greenery. On the walls, hang clan crests, a blue-and-white Saltire or the red-and-gold Lion Rampant, and a bit of stag imagery. Keep the light low and the wood dark for that baronial-hall-by-candlelight feeling.

Then leave room to move: a ceilidh is a dancing party, so clear a floor for Strip the Willow and the Gay Gordons. If it's a Burns Supper, give the haggis a place of honour — it gets piped in and addressed before anyone eats — with tatties and neeps alongside and whisky ready for the toasts.

03

Dressing the Room

  • Lay tartan down the table — a cloth or runner in a clan tartan, or a universal sett like Black Watch or Royal Stewart if you've no clan of your own.
  • Light it with candles in pewter or silver — candelabra, tapers, and a low, warm glow throughout.
  • Bring in thistle and heather — sprigs in jugs, tucked into napkins, or run down the centre as a garland.
  • Add Highland texture — tweed and tartan throws over chairs, sheepskin, antler, and plenty of dark wood.
  • Fly the flags and crests — the Saltire (St Andrew's Cross) and the Lion Rampant, plus your clan crest on the wall.
  • Set a proper whisky bar — a spread of Scotch, heavy tumblers, and the two-handled quaich for shared toasts.
  • Clear a floor for the ceilidh — the dancing is the party, so leave space for a caller and a reel.
  • (Burns Supper) give the haggis a grand entrance — piped in on a platter to a place of honour, with tatties and neeps.

05

Did You Know?

  • Over 7,000 tartans are recorded on the Scottish Register of Tartans, each tied to a clan, region, regiment, or institution. If your surname is Scottish a tartan may already be "yours" — and universal setts like Black Watch and Royal Stewart are open to everyone.
  • The sgian-dubh ("black knife") worn in the sock comes from an old custom: a guest gave up his weapons on entering a home but kept this small blade in plain sight, to show he had nothing to hide.
  • A Burns Supper (25 January) follows a set order — guests rise as the haggis is piped in, someone performs Burns's "Address to a Haggis" and cuts it open, and the night runs through the Selkirk Grace, the Immortal Memory, and the Toast to the Lassies.
  • The quaich — a shallow two-handled cup — is Scotland's "cup of friendship," offered with both hands so neither person could reach for a weapon. It's still used for whisky toasts at weddings and welcomes.

06

Room & Table Décor

The Table

  1. A tartan tablecloth or runner is the foundation of the whole look. Use a clan sett if you have one, or a universal tartan like Black Watch or Royal Stewart.
  2. Pewter or silver candelabra, tankards, and tapers. Keep the lighting low for the baronial-hall-by-candle feeling that anchors a Scottish table.
  3. Scotland's national flower and its moorland companion — sprigs in jugs, tucked into napkins, or run down the table as a simple garland.
  4. A two-handled quaich as centrepiece and a spread of Scotch for the toasts — the social heart of the evening. Offer the quaich with both hands.

Highland Accents

  1. Harris Tweed and tartan blankets over chairs and sofas, with sheepskin for texture — the cosy, layered feel of a Highland lodge.
  2. Antlers, a stag motif, or a "Monarch of the Glen" print bring the hunting-lodge grandeur without much effort.
  3. The blue-and-white Saltire (St Andrew's Cross) and the red-and-gold Lion Rampant — as flags, bunting, or a single bold banner over the room.
  4. A framed clan crest or coat of arms on the wall adds authenticity and a personal, heritage touch to the room.
07

What to Wear

For Her

  1. A tartan sash worn over one shoulder and pinned with a brooch is the classic women's Highland touch — often in a birth or married clan tartan.
  2. A long tartan skirt or dress with a white blouse and fitted vest — the traditional women's outfit, elegant and easy to dance in.
  3. A tonnag (small shawl) or a larger earasaid wrap in tartan, fastened with a brooch — a graceful, historically grounded layer.
  4. Soft laced ghillie shoes for dancing, finished with a sprig of thistle or heather. Comfort matters when the reels start.

For Him

  1. The centrepiece — a kilt in your clan tartan, or a universal sett. Tartan trews are the traditional alternative if a kilt isn't your thing.
  2. The pouch worn at the front of the kilt (kilts have no pockets). Plain leather for day, fur or silver-mounted "dress" sporran for a formal night.
  3. The small sgian-dubh knife in the right sock (handle showing), plus kilt hose, garter flashes, and a kilt pin. The details make the outfit.
  4. A Prince Charlie jacket for black-tie formality or an Argyll for something more relaxed, finished with laced ghillie brogues.
08

Music & Sound

The Ceilidh

  1. Fiddle, accordion, and drums with a caller who walks everyone through the steps — the beating heart of a Scottish party. No experience needed.
  2. A fast, spinning line dance that gets the whole room involved — chaotic, joyful, and usually the highlight of the night.
  3. A simple, marching couples dance — a friendly opener that even total beginners can manage after one run-through.
  4. Danced in sets of three facing three — sociable and easy to learn, keeping everyone mixing all evening.

Pipes & Songs

  1. The Great Highland Bagpipe — for piping in the haggis or the guests, or just as a playlist. The unmistakable sound of Scotland.
  2. Robert Burns's most famous song, sung in a crossed-hands circle at midnight on Hogmanay — and a fitting close to any Scottish gathering.
  3. Dougie MacLean's "Caledonia," the "Skye Boat Song," and other ballads for the softer, sing-along moments of the evening.
  4. For a contemporary lift — Runrig's Celtic rock and The Proclaimers' anthems get a crowd singing at full volume.
