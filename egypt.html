Egypt

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Egyptian cuisine is among the oldest continuously practised food cultures in the world — a tradition stretching back over 7,000 years of Nile Valley agriculture, shaped by one of history's greatest civilisations and refined through successive waves of Greek, Roman, Arab, Ottoman, and British influence. The ancient Egyptians were among the first peoples to bake leavened bread, brew beer, and cultivate a systematic agricultural food supply sufficient to support a population of millions. The foods they grew — wheat, barley, lentils, fava beans, onions, garlic, leeks, and figs — remain at the heart of the Egyptian diet today.

The Arab conquest of 641 CE brought Islam, the Arabic language, and a new culinary vocabulary that transformed Egyptian food — spice use deepened, new dishes arrived from the broader Arab world, and the Egyptian kitchen absorbed Levantine, Persian, and North African influences through its position at the crossroads of trade routes connecting Africa, Asia, and the Mediterranean. The Ottoman period from 1517 to 1798 added further layers, particularly in confectionery and rice traditions. Napoleon's expedition of 1798 and the subsequent British occupation introduced European cooking techniques to the upper classes, and cosmopolitan Alexandria developed a Mediterranean-influenced food culture all its own.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Egyptian cooking is rooted in pragmatism and abundance — a cuisine built to feed a very large, predominantly poor population from the extraordinarily fertile Nile Delta. Legumes are the great protein source: ful medames (fava beans) is eaten for breakfast by virtually every Egyptian regardless of class, and lentils, chickpeas, and split peas appear in soups, stews, and the national dish of koshary. Bread — aish baladi, the government-subsidised thick whole-wheat flatbread — is eaten with every meal and used to scoop, wrap, and carry everything.

Molokhia — the viscous, intensely flavoured green soup made from the leaves of the jute plant — is perhaps the most distinctively Egyptian dish, its slippery texture and deep green colour either beloved or startling depending on the diner. Cooked with garlic and coriander fried in clarified butter, poured over rice, it is a dish of ancient lineage and total national ownership. Koshary — the combination of rice, lentils, pasta, chickpeas, crispy fried onions, and spiced tomato sauce — is the street food that defines contemporary Egypt: cheap, filling, satisfying, and proudly incoherent in its origins.

03

At the Table

  • Ful medames — slow-cooked fava beans — is the national breakfast, eaten from Alexandria to Aswan every morning. A ten-pound pot of ful feeds a family for pennies and has done so for 5,000 years.
  • Egyptian bread — aish baladi, the thick, slightly sour whole-wheat flatbread — is subsidised by the government as a staple right. Egyptians eat more bread per capita than almost any other nation on earth.
  • Meals are communal and generous — dishes are placed in the centre and shared. An Egyptian host will insist guests eat far more than they planned.
  • Tea is the social glue — heavily sweetened black tea is drunk throughout the day at every social occasion, in every tea house, and after every meal.
  • Street food culture is profound — the koshary cart, the ful and ta'ameya stand, and the hawawshi baker are the alimentary backbone of Egyptian urban life.
  • Ramadan is the greatest food occasion of the year — the iftar breaking of the fast with dates, soup, and a full spread, followed by the late-night suhoor meal, transforms the food calendar for a full month.

Did You Know?

  • Koshary — Egypt's national dish of rice, lentils, pasta, chickpeas, crispy onions, and spiced tomato sauce — developed in the 19th century as Egypt absorbed Italian pasta, Indian lentil cooking, and its own rice and legume traditions under Cairo's cosmopolitan population.
  • Egypt has been continuously farmed for over 7,000 years, making the Nile Delta one of the oldest agricultural landscapes on earth. Ancient Egyptians cultivated wheat, barley, flax, onions, garlic, and figs in quantities sufficient to support one of the ancient world's largest urban populations.
  • Dukkah — the Egyptian spice and nut blend of sesame, coriander, cumin, and hazelnuts or almonds — has become fashionable internationally, but in Egypt it's eaten simply: bread dipped in olive oil, then pressed into dukkah. The word means 'to pound' in Arabic.
  • Ta'ameya — Egyptian falafel — is made from fava beans rather than chickpeas, distinguishing it from Levantine falafel. Egyptians consider the chickpea version an inferior imitation.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. A layered bowl of rice, lentils, pasta, and chickpeas topped with crispy onions and spiced tomato sauce.
  2. Slow-cooked fava beans dressed with oil, lemon, and cumin, Egypt's daily national breakfast.
  3. A viscous, garlicky green soup made from jute leaves, poured over rice.
  4. Flatbread stuffed with spiced minced meat and baked or grilled until crisp.
  5. Vegetables such as zucchini, peppers, or cabbage stuffed with spiced rice.

More Mains

  1. A warm, rich bread pudding baked with milk, nuts, and raisins, Egypt's beloved dessert-main crossover.
  2. Skewered and grilled spiced minced meat, seasoned simply with onion and parsley.
  3. A flaky, buttery layered pastry, eaten sweet or savory, often called Egyptian pancake.
  4. Fish cooked with rice in a fragrant, spiced fish stock, a coastal specialty.
  5. Rice slow-cooked in milk until creamy, a simple comforting dessert-main.

Pastries & Sweets

More Sweets

  4. A richer version of Om Ali topped with clotted cream.

Snacks & Street Food

Street Classics

  1. Egyptian falafel made from fava beans rather than chickpeas, giving it a greener color and earthier flavor.
  2. Whole-wheat flatbread dipped in olive oil and pressed into a nutty spice blend.
  3. Flatbread stuffed with spiced minced meat and baked or grilled until crisp.
  4. A flaky, buttery layered pastry, eaten sweet or savory, often called Egyptian pancake.

More Snacks

  1. Small crescent-shaped pastries filled with spiced meat or cheese and fried.
  2. Slow-cooked fava beans dressed with oil, lemon, and cumin, eaten any time of day.
  3. Spiced minced meat grilled on a skewer over charcoal.
  4. Fried potato cubes tossed with garlic, chili, and coriander.
