Cuisine Guide

The English Caribbean

Barbados, Grenada, St. Lucia, Antigua, Dominica, Guyana, and the Dutch Antilles — rum, rice and peas, and a West African foundation adapted over four centuries.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

The English-speaking Caribbean encompasses the former British colonies stretching from Barbados and the Windward Islands through the Leeward Islands to Guyana on the South American mainland, and extends to include the Dutch Antilles of Curaçao and Aruba whose food culture shares more with the English-speaking tradition than with the French. These islands share a common history of British plantation colonialism built on sugar, the transatlantic slave trade, and the West African culinary knowledge that enslaved people brought with them — and it is that African foundation, adapted to Caribbean ingredients over four centuries, that defines the flavour of the English-speaking Caribbean kitchen.

The British colonial period from the 17th century brought salt fish — preserved cod from the North Atlantic — which became the protein of the enslaved population when fresh fish was unavailable, and is now an honoured ingredient in the national dishes of virtually every English-speaking island. Breadfruit, brought to the Caribbean from the Pacific by Captain Bligh specifically to feed enslaved people cheaply, became so thoroughly embedded in Caribbean cooking that it is now considered a native ingredient. The Creole synthesis of British, African, Indian, and indigenous Amerindian culinary traditions produced the rum punch, rice and peas, pepperpot, and the distinctive spice palette of allspice, thyme, scotch bonnet, and coconut milk.

02

Character of the Cuisine

English Caribbean cooking is warm, direct, and built on simple excellence of ingredients. The Sunday rice and peas — kidney beans or gungo peas cooked with coconut milk and thyme, producing a fragrant, slightly creamy rice — is the dish that most connects the islands. Curried goat, brought by Indian indentured workers and now as Caribbean as anything else, is the party food of choice across the islands. Cou-cou and flying fish — Barbados's national dish of cornmeal and okra polenta with steamed flying fish — is a direct descendant of West African fufu and fish traditions, Caribbeanized into something entirely its own.

Rum is the region's greatest contribution to world drinks culture — Barbados claims the oldest rum distillation records (1620s), and Mount Gay, founded in 1703, is the oldest rum brand still in production. The rum punch — one of sour, two of sweet, three of strong, four of weak — is the universal welcome drink, mixed fresh at every gathering. Each island has its own rum: Barbados's elegant pot-still rums, Guyana's rich Demerara, and the lighter column-still rums of the smaller islands each have distinct characters. Grenada, the spice island, produces nutmeg, mace, cinnamon, cloves, and cocoa that give its cooking an aromatic distinctiveness.

03

At the Table

  • The rum punch is the universal welcome drink — every island has its own ratio, but the formula is consistent: one of sour (lime), two of sweet (syrup), three of strong (rum), four of weak (water or juice).
  • Sunday lunch is the most important meal of the week — rice and peas, fried chicken or stewed meat, macaroni pie, and coleslaw, eaten with the extended family after church.
  • Fish is the daily protein of coastal communities — flying fish in Barbados, red snapper across the islands, salt fish everywhere.
  • Hot sauce is a table fixture — each island has its own pepper sauce tradition, from mild Bajan to incendiary Scotch bonnet-based sauces. Food without pepper sauce is considered unfinished.
  • The Dutch Antilles add their own footnote — Curaçao's keshi yena (Edam cheese stuffed with spiced meat and baked), Indonesian-influenced rijsttafel, and the uniquely coloured Blue Curaçao liqueur.
  • Carnival seasons across the islands bring out specific festival foods — conkies, black cake, and pepperpot are associated with particular seasons and celebrations.

Did You Know?

  • Flying fish — the small, wing-finned fish that genuinely glides above the Atlantic waves — is so central to Barbadian identity that it appears on the country's coat of arms. Cou-cou and flying fish is the national dish.
  • Pepper pot — the Amerindian-origin stew preserved with cassareep — is perhaps the oldest continuously made dish in the Caribbean. Traditional pots are said to have been kept simmering for decades, with new ingredients added as old ones are consumed.
  • Rum originated in the Caribbean — the first distillation of sugarcane into rum is recorded in Barbados in the 1620s or 1630s, making it the oldest rum-producing country in the world. Mount Gay, founded 1703, is the oldest rum brand still in production.
  • Conch — the large sea snail whose shell is a symbol of the Caribbean — is the protein of the Bahamas and Turks and Caicos most of all, but appears across the English-speaking Caribbean in fritters, salads, and chowders.
Food & Drink Reference

The dishes, sweets, and drinks that define the everyday table.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. A cornmeal and okra polenta paired with steamed flying fish in tomato pepper sauce, Barbados's national dish.
  2. A slow-simmered meat stew preserved with cassareep, one of the oldest continuously made Caribbean dishes.
  3. Rice and kidney or gungo peas cooked in coconut milk with thyme, the backbone of Sunday lunch.
  4. Goat meat slow-cooked in a fragrant curry, the celebratory party dish of the islands.
  5. Sautéed ackee fruit with flaked salt cod, onion, and pepper.

More Mains

  1. Oxtail slow-braised until tender in a rich, dark gravy.
  2. Chopped conch meat mixed into a savory batter and deep-fried.
  3. Breadfruit and salted meat stewed down in coconut milk until deeply savory, Grenada's national dish.
  4. A light broth of fish and root vegetables, a coastal comfort food.
  5. Soft flatbread wrapped around a curried filling, a Trinidadian-influenced staple.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Cakes & Puddings

  1. A dense fruit cake soaked in rum and wine, the centerpiece of Caribbean Christmas and weddings.
  2. Sweetened cornmeal and coconut steamed in banana leaf, a Barbadian Crop Over treat.
  3. A dense, spiced pudding made from grated sweet potato and coconut milk.
  4. A dense, lightly sweet bread enriched with coconut milk and shredded coconut.

More Sweets

  1. Tamarind pulp rolled with sugar into a sweet-tart chewy candy.
  2. A dense, chewy candy of coconut cooked with molasses and ginger.
  3. A simple, dense candy of grated coconut cooked with sugar.
  4. Sweetened cornmeal pudding steamed in a banana leaf, similar to conkies.

Snacks & Street Food

Classics

  1. Fried or baked dough served alongside sautéed salt fish, a beloved breakfast.
  2. Deep-fried fritters of salt fish and seasoned batter.
  3. Two fried flatbreads sandwiching curried chickpeas, a Trinidadian-influenced street food.
  4. Thin slices of breadfruit fried until crisp.

More Snacks

  1. A hearty soup of corn and dumplings, a popular street food.
  2. Soft flatbread wrapped around a curried filling, a Trinidadian-influenced staple.
  3. Ripe plantain slices fried until caramelized and sweet.
  4. Small fried fritters of salt fish and seasoned batter.
