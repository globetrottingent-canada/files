Cuisine Guide

Ethiopia

Injera and wat, berbere and the coffee ceremony — one of the world's oldest, most self-contained food cultures.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Ethiopian cuisine is one of the most ancient, self-contained, and distinctive food cultures on earth — a tradition that developed in near-total isolation from outside influence for thousands of years, producing a cuisine unlike anything found elsewhere. Ethiopia sits in the Horn of Africa at the intersection of ancient trade routes linking the Red Sea, the Nile valley, and the Indian Ocean, and its highland geography — a vast plateau rising above 2,000 metres — created the conditions for the cultivation of unique crops found nowhere else: teff, enset, korarima, and gesho. The Aksumite Empire, one of the great civilisations of antiquity, was trading with Rome, Persia, India, and Arabia by the 1st century CE, and some spice trade influence filtered into the cuisine. But the core of Ethiopian food remained stubbornly, magnificently its own.

The Ethiopian Orthodox Christian tradition, which dates to the 4th century CE, is the single most important cultural force shaping the cuisine. Approximately 180 fasting days per year during which meat, fish, and dairy are prohibited have driven the development of one of the world’s most sophisticated vegan cooking traditions — the array of lentil, chickpea, split pea, and vegetable stews that make up the fasting table are dishes of genuine complexity and nutritional completeness. The Islamic communities of Harar and the eastern lowlands contributed their own distinct culinary layer, with spice use and cooking techniques that differ from the Christian highland tradition. Somali, Yemeni, and Indian Ocean trade influence appears in the coastal and eastern cooking.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Ethiopian cooking is built around the injera-wat combination — the large, spongy sourdough flatbread made from teff flour, fermented for several days until deeply sour, and the rich, spiced stews called wat that are placed upon it. Injera is simultaneously the plate, the utensil, and a substantial part of the meal itself, its tangy sourness providing essential contrast to the fiery richness of berbere-spiced wats. Berbere — the master spice blend of dried chilies, fenugreek, coriander, cinnamon, and the uniquely Ethiopian korarima cardamom — underpins most meat stews and is one of the most complex spice preparations in any cuisine.

Niter kibbeh — spiced clarified butter infused with onion, garlic, ginger, and a dozen aromatics — is the cooking fat of the Ethiopian highland kitchen, providing a deep, warm richness to stews and fried dishes that plain butter or oil cannot replicate. The doro wat — a chicken stew in berbere and niter kibbeh with whole hard-boiled eggs — is the national dish and the centrepiece of every celebration meal, served at weddings, Timkat, and Ethiopian Christmas. Kitfo — minced raw beef seasoned with mitmita and niter kibbeh — is Ethiopia’s steak tartare and a mark of serious hospitality. The coffee ceremony, performed daily across the country, is as much a social institution as a means of consuming caffeine.

03

At the Table

  • Injera is both plate and utensil — the large spongy sourdough flatbread is spread on a communal platter and the stews and salads placed directly on top. Diners tear pieces and scoop, eating without cutlery.
  • Eating from a shared platter is deeply embedded in Ethiopian hospitality — gursha, the act of placing a rolled piece of food directly into another person’s mouth, is a gesture of affection, celebration, and deep respect.
  • Orthodox Christian fasting days shape the weekly food calendar — approximately 180 fasting days per year prohibit meat, fish, and dairy, making Ethiopia’s vegan cuisine one of the most sophisticated in the world.
  • Coffee ceremonies are a central social institution — raw beans are roasted over charcoal, ground by hand, brewed in a jebena clay pot, and served in three rounds: the first most potent, each subsequent round weaker.
  • Tej — honey wine — is Ethiopia’s ancient mead, brewed with wild hops called gesho that give it a distinctive bitterness. It is served in a berele, a round-bottomed flask, and is drunk at celebrations and tej houses.
  • The quantity of food placed before a guest signals the depth of welcome — an Ethiopian host will pile the injera high with multiple wats and salads, and insisting a guest eat more is the language of love.

Did You Know?

  • Ethiopia is the birthplace of coffee — the wild coffee plant, Coffea arabica, originates in the forests of the Kaffa region in southwestern Ethiopia, where it has grown for over a thousand years. Legend holds that a goat herder named Kaldi noticed his goats became unusually energetic after eating the red berries, leading to the discovery of coffee. Ethiopia remains one of the world’s top coffee producers, with single-origin beans from Yirgacheffe, Sidama, and Harrar among the most prized specialty coffees globally.
  • Injera is made from teff, an ancient grain native to the Ethiopian highlands and one of the oldest cultivated grains on earth. Teff is approximately the size of a poppy seed, grows at high altitudes where no other grain survives, and is extraordinarily nutrient-dense. It has been cultivated in Ethiopia for at least 3,000 years and remains the country’s most important staple crop.
  • Berbere contains up to twenty ingredients including dried chilies, fenugreek, coriander, black pepper, cinnamon, cardamom, and the distinctively Ethiopian spice korarima. Each household and region has its own berbere recipe, and its preparation is considered a mark of a cook’s skill and family tradition.
  • Ethiopia was never colonized by a European power — it is one of only two African countries (with Liberia) to have maintained full independence throughout the colonial era. This means Ethiopian cuisine developed entirely on its own terms, still recognizable from medieval accounts of Ethiopian court cuisine.

The stews, breads, and drinks that define the everyday Ethiopian table.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Wats & Meats

  1. Chicken simmered in berbere and niter kibbeh with whole hard-boiled eggs; Ethiopia's national dish.
  2. Cubed meat pan-seared or sautéed with onion, garlic, and rosemary, served sizzling.
  3. Minced raw beef seasoned with mitmita and niter kibbeh, Ethiopia's answer to steak tartare.
  4. A smooth, spiced stew of ground chickpea or broad bean flour, a staple of fasting days.
  5. Red lentils simmered in berbere until thick and deeply spiced.

Vegan Fasting Dishes

  1. Collard greens stewed with garlic, ginger, and onion, a staple fasting-day side.
  2. A mild turmeric-spiced stew of cabbage, carrot, and potato.
  3. Lamb slow-cooked in a rich berbere and niter kibbeh sauce.
  4. Minced tripe, liver, and beef sautéed with chili and spiced butter.
  5. Torn injera tossed in berbere sauce or leftover wat, a common breakfast.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Breads & Porridge

  1. A lightly sweetened, cardamom-scented round bread baked for holidays and celebrations.
  2. Phyllo pastry layered with nuts and honey, adopted from Greek and Italian bakeries into the Ethiopian sweet table.
  3. A thick barley porridge served with a well of spiced butter and berbere, often for breakfast.
  4. A simple cracked-wheat porridge served with butter, a homely everyday breakfast.

Snacks & Sweets

  1. Injera torn and warmed through berbere sauce, eaten as a quick meal or leftover breakfast.
  2. Small fried sweet dough pieces, a simple treat often served with coffee.
  3. Small crunchy fried wheat crackers seasoned with berbere or sugar, eaten as a snack.
  4. Roasted barley flour mixed into a paste or drink, a traditional energy-dense snack.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Classics

  1. Crisp fried triangular pastries filled with spiced lentils or meat, a popular street snack.
  2. Small crunchy fried wheat crackers seasoned with berbere or sugar.
  3. Roasted barley, chickpeas, and peanuts, a simple everyday snack sold on the street.
  4. Torn injera tossed in berbere sauce, eaten as a snack or light meal.

More Snacks

  1. A layered, pan-fried flatbread, sometimes filled with egg or honey, of Yemeni-influenced origin.
  2. Shredded flatbread pan-fried with berbere and spiced butter, a hearty breakfast snack.
  3. Scrambled eggs cooked with onion, tomato, and chili, a common breakfast dish.
  4. Injera toasted and brushed with berbere and niter kibbeh, similar to a spiced flatbread.
