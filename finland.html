Cuisine Guide

Finland

Rye, lake fish, and foraged berries — a Nordic table shaped by forest, sauna, and centuries between Sweden and Russia.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Finnish cuisine is one of the most distinctive in northern Europe — a tradition shaped by the country's extraordinary natural landscape of forests, lakes, and coastline, its position at the cultural crossroads between Scandinavia and Russia, and centuries of Swedish and Russian political dominance that left culinary traces without fully displacing the indigenous Finnish food culture. Finland was part of the Swedish kingdom for 600 years and a Russian Grand Duchy for over a century, and both relationships left lasting marks: Swedish-influenced dishes in coastal and southwestern Finland, Russian baking and pastry traditions in the east and in Karelian cuisine, and a distinctly Finnish forest and lake tradition running through everything.

The indigenous Finnish culinary tradition is rooted in the forest and the lake — wild mushrooms, berries, freshwater fish, game, and rye grown in the short but intense Nordic summer. The foraging tradition remains one of the most actively practised in Europe: virtually every Finnish family spends time in the forest in late summer gathering chanterelles, ceps, lingonberries, blueberries, and cloudberries that are then preserved for the long winter. Finnish cooking absorbed significant influences from Russian and Karelian neighbours — the piirakka pastry tradition, the use of buckwheat, and the sour dairy fermentation practices all show eastern influence. Swedish culinary tradition shaped the coastal and archipelago cooking, with its emphasis on Baltic herring, cream, and Jansson's temptation.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Finnish cooking is unpretentious, honest, and deeply seasonal — a cuisine that celebrates the short summer with intense freshness and makes the long winter bearable through preserved, smoked, and fermented foods of considerable quality. Rye bread — ruisleipä — is the cornerstone of the Finnish table, a dense, slightly sour sourdough loaf made from the rye that thrives in the Finnish climate where wheat struggles. It is eaten at every meal and is the food Finns most miss when abroad. The quality of Finnish butter and dairy products is exceptional, and they are used without restraint.

Lohikeitto — the salmon and potato cream soup with dill — is the most beloved everyday Finnish dish: simple, warming, and dependent entirely on the quality of the salmon and the freshness of the dill. Karjalanpaisti — the Karelian hot pot of beef, pork, and lamb slow-cooked in the oven for hours — embodies the Finnish cooking philosophy of long, patient heat transforming good ingredients. The Midsummer celebration by the lake — sauna, grilled sausage, new potatoes, herring, and beer or lonkero — is perhaps the most complete expression of Finnish food culture. Salmiakki, the salty liquorice that challenges every non-Finn, is the taste that most defines Finland to outsiders.

03

At the Table

  • The Finnish meal structure is practical and unhurried — lunch is the main cooked meal of the day, often a simple soup or casserole. Dinner is lighter, and the elaborate weekend lunch is the week's social centrepiece.
  • Rye bread — ruisleipä — is eaten at every meal and with deep cultural significance. Finland's dark, dense, slightly sour sourdough rye is one of the finest breads in the world and the food Finns miss most when abroad.
  • The sauna and food are inseparable in Finnish culture — the traditional midsummer sauna is followed by grilled sausages, new potatoes, salted herring, and cold beer by the lake. This is not a recipe; it is a ritual.
  • Coffee is Finland's national obsession — Finland has the highest per-capita coffee consumption in the world. Coffee is drunk at every social occasion, and offering coffee to a guest is the first act of Finnish hospitality.
  • Wild foods are taken seriously — mushroom and berry picking in the forests is a national autumn ritual. Chanterelles, ceps, lingonberries, and cloudberries are gathered by virtually every Finnish family and preserved for winter.
  • Finnish silence at the table is not rudeness but respect — Finns eat with focus and appreciation, and lengthy conversation during a meal is less expected than in more extrovert food cultures.

Did You Know?

  • Finland has the highest per-capita coffee consumption in the world, drinking approximately 12 kilograms per person per year — nearly double the consumption of Italy or France. Finnish coffee is typically light-roasted and filter-brewed, drunk in large quantities throughout the day rather than in small ceremonial shots.
  • Salmiakki — salty liquorice flavoured with ammonium chloride — is Finland's most distinctive and internationally baffling confectionery. The intense, mouth-puckering combination of salt, bitter liquorice, and sharp chemical tang is deeply beloved by Finns and almost universally rejected by non-Scandinavians on first encounter.
  • Finland has 188,000 lakes and the world's largest archipelago by number of islands — over 40,000 islands in the southwestern Turku archipelago alone. This extraordinary water geography means freshwater fish — pike, perch, zander, and vendace — are as central to Finnish cooking as saltwater fish.
  • Karelian pasties — karjalanpiirakka — are small oval rye pastry cases filled with rice porridge, originating from the Karelia region now mostly within Russia. After the Winter War of 1939–40, over 400,000 Karelian Finns were evacuated to Finland proper, bringing their food traditions with them; the pasties are now protected by EU geographical indication.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Beef, pork, and lamb slow-cooked together in the oven for hours until meltingly tender.
  2. A warming salmon and potato soup finished with cream and dill.
  3. A hearty split pea and ham soup, traditionally eaten on Thursdays.
  4. Fish and pork baked inside a dense rye bread crust, a Savonian specialty.
  5. Thinly sliced sautéed reindeer served over mashed potato with lingonberry, a Lapland classic.

More Mains

  1. Simple grilled sausage, the centrepiece of every Finnish summer cookout.
  2. Baltic herring fillets sandwiched and pan-fried into crisp patties.
  3. A creamy potato and anchovy casserole of Swedish origin, popular at Christmas.
  4. Tiny lake fish, lightly floured and fried whole, eaten crisp with rye bread.
  5. A baked casserole of liver, rice, and raisins, traditionally served with lingonberry jam.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Pastries & Sweets

  1. Oval rye pastry cases filled with rice porridge, eaten with egg butter.
  2. A simple, lattice-topped blueberry pie made with foraged summer berries.
  3. Finland's cardamom-and-cinnamon spiced bun, a fixture of every coffee break.
  4. A small almond and rum-soaked cake topped with raspberry jam, eaten around Runeberg Day.

More Sweets

  1. A cardamom bun split and filled with almond paste and whipped cream, eaten before Lent.
  2. A dark, malty rye pudding traditionally eaten cold with cream and sugar at Easter.
  3. A thick, custardy baked pancake, often served with berries or jam.
  4. A lightly fermented, honey-sweetened lemon drink brewed for May Day.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Classics

  1. The classic rye rice pasty topped with a spread of chopped hard-boiled egg and butter.
  2. Finland's beloved and internationally baffling ammonium-chloride liquorice candy.
  3. Makkara grilled on a stick over an open fire, a summer cottage ritual.
  4. A slice of dense sour rye bread with a generous layer of butter, eaten at any meal.

More Snacks

  1. Small fried lake fish eaten crisp as a snack, especially at markets and festivals.
  2. A lighter snack-sized portion of the classic split pea and ham soup.
  3. Leftover karjalanpaisti reheated and eaten as a quick, hearty snack.
  4. Small squares of dense rye bread, often topped with butter or cheese.
