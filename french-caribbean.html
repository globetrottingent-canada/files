Cuisine Guide

The French Caribbean

Martinique, Guadeloupe, and Haiti — rhum agricole, Creole cooking, and a fusion of French technique with West African roots.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

The French Caribbean encompasses one of the most sophisticated Creole food cultures in the world — a tradition forged in the crucible of French colonialism, West African enslaved people's culinary knowledge, Indian indentured labour, and the extraordinary natural abundance of the Lesser Antilles. Martinique, Guadeloupe, and their smaller island neighbours are French overseas departments — constitutionally part of France and the EU — while Haiti is an independent republic whose history is unlike any other in the hemisphere. What unites them culinarily is the Creole tradition: a synthesis of French technique, African ingredients and cooking knowledge, and Caribbean produce that produces a cuisine of genuine sophistication and considerable depth.

The French colonial period from the 17th century established plantation economies based on sugar and rum, and the West African enslaved people brought to work them carried with them the culinary knowledge that defines Creole cooking today: the use of salt cod, okra, breadfruit, plantain, and the aromatic herb bundles (bois d'inde, thyme, chive) that season every pot. French culinary technique — the court-bouillon, the braising method, the emphasis on sauce — overlaid this African foundation, producing dishes that are recognisably French in structure and West African in ingredient and spirit. Indian indentured workers arriving from the 1850s added the colombo curry tradition. Haiti's history — the only successful slave revolution in history, independence in 1804, and two centuries of political and economic hardship — produced a food culture of great resilience built on the same West African Creole foundations but with its own distinct character.

02

Character of the Cuisine

French Antillean cooking is vibrant, aromatic, and built on a foundation of fresh seafood, tropical vegetables, and rhum agricole — the grassy, complex cane-juice spirit that is to Martinique and Guadeloupe what wine is to Burgundy. The ti' punch — white agricole, a squeeze of lime, a drop of cane syrup — is the ritual drink of the islands, made and drunk with the same seriousness that a French person brings to a glass of Burgundy. Accras de morue, the salt cod fritters eaten with every meal and gathering, are the flavour that says French Antilles more than any other: crispy, salty, herbal, eaten with a squeeze of lime and a glass of ti' punch.

Colombo — the curry of the Antilles, made with a blend of coriander, cumin, turmeric, and mustard adapted from the Indian indentured workers' tradition — is the most distinctive main dish of the islands, made with pork, chicken, goat, or seafood. Boudin créole, the blood sausage spiced with scotch bonnet and local herbs, is entirely its own thing — richer and more aromatic than any European boudin. Haiti's kitchen is simpler and more direct: griot (marinated and fried pork), diri ak pwa (rice and beans), and legim (the thick vegetable stew) are the daily foundation, and the djon djon mushroom found nowhere else on earth gives Haitian rice its distinctive black colour and deep, earthy flavour.

03

At the Table

  • The ti' punch is not a cocktail — it is a ritual. White rhum agricole, a squeeze of lime, and a drop of cane syrup, stirred once, drunk slowly. In Martinique and Guadeloupe, making your own ti' punch is considered basic adult competence.
  • Meals begin with accras — salt cod fritters, crispy outside and soft within, eaten with a squeeze of lime and a glass of ti' punch while standing in the kitchen. No Antillean gathering begins without them.
  • The Creole table is communal and abundant — dishes placed in the centre, eaten with rice, breadfruit, or plantain. The host's measure of success is that the table never empties.
  • Colombo — the curry of the French Antilles — is not Indian curry despite using similar spices. It was developed by Indian indentured workers and adapted to local ingredients: colombo powder with local meat and manioc. It is now distinctly Antillean.
  • Sunday is the day of the great family lunch — grillades on the beach, bokit sandwiches, and the whole extended family gathered. The midday meal can last from noon until evening.
  • Haiti has a distinct and proud food identity despite great hardship — griot, bannann peze (fried plantain), and rice and beans are the national table, carrying the history of the first successful slave revolution in the world.

Did You Know?

  • Rhum agricole — made from fresh sugarcane juice rather than molasses — was developed in Martinique in the 19th century when the island's sugar industry collapsed and distillers switched to using fresh juice instead of the molasses by-product of refined sugar. Martinique's rhum agricole was the first rum to receive an Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée, in 1996 — the only rum in the world with this French wine-equivalent designation.
  • Haiti was the first country in the world established by a successful slave revolt — the Haitian Revolution of 1791–1804 overthrew French colonial rule and created the first Black republic. The food culture that survived this history is a direct continuation of West African culinary traditions, expressed through dishes like griot, legim, and diri ak djon djon.
  • The colombo curry spice blend used across the French Antilles takes its name not from the South American country but from a Tamil word — 'kari' — brought by the Indian indentured workers who arrived in Martinique and Guadeloupe from the 1850s onwards. Their culinary influence — colombo curry, roti, and the use of tamarind — is a layer of French Caribbean food often overlooked.
  • Guadeloupe and Martinique are not Caribbean countries in the political sense — they are French overseas departments, meaning they are constitutionally part of France and the European Union. A croissant and a café au lait in a Pointe-à-Pitre boulangerie is as French as one in Paris, while an accras de morue at the same counter is purely Antillean.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Antilles Classics

  1. Crispy salt cod fritters, herbal and salty, eaten before every meal and gathering.
  2. Pork simmered in colombo powder — coriander, cumin, turmeric, and mustard — with manioc.
  3. Haitian marinated and fried pork, crisp outside and tender within, a national dish.
  4. Blood sausage spiced with scotch bonnet and local herbs, richer than European boudin.
  5. Fish gently poached in a tomato and herb broth, a classic French-technique Creole preparation.

Haiti & More

  1. A thick okra and leafy greens stew, often with crab or salted meat.
  2. Small dumplings simmered with shrimp in a Creole sauce.
  3. A spiced rice pilaf cooked with crab, a Guadeloupean specialty.
  4. Haitian rice cooked with djon djon mushroom, giving it a distinctive black colour and earthy flavour.
  5. Conch simmered in a spiced Creole sauce, a prized island delicacy.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Coconut & Sweets

  1. A silky set coconut milk pudding, a classic Creole dessert.
  2. Small tarts filled with coconut custard, a specialty of Les Saintes.
  3. Sweet fried banana doughnuts, a common street dessert.
  4. A dense, sweet spiced bread often flavoured with coconut and cinnamon.

More Sweets

  1. A chewy coconut and condensed milk sweet bar.
  2. A dense, sugary coconut candy bar, a favourite childhood treat.
  3. A dense, spiced sweet potato cake flavoured with vanilla and rum.
  4. A rich, creamy coconut sorbet, a refreshing tropical dessert.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Classics

  1. Crispy fritters, most often salt cod, the essential Antillean starter.
  2. A fried bread pocket stuffed with meat, fish, or cheese, a Guadeloupean street classic.
  3. A spicy mash of avocado, salt cod, cassava flour, and scotch bonnet.
  4. A chilled salad of shredded christophine (chayote), lime, and herbs.

More Snacks

  1. The ritual drink of the islands: white rhum agricole, lime, and cane syrup, stirred once.
  2. A fruity rum punch blending rhum agricole with tropical fruit juices and spices.
  3. Fish poached in a spiced, limey court-bouillon, light and aromatic.
  4. Octopus stewed until tender in a Creole tomato and herb sauce.
