Cuisine Guide

Ghana

Fufu and shito, palm oil and groundnut soup — a West African cuisine of communal, slow-cooked abundance.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Ghanaian cuisine is deeply rooted in the agricultural and cultural traditions of the Akan, Ga, Ewe, Dagomba, and other peoples of the West African forest and savannah zones. The food reflects the land: palm oil and palm nuts from the forest belt, yam and cassava from the fertile middle belt, sorghum and millet from the drier north, and an extraordinary abundance of fish from the Atlantic coast and Volta River. Starchy staples — fufu, banku, kenkey, omo tuo — are the foundations, each with its own preparation method, regional identity, and cultural significance.

Arab traders across the Sahara brought spices and dried goods from the north for centuries before European contact. Portuguese traders arrived on the Gold Coast in 1471, followed by Dutch, British, Danish, and Swedish — each leaving culinary traces, most notably in the introduction of American crops: tomatoes, cassava, groundnuts, peppers, and maize, all of which are now so thoroughly embedded in Ghanaian cooking that they feel ancient. The transatlantic slave trade, in which the Gold Coast was a major departure point, created profound culinary connections between West Africa and the Americas — soul food, Brazilian vatapá, and many Caribbean dishes trace their DNA directly to cooking traditions from what is now Ghana.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Ghanaian cooking is hearty, deeply savory, and built around the combination of a starchy base with a boldly flavored soup or stew. The soups — groundnut, palm nut, light soup — are not thin broths but rich, complex, slow-cooked preparations that take hours and require skilled hands. Fufu, the most iconic Ghanaian starch, is made by pounding boiled cassava and plantain together until smooth and elastic, then rolled into balls and swallowed — not chewed — with soup. Each ethnic group has its own preferred starch and preparation.

Heat comes from scotch bonnet peppers used without restraint. Palm oil gives many dishes their characteristic orange-red color and deep, slightly funky richness that is the flavor signature of West African cooking. Dried and smoked fish add umami depth to almost every stew and soup. Shito — the black pepper sauce of fermented dried shrimp, herrings, and chili — is Ghana's essential condiment, found on every table. The food is communal, abundant, and eaten with pride.

03

At the Table

  • Meals are eaten communally from shared bowls — the eldest person at the table eats first. Food is traditionally eaten with the right hand, rolling rice and stew together into a ball before eating.
  • Fufu is not merely food but a ritual — pounded in a wooden mortar together, the rhythmic alternation of pounding and turning requires trust and coordination between two people.
  • Hospitality is sacred — a guest must be offered food regardless of the time of day or what is available. Refusing offered food is considered disrespectful in many communities.
  • Sunday is chop day — the most elaborate meal of the week, typically featuring a large pot of groundnut soup or palm nut soup with fufu, prepared over several hours.
  • Kenkey and fish is the iconic everyday Ga meal of Accra — fermented corn dumplings with fried or grilled fish and pepper sauce, eaten at roadside chop bars throughout the city.
  • Palm wine is tapped fresh from the tree and consumed the same day — it ferments within hours and becomes more alcoholic and sour as the day progresses.

Did You Know?

  • Ghana is the world's second-largest producer of cocoa, after Côte d'Ivoire, and supplies roughly 20% of global cocoa. Ghanaian cocoa is considered among the finest quality in the world — yet historically most of the chocolate made from it was consumed in Europe, not Ghana itself.
  • Groundnut soup — a rich peanut-based stew — reflects the West African origin of peanut cultivation and cooking traditions that travelled to the Americas via the transatlantic slave trade. Many Southern American dishes using peanuts trace their lineage directly to West African cooking.
  • Shito — Ghana's fiercely pungent black pepper sauce made from dried shrimp, herrings, and chili — is arguably the most distinctive condiment in West African cooking. A Ghanaian meal without shito is considered incomplete by many.
  • Ghana's kelewele — spiced fried plantain — is one of the most beloved street foods on the continent. The use of spices like ginger, cayenne, and cloves to season fried ripe plantain is a Ghanaian technique that distinguishes it from the plain fried plantain common elsewhere in West Africa.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Pounded cassava and plantain served with a rich, slow-cooked peanut-based soup.
  2. Rice cooked in a rich tomato and pepper sauce, West Africa's most celebrated rice dish.
  3. Fermented corn and cassava dough served with grilled or fried tilapia and pepper sauce.
  4. Fermented corn dumplings served with fried fish and pepper sauce, an iconic Ga meal.
  5. A rich, deep-red soup made from palm fruit, served with pounded fufu.

More Mains

  1. Rice and beans cooked together with dried millet leaves, giving it a reddish-brown colour.
  2. Soft pounded rice balls, typically eaten with soup in place of fufu.
  3. Black-eyed peas stewed in palm oil and tomato, often served with fried plantain.
  4. A lighter, tomato-based broth soup served with pounded fufu.
  5. Cocoyam leaves stewed with palm oil, fish, and egg, similar to a West African spinach stew.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Sweet Snacks

  1. Ripe plantain fried with ginger, cayenne, and cloves, one of Ghana's most beloved street foods.
  2. Soft, sweet fried dough balls, similar to a doughnut, popular for breakfast.
  3. Small crunchy fried dough crackers, lightly sweetened.
  4. A dense, chewy coconut and sugar candy, sold in small blocks.

More Sweets

  1. Small sweet balls made from coconut and sugar, a simple homemade treat.
  2. Sweet, spiced pancakes made from mashed overripe plantain.
  3. Crisp fried yam served with Ghana's fiery black pepper sauce.
  4. A smooth porridge made from roasted, milled maize, often eaten for breakfast.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. Ripe plantain fried with ginger, cayenne, and cloves, sold across the country.
  2. Fritters made from ground black-eyed peas, a popular breakfast street food.
  3. Soft, sweet fried dough balls, popular alongside tea or coffee.
  4. Roasted corn on the cob paired with fresh coconut, a classic roadside snack.

Snacks & Sides

  1. Ghana's fiery black pepper sauce spread simply on bread, a quick snack.
  2. Thin slices of plantain fried until crisp, a common packaged snack.
  3. Grated plantain fritters spiced with ginger and pepper, similar to kelewele in fritter form.
  4. A dense peanut and sugar bar, similar to peanut brittle.
