Greece

Olive oil, feta, and mezedes — one of the oldest continuously practiced food cultures in the West.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Greek cuisine is among the oldest continuously practiced food cultures in the Western world — a tradition stretching back over 3,000 years, deeply rooted in the Mediterranean triad of olive oil, wheat, and wine. Ancient Greek texts contain some of the earliest recorded recipes in history, and the philosophical relationship between food, health, and virtue was already being debated by Hippocrates and Plato in the 5th century BCE. The Greek approach to eating — moderate, seasonal, plant-forward, and social — is what modern nutrition science now recognizes as the Mediterranean diet, one of the most studied and validated dietary patterns in the world.

Byzantine civilization preserved and elaborated Greek culinary traditions for over a thousand years, adding Persian spice influences, refined confectionery, and the tradition of elaborate banqueting. The four centuries of Ottoman rule left lasting culinary traces — moussaka, baklava, tzatziki, and many mezedes dishes reflect the shared Ottoman-Greek food culture of the eastern Mediterranean. Greek communities across the diaspora in Egypt, Turkey, and the Americas maintained their food traditions, and the mid-20th century return and consolidation of Greek identity brought many regional traditions into the national cuisine.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Greek cooking is defined by the quality of its raw ingredients — the finest olive oil in the world, sheep's milk feta of protected designation, wild herbs from mountain slopes, and seafood from the Aegean and Ionian seas. The cuisine uses these ingredients with confidence and restraint: a drizzle of good olive oil, a squeeze of lemon, dried oregano, and salt can elevate the simplest ingredients to something magnificent. The Mediterranean diet principle of letting quality produce speak is nowhere more naturally expressed than in Greek cooking.

Mezedes — the culture of small shared dishes — is the social heart of Greek eating: tzatziki, taramasalata, grilled octopus, saganaki, and dolmades arriving in succession with bread and wine before anything substantial. The island tradition of fresh grilled fish, the Athenian taverna's moussaka and pastitsio, and the mountain tradition of kleftiko slow-roasted lamb are all expressions of the same philosophy: patience, quality ingredients, and the pleasure of eating together. Spices are used with subtlety — cinnamon and allspice in meat dishes, honey in pastries, and the anise-forward bitterness of ouzo setting the tone for any serious meal.

03

At the Table

  • Greek meals are slow and social — dining is not an act of refueling but a ritual of conversation, hospitality, and pleasure. A table set without wine and mezedes is considered inhospitable.
  • Mezedes culture defines Greek eating — small shared plates of olives, dips, cheese, and fried dishes arrive before the main course and can expand into a full meal in themselves.
  • The Greek table never empties — hosts refill plates without asking, insisting guests eat more. Refusing food is gently but persistently ignored. Leaving a table hungry is a host's failure.
  • Name day celebrations are often more important than birthdays — the person whose saint's name day it is hosts, providing food and drink for all guests who come unannounced.
  • Easter is the most important food occasion in the Greek year — the midnight Resurrection service is followed by a meal of magiritsa soup, and Easter Sunday brings whole lamb roasted on a spit.
  • Coffee culture is serious — Greek coffee is brewed in a small pot, served in a demitasse with the grounds in the bottom, and drunk slowly. Reading the grounds afterward is a tradition.

Did You Know?

  • Greece is the world's third-largest producer of olive oil and the largest per-capita consumer — Greeks consume roughly 20 liters of olive oil per person per year, nearly three times the average of the next highest European consumers. Greek olive cultivation goes back at least 5,000 years.
  • Feta cheese has Protected Designation of Origin status under European Union law — only cheese made in specific regions of Greece from sheep's milk (or a mixture of up to 30% goat's milk) may legally be called feta. Denmark and other countries fought this designation for years before the EU ruled definitively in Greece's favor in 2002.
  • The word 'symposium' — now meaning an academic conference — originally referred to a Greek drinking party at which wine was diluted with water and philosophical discussion took place. The Greeks considered drinking undiluted wine barbaric; the proper ratio was typically three parts water to two parts wine.
  • Honey from Mount Hymettus near Athens has been considered the finest in the world since antiquity — Athenian honey was exported across the Mediterranean and praised by ancient writers. Greek thyme honey remains among the world's most prized, with bees collecting nectar from wild thyme, herbs, and wildflowers across the rocky hillsides.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Layers of eggplant, spiced minced meat, and béchamel, baked until golden.
  2. Skewered, grilled meat, often served wrapped in pita with tzatziki.
  3. Flaky phyllo pastry filled with spinach and feta.
  4. Baked pasta layered with spiced minced meat and béchamel, Greece's answer to lasagna.
  5. Tender octopus grilled over charcoal and dressed simply with olive oil and lemon.

More Mains

  1. Lamb slow-roasted for hours, traditionally in a sealed clay oven, until falling apart.
  2. A rich, slow-braised beef stew with sweet baby onions, cinnamon, and red wine.
  3. Tomatoes or peppers stuffed with rice and herbs, baked until tender.
  4. A hearty white bean soup often considered Greece's national dish of the poor table.
  5. Giant butter beans baked in a rich tomato sauce with herbs.

Puddings & Sweets

Pastries & Sweets

  1. Layers of phyllo filled with chopped nuts and soaked in honey syrup.
  2. Small fried dough puffs drenched in honey syrup and cinnamon.
  3. A creamy semolina custard baked between layers of phyllo, soaked in syrup.
  4. A moist semolina cake soaked in citrus syrup.

More Sweets

  1. Buttery almond shortbread cookies dusted with powdered sugar, a Christmas classic.
  2. Spiced honey cookies soaked in syrup and topped with walnuts, made at Christmas.
  3. A creamy cinnamon-dusted rice pudding, a comforting everyday dessert.
  4. Shredded phyllo-like pastry wrapped around nuts and soaked in syrup.

Snacks & Street Food

Mezedes Classics

  1. A cooling dip of strained yogurt, cucumber, garlic, and olive oil.
  2. A pale pink dip made from cured fish roe blended with bread or potato.
  3. Bite-sized triangles of flaky phyllo filled with spinach and feta.
  4. Flaky phyllo pastry filled with a rich mix of Greek cheeses.

More Mezedes

  1. Vine leaves stuffed with rice and herbs, served warm or cold.
  2. A slab of cheese fried until crisp outside and molten within.
  3. Tomato, cucumber, onion, olives, and a thick slab of feta, dressed in olive oil.
  4. A pungent garlic and potato dip, often served with fried fish or vegetables.
