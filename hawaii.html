Cuisine Guide

Hawaii

Poke, luau, plate lunch, and plantation food culture — a 1,500-year layering of Polynesian, Native Hawaiian, and immigrant plantation traditions into something entirely its own.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Hawaiian cuisine is one of the most genuinely multicultural food cultures in the world — a tradition built in layers over roughly 1,500 years, beginning with the Polynesian voyagers who settled the Hawaiian islands around 400–600 CE and brought with them the canoe plants: taro, breadfruit, banana, coconut, sweet potato, sugarcane, and the pig. These foundational ingredients sustained a sophisticated pre-contact Hawaiian civilization whose food culture centred on taro and poi, fish from the sea and fishponds, and the imu underground oven for communal feasting. Native Hawaiian food knowledge — the ahupuaa land management system, the cultivation of over 300 varieties of taro, and deep understanding of Pacific Ocean fish — was the foundation on which everything else was built.

The plantation era from the 1850s to the early 20th century transformed Hawaiian food culture irreversibly. Sugar and pineapple plantation owners recruited labour from China, Japan, Korea, the Philippines, and Portugal, and the workers brought their food traditions with them. Eating together across ethnic lines in the plantation camps led to the blending of food cultures that produced the modern Hawaiian plate lunch — the daily meal combining Japanese rice, Filipino and Portuguese proteins, and communal Hawaiian eating spirit. American annexation in 1898 and statehood in 1959 brought the American diner, fast food, and Spam. World War II's military presence embedded canned meat so deeply that Hawaii now consumes more Spam per capita than any other US state.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Hawaiian food is defined by aloha — generosity, inclusion, and the pleasure of sharing. The luau feast, centred on kalua pig slow-cooked in the imu, is the highest expression of Hawaiian hospitality: the preparation is communal work, the eating is communal pleasure. Poi — the pounded taro paste that is simultaneously the everyday staple and a sacred food — is eaten with the fingers and shared from a common bowl. Poke, the seasoned raw fish that has conquered the world, is in its traditional Hawaiian form a simple thing: cubed ahi or octopus dressed with sea salt, seaweed, and roasted kukui nut.

The plate lunch is the democracy of Hawaiian food — two scoops rice, macaroni salad, protein — available from lunch wagons and diners across every island for a few dollars. It is not high cuisine but it is deeply Hawaiian: a meal that could only exist where Japanese, Filipino, Korean, and Hawaiian food traditions merged in a specific historical moment. Shave ice, malasadas, and spam musubi are similarly products of their specific cultural history. The islands' extraordinary produce — Maui onions, Kona coffee, macadamia nuts, passion fruit, and pineapple — gives Hawaiian food a tropical richness found nowhere on the mainland.

03

At the Table

  • The plate lunch is Hawaii's democratic daily meal — two scoops of white rice, macaroni salad, and a protein (katsu, teriyaki chicken, kalua pork, or loco moco) served from a lunch wagon, combining Japanese, Korean, Filipino, and Hawaiian food traditions.
  • The luau is not a tourist show but a genuine Hawaiian feast tradition — a celebration centred on the imu underground oven in which a whole pig and other foods are slow-cooked on hot lava rocks wrapped in ti and banana leaves for hours.
  • Poke — cubed raw fish seasoned with soy sauce, sesame oil, green onion, and inamona roasted kukui nut — is ancient Hawaiian food that became a global food trend. The original is ahi tuna or octopus dressed simply.
  • Spam is eaten without irony in Hawaii — the islands consume more Spam per capita than any US state, a legacy of World War II military rationing. Spam musubi, Spam on rice wrapped in nori, is the quintessential convenience snack.
  • Shave ice is not a snow cone — it is ice shaved to a feather-light texture and drenched in flavoured syrups, often with azuki beans and ice cream at the bottom, a technique brought by Japanese plantation workers.
  • Hawaiian food is deeply multicultural by necessity — the plantation era brought Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Filipino, Portuguese, and Puerto Rican workers whose food traditions merged with Native Hawaiian cooking.

Did You Know?

  • Poke has become one of the most successful global food export stories of the 21st century — the poke bowl spread from Hawaiian food trucks to global restaurant chains with extraordinary speed from around 2012. By 2019 there were an estimated 700 poke restaurants in New York City alone. The word poke simply means 'to slice or cut crosswise' in Hawaiian.
  • The Hawaiian plate lunch emerged directly from the plantation era, when workers from Japan, China, Korea, the Philippines, and Portugal brought their own lunches to the fields. The communal sharing of different workers' lunches over time produced the eclectic combination that defines the plate lunch today.
  • Poi — made by pounding cooked taro root into a smooth, slightly fermented paste — has been the dietary foundation of Native Hawaiian culture for over a thousand years. It is classified by consistency: one-finger, two-finger, or three-finger poi depending on thickness. Taro is sacred in Hawaiian culture, considered the elder sibling of the Hawaiian people in creation mythology.
  • The Portuguese malasada — a deep-fried yeasted dough ball rolled in sugar — became a Hawaiian institution through Portuguese workers who arrived on sugar plantations in the 1870s. Leonard's Bakery in Honolulu, opened in 1952, made malasadas famous and still has a line outside the door every day.
Food & Drink Reference

The dishes, sweets, and drinks that define the everyday table.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Native & Local

  1. Whole pig slow-cooked underground in the imu on hot lava rocks wrapped in ti and banana leaves, the centrepiece of any luau.
  2. White rice topped with a hamburger patty, a fried egg, and brown gravy — Hawaii's iconic diner plate.
  3. Two scoops rice, macaroni salad, and a protein such as katsu or teriyaki chicken, Hawaii's everyday democratic meal.
  4. Shell-on shrimp sautéed in garlic and butter, served plate-lunch style from North Shore Oahu food trucks.
  5. Chicken grilled over an open flame and basted with a sweet soy-based glaze, turned ('huli') repeatedly on a rotisserie.

Plate Lunch Classics

  1. A local noodle soup in a light dashi-based broth, topped with green onion, kamaboko, and egg — a plantation-era fusion dish.
  2. A hearty beef and vegetable stew served over rice, a plate lunch counter staple.
  3. Breaded, deep-fried chicken cutlet sliced and served with tonkatsu sauce over rice.
  4. Squid stewed with taro leaves and coconut milk, a local variation on the traditional laulau family of dishes.
  5. Salted, dried, and often smoked beef strips, a Hawaiian take on jerky traditionally fried before serving.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Ice shaved to an almost powdery texture and drenched in flavoured syrups, often with azuki beans at the bottom.
  2. A firm, milky coconut pudding cut into squares, a staple dessert at luaus.
  3. A deep-fried, sugar-rolled Portuguese dough ball, a Hawaiian institution since the 1870s plantation era.
  4. A cake baked upside down with caramelized pineapple rings and cherries on top.

More Sweets

  1. Sweet rice cake made with poi, fried until crisp outside and chewy within.
  2. A dense, caramel-like steamed pudding made from grated taro and coconut.
  3. Ice cream wrapped in a thin layer of sweet, chewy mochi rice dough.
  4. A dense, chewy baked rice-flour cake made rich with coconut milk and butter.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Classics

  1. A block of grilled Spam over rice, wrapped together with a strip of nori — Hawaii's quintessential convenience snack.
  2. Cubed raw ahi or octopus dressed with soy sauce, sesame oil, and roasted kukui nut.
  3. A steamed or baked bun filled with sweet char siu pork, adapted from Chinese plantation workers' cha siu bao.
  4. A broader category of rice snacks wrapped in nori, of which Spam musubi is the best-known variety.

More Snacks

  1. Chinese-style preserved fruit, sweet, salty, and sour, sold from candy counters across the islands.
  2. A rich, mayonnaise-heavy pasta salad, the standard accompaniment to any plate lunch.
  3. A cold salad of diced salted salmon, tomato, and onion, a luau side dish with Western influence.
  4. A light, gingery soup of chicken and glass noodles, a Chinese-influenced luau staple.
