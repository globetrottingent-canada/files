Cuisine Guide

Iceland

Lamb, cold-water fish, and skyr — a North Atlantic larder of extraordinary quality shaped by preservation and isolation.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Icelandic cuisine is one of the most geographically determined food cultures in the world — shaped almost entirely by what a remote North Atlantic island, settled by Norse Vikings in the 9th century, could produce, catch, and preserve through long winters of near-total darkness. The island's volcanic soil, glacial rivers, unpolluted coastal waters, and extensive highland pastures define a larder of extraordinary quality but limited variety: world-class lamb, pristine cold-water fish, Arctic char from glacial rivers, langoustine from the sea floor, wild berries in late summer, and the dairy tradition that produced skyr. For over a thousand years this was essentially the entire Icelandic diet, supplemented by preserved and fermented foods developed to survive the winter.

The Norse settlers brought with them the Scandinavian tradition of preservation — smoking, salting, drying, and fermentation — and Iceland's isolation meant these techniques remained central to the food culture long after refrigeration made them redundant elsewhere. Dried salt cod, smoked lamb, fermented shark, and rúgbrauð — the dense rye bread slow-baked underground in geothermal heat — are all survivals of this preservation culture. Danish colonial rule from 1380 to 1944 brought some continental European influence to elite cooking, but the peasant food of Iceland remained largely unchanged. The great transformation came with the New Nordic cuisine movement of the 2000s, which reframed Iceland's austere larder as a virtue and placed Reykjavík restaurants on the international map.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Icelandic cooking is defined by the exceptional quality of its primary ingredients and a philosophy of minimal interference. The lamb — raised on highland pastures grazed with wild herbs including thyme, sorrel, and angelica — is considered among the finest in the world, with a distinctive herbal flavour that needs nothing added to it. The seafood — cod, haddock, langoustine, and Arctic char from rivers so clean they are drinkable — is simply prepared: grilled, poached, or smoked, with butter, dill, and perhaps a squeeze of lemon. The New Nordic movement that emerged from Copenhagen's Noma and spread to Reykjavík has added foraging, fermentation, and hyperlocal creativity to this tradition, finding food value in sea purslane, birch leaves, crowberries, and the island's extraordinary volcanic mineral water.

Traditional Icelandic food has a more challenging dimension: the Þorrablót midwinter festival celebrates the old preservation foods — hákarl (fermented shark), svið (singed sheep's head), hrútspungar (pickled ram's testicles), and blood pudding — eaten with brennivín, the caraway-flavoured schnapps sometimes called Black Death. These foods are less a daily diet than a cultural ritual, connecting modern Icelanders to their Viking past. Skyr, the thick cultured dairy product made since the settlement of the island, is the great everyday food — eaten for breakfast with berries, used in cooking, and recently exported to the world as a fashionable high-protein dairy product.

03

At the Table

  • Fish and lamb are the twin pillars of the Icelandic table — the sea and the highland pasture define what is eaten, and both are of extraordinary quality, shaped by clean cold water and unpolluted grazing.
  • Skyr — the thick, high-protein cultured dairy product — is eaten at breakfast daily, often with berries and a drizzle of cream. It is technically a fresh cheese but eaten as a yogurt, and has been made in Iceland since the settlement of the island in the 9th century.
  • Coffee culture is serious and strong — Iceland consistently ranks among the world's highest per-capita coffee consumers. The kaffi break is a social institution, and Icelanders drink their coffee dark, strong, and frequently.
  • The traditional Þorrablót midwinter festival in January and February brings out the old preserved foods — hákarl, svið, hrútspungar, and blood sausage — eaten with brennivín. It is an act of cultural memory as much as appetite.
  • New Nordic cuisine has transformed Icelandic fine dining — Reykjavík restaurants now rank internationally, built on hyperlocal ingredients: wild crowberries, sea purslane, Arctic char, skyr, and the extraordinary quality of Icelandic lamb and seafood.
  • Alcohol was prohibited in Iceland until 1989 — beer was banned until March 1, 1989 (now celebrated as Beer Day), and the legacy is visible in the strong coffee culture and the social centrality of the State Alcohol and Tobacco Company Vínbúðin.

05

Did You Know?

  • Iceland has no McDonald's and has never had a permanent one — the last franchise closed in 2009 during the financial crisis and was never replaced. Iceland is one of very few countries in the world without a McDonald's presence, and Icelanders are generally proud of this fact.
  • Hákarl — fermented Greenlandic shark — is one of the most challenging traditional foods in the world. The shark contains high levels of trimethylamine oxide and urea that make it toxic when fresh. Traditional preparation involves burying the shark for several months to ferment and then hanging it to dry. Anthony Bourdain called it the single worst thing he ever ate.
  • Iceland is home to some of the world's finest wild Atlantic salmon and Arctic char, both of which thrive in the island's pristine glacial rivers. Icelandic fish — particularly cod, haddock, and langoustine — are considered among the finest in the world and are exported to the finest restaurants across Europe.
  • Skyr has been made in Iceland for over 1,000 years and is one of the few Viking-age foods still eaten in virtually unchanged form today. Its recent global popularity — marketed as a high-protein yogurt alternative — has made it Iceland's most successful food export.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. A comforting mash of white fish, potato, and béchamel, a homely everyday dish.
  2. A hearty lamb and root vegetable soup, Iceland's classic comfort food.
  3. Smoked, cured lamb, traditionally smoked over birch or dried sheep dung.
  4. Sweet, delicate langoustine simply grilled with butter.
  5. A rich, creamy langoustine bisque, an Icelandic seafood classic.

More Mains

  1. Arctic char from glacial rivers, simply grilled with butter and dill.
  2. Dried, salted cod, rehydrated and cooked, a preservation-era staple.
  3. Herb-grazed lamb chops, grilled simply to let the meat's flavour speak.
  4. Salmon marinated in tangy skyr before cooking, a modern Icelandic touch.
  5. Dense geothermal rye bread topped with cold-smoked salmon.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Thick cultured skyr topped with fresh or preserved wild berries.
  2. A twisted, deep-fried cardamom-scented pastry, similar to a doughnut.
  3. Thin, delicate pancakes rolled with sugar or jam and cream.
  4. A layered cardamom cake filled with prune jam, an Icelandic-Canadian heritage cake.

More Sweets

  1. A simple chocolate sheet cake topped with chocolate glaze and coconut.
  2. Rich, creamy Icelandic ice cream, eaten enthusiastically year-round.
  3. A rhubarb jam cake topped with an oat crumble, whose name means 'marital bliss'.
  4. A dense, buttery almond cake, often served with coffee.

Snacks & Street Food

Classics

  1. Wind-dried fish, eaten in strips with butter, a traditional protein snack.
  2. Thick cultured skyr eaten plain or with a touch of sugar, any time of day.
  3. Dense geothermally-baked rye bread with a thick layer of butter.
  4. A lamb-and-pork hot dog topped with crispy onions and remoulade, a beloved snack.

More Snacks

  1. A smaller, snack-portion version of Iceland's rich langoustine bisque.
  2. Cold-smoked lamb slices, eaten as a light snack on bread.
  3. Vanilla ice cream mixed with toasted rye bread crumbs, a beloved local treat.
  4. Thin, decoratively cut fried bread, traditionally made at Christmas.
