India

Spice mastery and regional diversity — less a single cuisine than a subcontinent of food traditions.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Indian cuisine is not a single cuisine but a continent of food traditions — a subcontinent of 1.4 billion people, 22 official languages, and extraordinary geographical variation from Himalayan highlands to tropical coastline, whose food reflects every dimension of that diversity. Its roots reach back 5,000 years to the Indus Valley Civilization, and the cuisine has been shaped by successive waves of influence: the Vedic traditions that established the spiritual relationship between food and the self; the Mughal empire that brought Persian cooking, rich meat dishes, and biryani to northern India; Arab traders who brought saffron and dried fruits; Portuguese colonizers who introduced chili, tomatoes, and vinegar to Goa; and the British Raj that both absorbed Indian spice culture and imposed its own institutional simplifications.

Regional variation is the defining truth of Indian food — the creamy, dairy-rich, wheat-based cooking of the Punjab has almost nothing in common with the sour, fermented, rice-based cooking of Tamil Nadu, the fish-and-mustard traditions of Bengal, the vinegar-marinated pork dishes of Goa, or the pure vegetarian Jain cooking of Gujarat. To speak of Indian food as a single entity is roughly as accurate as speaking of European food as one thing.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Indian cooking is defined by its extraordinary mastery of spice — not merely heat, but the layering of dozens of aromatic compounds to create complexity that unfolds through a meal. The tadka — the technique of blooming whole spices in hot ghee or oil before adding other ingredients — is the foundation of flavor in most Indian cooking. Ghee, the clarified butter revered in Ayurvedic tradition, carries flavors and adds a deep, nutty richness. Dal — lentils in all their forms — is the great democratic food of India, eaten daily from the richest households to the humblest.

The north favors bread — roti, naan, paratha — and rich, creamy sauces. The south favors rice, fermented batters, and sharp, tamarind-soured preparations. The east uses mustard oil and fish. The west ranges from the fiery vindaloos of Goa to the delicate, purely vegetarian thalis of Gujarat. Street food culture is extraordinary: chaat, vada pav, pani puri, and samosas are as technically specific and beloved as any restaurant dish. India's sweet tradition — mithai — is its own universe of milk-based, nut-studded, syrup-soaked confections.

03

At the Table

  • Indian meals are served all at once on a thali — a round tray with small bowls of dal, vegetables, chutneys, and rice or bread, allowing every flavor to be tasted together or separately.
  • Eating with the right hand is traditional across much of India — bread is torn and used to scoop, and the act of touching food with the hand is considered to connect the eater with what they consume.
  • Vegetarianism is far more widespread in India than anywhere else on earth — around 30-40% of the population eats no meat, driven by Hindu, Jain, and Buddhist traditions. Entire states have large vegetarian majorities.
  • Chai is not merely a drink but a social ritual — tea stalls exist on every corner, and pausing for a clay cup of milky spiced tea is a reason to stop, talk, and connect.
  • The concept of 'curry' as a single dish does not exist in India — it is a British colonial simplification. Indian cooking distinguishes between hundreds of distinct preparations: sabzi, dal, korma, rogan josh, biryani, sambar, rasam.
  • Diwali, Eid, Holi, and Pongal each bring specific festival foods — mithai sweets, biryani, gujiya, and pongal rice are as tied to their occasions as Christmas pudding is to December.

Did You Know?

  • India is the world's largest producer and consumer of spices — it grows over 70 different spice varieties and accounts for roughly 75% of global spice production. The spice trade was the primary motivation for European exploration of sea routes to Asia, making Indian spices arguably the catalyst for the Age of Discovery.
  • Chicken tikka masala — one of Britain's most popular dishes — was almost certainly created in Britain by South Asian immigrant cooks, not in India. It does not exist as a traditional dish in any Indian regional cuisine.
  • India has the world's largest vegetarian population by a significant margin — an estimated 350-400 million people who eat no meat. This has driven the development of one of the world's most sophisticated vegetarian culinary traditions, from the thalis of Gujarat to the dosas of Tamil Nadu.
  • Black pepper — the world's most traded spice — originates from the Kerala coast of southwest India, where it has been cultivated for over 3,000 years. Roman traders paid for it in gold; it was so valuable in medieval Europe that it was used as currency and given as gifts to royalty.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Fragrant rice layered and slow-cooked with spiced meat or vegetables.
  2. Chicken simmered in a rich, buttery tomato and cream sauce.
  3. Black lentils slow-cooked with butter and cream until velvety.
  4. A deep red, aromatic lamb curry from Kashmir, coloured with Kashmiri chili.
  5. A crisp fermented rice and lentil crepe filled with spiced potato.

More Mains

  1. Fresh cheese cubes simmered in a smooth spiced spinach purée.
  2. Spiced chickpea curry served with fried, fluffy leavened bread.
  3. A tangy, tamarind-soured lentil and vegetable stew from South India.
  4. A slow-cooked, sealed-pot biryani layered with marinated meat and fragrant rice.
  5. Fish simmered in a regional curry base, varying widely from Bengal to Goa.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Deep-fried milk dumplings soaked in rose-scented sugar syrup.
  2. A fragrant rice pudding simmered with milk, cardamom, and nuts.
  3. Crispy fried batter spirals soaked in saffron sugar syrup.
  4. Soft cheese dumplings soaked in sweetened, cardamom-scented milk.

More Sweets

  1. A dense, ghee-rich sweet pudding made from semolina, carrot, or lentils.
  2. Round sweets made from flour, ghee, and sugar, formed into balls.
  3. A dense, slow-frozen Indian ice cream, often pistachio or mango flavoured.
  4. Grated carrot slow-cooked in milk and ghee until dense and sweet.

Snacks & Street Food

Street Classics

  1. A crisp fried pastry triangle filled with spiced potato and peas.
  2. Vegetables dipped in spiced chickpea batter and deep-fried.
  3. Crisp hollow puris filled with spiced tamarind water and potato.
  4. A tangy, spiced mix of fried dough, potato, chutneys, and yogurt.

More Snacks

  1. A spiced fried potato patty served in a bread roll, Mumbai's iconic snack.
  2. A crisp-fried spiced potato patty, often topped with chutneys and yogurt.

  4. A spiced mashed vegetable curry served with buttered bread rolls.
