Indonesia

Bumbu spice pastes, sambal, and 17,000 islands — one of the most diverse and complex food cultures on earth.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Indonesian cuisine is one of the most diverse and complex food cultures on earth — a vast archipelago of over 17,000 islands spanning nearly 5,000 kilometres, home to over 300 ethnic groups each with their own cooking tradition, shaped by millennia of trade with India, China, Arabia, and later Portugal and the Netherlands. The spice islands of Maluku — the only source of nutmeg and cloves in the world for centuries — made the Indonesian archipelago the most commercially valuable territory on earth during the age of European exploration, and the Dutch colonial empire that resulted from the spice trade left deep culinary traces in a cuisine that was already extraordinarily rich.

Indian Hindu and Buddhist influence, arriving through maritime trade from at least the 1st century CE, introduced complex spice cooking, the use of coconut milk, and the ceremonial food traditions still visible in Balinese Hindu cuisine today. Chinese immigrants, present in coastal trading communities for over a thousand years, contributed noodle dishes, soy sauce, tofu, and stir-frying techniques that became thoroughly Indonesianized. Arab traders brought Islam from the 13th century onwards, and with it dietary restrictions that shaped the majority Muslim cuisine of Java, Sumatra, and Sulawesi — halal cooking now defines the mainstream while Bali's Hindu minority maintains its distinct pork-centred tradition. Dutch colonialism introduced bread, dairy, and the rijsttafel — a colonial dining format of dozens of small Indonesian dishes served simultaneously that has since become iconic.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Indonesian cooking is defined by the depth and variety of its spice pastes — bumbu — which form the aromatic foundation of virtually every dish. Unlike the curry powders of South Asian cooking, Indonesian bumbu are freshly ground from combinations of shallots, garlic, galangal, ginger, lemongrass, turmeric, candlenut, and chili, producing pastes of extraordinary fragrance that are fried in oil before other ingredients are added. Sambal, the chili-based condiment or sauce, exists in dozens of regional varieties — sambal oelek, sambal terasi, sambal matah — and is considered essential to the Indonesian table in a way that has no Western parallel.

Rendang from West Sumatra represents the philosophical peak of Indonesian cooking — beef slow-cooked with coconut milk and a complex spice paste for four to six hours until the liquid is entirely absorbed and the meat is dark, intensely flavoured, and shelf-stable without refrigeration. Nasi goreng, the fried rice made with kecap manis sweet soy sauce and whatever protein is available, is the universal everyday dish eaten from Jakarta street stalls to five-star hotel breakfasts. The nasi padang tradition of West Sumatra — dozens of small dishes placed before the diner, paying only for what is eaten — is one of the world's great democratic restaurant formats. Tempeh, the fermented soybean cake unique to Java, is one of Indonesia's most important gifts to world food.

03

At the Table

  • Rice is the centre of every Indonesian meal — nasi is eaten three times a day across most of the archipelago, and a meal without rice is not considered a proper meal, regardless of how much other food is present.
  • Eating is communal but often informal — dishes are placed in the centre and shared, with rice served individually. Warung street stalls and nasi padang restaurants where you eat whatever is in the glass cases are the daily eating culture.
  • Nasi padang restaurants serve Minangkabau food from West Sumatra — one of the world's great street dining experiences. Dozens of small dishes are placed on the table and you pay only for what you eat.
  • Sambal is not a condiment but a structural component — the chili-based paste or sauce appears in dozens of varieties across the archipelago and is considered essential to virtually every meal.
  • Indonesian hospitality means food is always offered — arriving at any Indonesian home at any time of day will result in being offered tea and something to eat. Refusing is mildly impolite.
  • Ramadan transforms food culture across Muslim-majority regions — the breaking of the fast with kolak, dates, and sweet drinks, followed by a full iftar meal, is a major social occasion across Java, Sumatra, and Sulawesi.

Did You Know?

  • Indonesia comprises over 17,000 islands and is home to over 300 distinct ethnic groups, each with its own culinary tradition. The diversity is so vast that it is more accurate to speak of Indonesian cuisines — Balinese Hindu cooking bears no resemblance to the fish-centred cooking of Sulawesi or the sago-based diet of Papua.
  • Rendang — the slow-cooked dry beef curry of the Minangkabau people of West Sumatra — was voted the world's most delicious food in multiple international polls, most notably CNN Travel's reader survey. It is cooked for hours until the meat becomes dark, intensely flavoured, and almost dry.
  • Tempeh — fermented soybean cake — was invented in Java at least several centuries ago and is one of Indonesia's most important contributions to global food. Unlike tofu, which is a Chinese invention, tempeh is uniquely Indonesian, produced by fermenting soybeans with a Rhizopus mould.
  • The spice islands of Maluku — the Banda Islands and the Moluccas — were the only source of nutmeg and cloves in the world for centuries, and control of these islands was the primary motivation for the Portuguese and Dutch colonisation of the Indonesian archipelago.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Fried rice with kecap manis sweet soy sauce, Indonesia's everyday dish.
  2. Beef slow-cooked in coconut milk and spice paste until dark and intensely flavoured.
  3. Skewered grilled meat served with a rich peanut sauce.
  4. Blanched vegetables and tofu dressed in a rich peanut sauce.
  5. A fragrant turmeric-yellow chicken soup with noodles and boiled egg.

More Mains

  1. Dozens of small West Sumatran dishes presented at once, pay for what you eat.
  2. Beef meatballs in a clear broth with noodles, a beloved street food.
  3. Chicken simmered gently in a mild coconut milk curry, a festive dish.
  4. A dark, earthy beef soup coloured and flavoured with black keluak nut.
  5. Rice cooked in coconut milk, served with a variety of side dishes.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. A sweet iced dessert of avocado, coconut, jackfruit, and condensed milk.
  2. Pandan-coloured rice cakes filled with molten palm sugar, coated in coconut.
  3. A green pandan crepe rolled around sweetened grated coconut.
  4. A thick, fluffy sweet pancake folded around chocolate, cheese, or nuts.

More Sweets

  1. Banana and sweet potato simmered in coconut milk and palm sugar syrup.
  2. Sesame-coated balls filled with sweet mung bean paste.
  3. Battered, deep-fried banana, a common sweet street snack.
  4. A soft coconut milk rice pancake, often topped with palm sugar syrup.

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. Fermented soybean cake sliced and fried until crisp.
  2. Fried tofu, often served with sweet soy and chili sauce.
  3. Mashed potato fritters mixed with herbs and fried golden.
  4. Crisp fried spring rolls filled with vegetables and sometimes meat.

More Snacks

  1. Crisp fried crackers, often shrimp-flavoured, served alongside meals.
  2. Steamed fish dumplings served with peanut sauce.
  3. Fried tofu and fish dumplings served with a rich peanut sauce.
  4. Spiced fish paste grilled in a banana leaf wrapper.
