Cuisine Guide

Japan

A codified, philosophically developed food culture built on seasonality, precision, and reverence for natural ingredients.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Japanese cuisine is one of the most refined, codified, and philosophically developed food cultures in the world — a tradition shaped by geography, Buddhism, aesthetics, and a deep reverence for natural ingredients. Japan's island geography provided an extraordinary abundance of seafood, and the Buddhist prohibition on meat-eating for much of its history drove the development of a plant and fish based cuisine of remarkable sophistication. Rice, introduced from China around 300 BCE, became the cultural and caloric center of the diet and remains so today.

Chinese influence was profound and formative — Buddhism, writing, and many cooking techniques arrived from the continent, including the use of soy sauce, tofu, and noodles. Portuguese missionaries in the 16th century introduced tempura, bread, and sugar confectionery. The Meiji Restoration of 1868 opened Japan to Western influence: beef eating — banned for over a millennium — was encouraged by the Emperor himself, leading to the development of distinctly Japanese Western-influenced dishes like tonkatsu, curry rice, and hamburger steak. These yoshoku dishes are now as Japanese as sushi.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Japanese cooking is governed by a philosophy of restraint, precision, and respect for ingredients. The concept of umami — the fifth taste, identified and named by Japanese chemist Kikunae Ikeda in 1908 — was born in Japanese cooking, where dashi broth (made from kombu seaweed and dried bonito) delivers a deep, savory richness that underpins almost every dish. Seasonality is not a preference but a discipline: ingredients eaten at their shun peak are considered superior in flavor and virtue.

The cuisine spans extremes: the exquisite minimalism of kaiseki, where each small course celebrates a single seasonal ingredient through precise technique; the boisterous communality of ramen shops and izakayas where salarymen eat and drink after work; the reverent silence of a sushi counter where a master chef's thirty-year training is delivered in a single piece. Japan has more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other country, and simultaneously some of the world's most democratic and delicious street and convenience food.

03

At the Table

  • Meals are eaten with chopsticks and the bowl lifted to the mouth — it is polite to slurp noodles and soup audibly, signaling appreciation to the cook.
  • Itadakimasu is said before eating and gochisosama deshita after — both express gratitude, to the food, the cook, and the living things that gave their life for the meal.
  • Rice is served plain alongside dishes, not mixed in — seasoning rice at the table is unusual. A meal without rice is considered incomplete by many Japanese.
  • Seasonality is fundamental — shun refers to the peak season of an ingredient, and eating foods at their shun is considered both better tasting and more virtuous.
  • Japan has the highest density of Michelin-starred restaurants of any country on earth, including more three-star restaurants than France.
  • Food presentation is an art form — the arrangement, color, and vessel of a dish are considered as important as its flavor. Ugly food is considered to taste worse.

Did You Know?

  • Japan introduced tempura — the technique of battering and deep-frying vegetables and seafood — directly from Portuguese missionaries in the 16th century. The word derives from the Portuguese 'têmporas.' Japan then perfected the technique far beyond its origins.
  • Ramen, now considered quintessentially Japanese, was introduced from China in the late 19th century. Each of Japan's regions has developed a radically distinct regional style — Sapporo's miso, Hakata's tonkotsu, Tokyo's shoyu, Kyoto's shio — reflecting local ingredients and tastes.
  • Japan consumes roughly 80% of the world's bluefin tuna supply. The single tuna auction at Toyosu Market in Tokyo sets world records annually — a single bluefin sold for $3.1 million in 2019.
  • Washoku — traditional Japanese cuisine — was inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2013, recognized for its expression of respect for nature, use of diverse fresh ingredients, nutritional balance, and connection to the Japanese New Year.
Food & Drink Reference

The dishes, sweets, and drinks that define the everyday table.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Vinegared rice paired with raw fish, shellfish, or vegetables — Japan's most globally recognized dish, requiring years of training to master.
  2. Wheat noodles in a savory broth — shoyu, miso, tonkotsu, or shio — each region developing its own signature style.
  3. A breaded, deep-fried pork cutlet, one of Japan's beloved yoshoku (Western-influenced) dishes.
  4. Vegetables and seafood battered and deep-fried to a light, crisp finish, perfected from a 16th-century Portuguese technique.
  5. A simmered hot pot of thinly sliced beef, vegetables, and tofu in a sweet-savory soy broth, often dipped in raw egg.

More Mains

  1. Bite-sized chicken pieces grilled on skewers over charcoal, a staple of izakaya drinking culture.
  2. Thin slices of meat and vegetables swished briefly through simmering broth at the table.
  3. Freshwater eel split, grilled, and basted repeatedly in a sweet soy glaze.
  4. An elaborate, seasonally driven multi-course meal built around meticulous technique and presentation.
  5. Buckwheat or thick wheat noodles served hot in broth or cold with dipping sauce.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Glutinous rice pounded into a chewy, elastic cake, eaten plain or filled with sweet red bean paste.
  2. Ice cream flavored with powdered green tea, prized for its bittersweet, earthy balance.
  3. Two small pancakes sandwiching a filling of sweet red bean paste.
  4. A fish-shaped cake, typically filled with sweet red bean paste, sold hot from street stalls.

More Sweets

  1. A jelly-like mochi made from bracken starch, dusted with sweet soybean flour.
  2. A dessert of cubed agar jelly, fruit, and sweet red bean paste, drizzled with syrup.
  3. Finely shaved ice topped with flavored syrups, a beloved summer treat.
  4. An umbrella term for delicate, artfully shaped traditional sweets often served with tea.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. A hand-shaped rice ball, often wrapped in nori and filled with pickled plum, tuna, or salmon.
  2. Ball-shaped batter snacks filled with diced octopus, grilled in a special molded pan.
  3. A savory cabbage pancake topped with sauce, mayonnaise, and bonito flakes, cooked on a griddle.
  4. Young soybeans boiled in the pod and lightly salted, a ubiquitous izakaya starter.

Small Dishes

  1. Pan-fried dumplings filled with seasoned ground pork and cabbage, crisp on one side.
  2. Bite-sized chicken marinated in soy and ginger, then lightly floured and deep-fried.
  3. Stir-fried wheat noodles with vegetables and meat in a sweet-savory sauce.
  4. A layered, lightly sweetened rolled omelette, sliced and served as a side or in bento boxes.
