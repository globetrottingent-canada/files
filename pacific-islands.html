Cuisine Guide

The Pacific Islands

Polynesia, Melanesia, and Micronesia — Fiji, Samoa, Tonga, Tahiti, Papua New Guinea — an ecologically intelligent food culture carried across the largest ocean on earth by history's greatest navigators.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Pacific Island cuisine is one of the oldest and most ecologically intelligent food cultures on earth — built over three millennia by seafaring peoples who settled the largest ocean on the planet, carrying with them a carefully curated set of food plants and animals selected for ocean crossing and island colonisation. The ancestors of today's Polynesian, Melanesian, and Micronesian peoples are among history's greatest navigators, and the food systems they established across tens of thousands of islands represent an extraordinary achievement of agricultural adaptation. At the heart of this food culture are the staple crops: taro, the sacred starchy root that sustained Pacific civilisations; breadfruit, one of the most productive food plants on earth; coconut, a complete pantry in a single palm; and the pig, brought by canoe across the open ocean.

European colonialism from the 16th century onwards transformed Pacific food cultures in ways that varied enormously by island group and colonial power — Tahiti's French food culture, the British meat-and-bread tradition in Samoa and Tonga, the German influence in Papua New Guinea. The most significant colonial transformation came in Fiji, where British planters brought approximately 60,000 Indian indentured workers between 1879 and 1916, creating a parallel South Asian food culture that now sits alongside the indigenous Fijian tradition. Canned and processed foods arrived with colonialism and World War II's military presence, and spam, corned beef, and white rice became embedded in Pacific diets.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Pacific Island cooking is defined by simplicity, abundance, and the umu — the underground earth oven that is the universal Pacific cooking method. Food wrapped in banana or taro leaves is placed on heated stones in a pit, covered with earth, and left to slow-cook in steam for hours. The umu is not merely a cooking technique but a social institution: its preparation is communal work, and the feast it produces is eaten communally. The flavours it produces — the smoky sweetness of taro steamed in coconut cream, the tender richness of pork cooked underground — cannot be fully replicated by other methods.

Coconut cream is the defining cooking medium and flavour of the Pacific kitchen — used to cook taro, fish, and meat, to make desserts, and drunk fresh from the nut. Kokoda (raw fish marinated in citrus and coconut cream, the Pacific ceviche) is the dish that most clearly expresses Pacific Island flavour philosophy. Kava, the ceremonial root drink prepared by mixing powdered piper methysticum with water, governs social life across most of the Pacific with a ceremony and protocol that varies by island group but everywhere signals welcome, respect, and community.

03

At the Table

  • The umu or imu — the underground earth oven — is the most universal Pacific cooking method, used from Samoa to Fiji to the Cook Islands. Heated stones are layered with leaf-wrapped food, covered with earth, and steamed for hours — the centrepiece of any feast.
  • Eating is communal and generous — Pacific Island hospitality requires that guests are fed first and fed abundantly. A feast table that does not overflow is considered a failure of generosity.
  • Kava is the social and ceremonial drink of most of the Pacific — a mildly sedative beverage from the ground root of the piper methysticum plant, drunk from a coconut shell bowl in ceremonies that govern social rank and welcome.
  • Taro is sacred in many Pacific cultures — in Samoa, Fiji, and Tonga, taro cultivation carries spiritual significance, and specific varieties are associated with chiefly status.
  • Coconut is not an ingredient but a complete pantry — providing drinking water, cooking cream, frying oil, flesh, fuel, weaving material, and timber. A Pacific meal without coconut in some form is unusual.
  • Regional variation within the Pacific is significant — Fiji's food shows strong Indian influence, Tahiti's shows French influence, and Papua New Guinea's cultural diversity produces dozens of distinct food traditions within one country.

05

Did You Know?

  • The Lapita people — the ancestors of all Polynesian cultures — undertook the greatest navigational feat in human history between roughly 1500 and 500 BCE, sailing outrigger canoes across thousands of kilometres of open ocean to settle Polynesia from Tonga to Samoa to Hawaii to Easter Island, bringing the canoe plants that define Pacific food.
  • Fiji has two parallel food cultures existing side by side — the indigenous iTaukei Fijian tradition of taro, cassava, and kokoda; and the Indo-Fijian tradition brought by Indian indentured workers from 1879 to 1916, who introduced curry, roti, and dal. Indo-Fijians now constitute roughly 37% of the population.
  • Breadfruit — the starchy tropical fruit that Captain Bligh was transporting on the Bounty when the famous 1789 mutiny occurred — is one of the most productive food plants on earth. A single tree can produce up to 200 fruits per year for decades.
  • Kava has been used in Pacific Island cultures for at least 3,000 years, producing mild sedation without impairing cognitive function. Vanuatu produces the most potent kava in the world and has the highest per-capita kava consumption of any nation.
Food & Drink Reference

The dishes, sweets, and drinks that define the everyday table.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Polynesia & Melanesia

  1. A whole communal feast of meat, fish, and root vegetables slow-cooked together in the underground earth oven.
  2. Raw fish marinated in citrus juice and coconut cream, Fiji's version of the Pacific-wide raw fish salad.
  3. Taro leaves parcelled around coconut cream (and often corned beef or onion) and baked until tender, a Samoan classic.
  4. Raw fish marinated in lime juice and coconut cream with vegetables, the Cook Islands' version of the regional raw fish dish.
  5. Corned beef or chicken wrapped and cooked with taro leaves in coconut cream, a common Samoan and Tongan weeknight dish.

More Mains

  1. Raw fish fermented in seawater until pungent, a strong-flavoured Tahitian delicacy.
  2. Raw tuna marinated in lime juice and coconut milk with vegetables, Tahiti's iconic dish.
  3. Grated taro, yam, or breadfruit mixed with coconut milk, wrapped in banana leaf and earth-oven baked — Vanuatu's national dish.
  4. Whole chicken slow-roasted in the underground earth oven alongside root vegetables.
  5. Whole pork roasted in the umu and served with steamed taro, a feast-day staple across the Pacific.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Cooked taro root pounded into a smooth, slightly fermented paste, a foundational Polynesian staple food.
  2. A dense, lightly sweet bread enriched with coconut milk and shredded coconut.
  3. A moist cake made with mashed ripe banana, popular across the Pacific for gatherings.
  4. A Samoan savoury dish of corned beef simmered in coconut milk, tomato, and onion.

More Sweets

  1. Rice slow-cooked in sweetened coconut milk until thick and creamy.
  2. Ripe papaya baked with sugar and lime until soft and caramelized.
  3. Raw fish marinated in citrus and coconut cream, the Tongan version of the Pacific-wide raw fish dish.
  4. Sweet dumplings served in a warm coconut caramel sauce, a Tongan festive dessert.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. A Pacific-adapted stir-fried noodle dish reflecting Chinese plantation-era influence across the islands.
  2. Thin slices of breadfruit fried until crisp, a popular island snack.
  3. Thin slices of taro root fried into crunchy chips.
  4. Soft buns filled or topped with sweetened shredded coconut.

More Snacks

  1. Ripe banana slices dipped in batter and deep-fried until golden.
  2. Small, round, pillowy fried dough balls, a beloved Samoan street food and church fundraiser staple.
  3. Taro leaves stewed in coconut milk, a Fijian side dish or light soup.
  4. A dense, chewy baked cake made from grated cassava and coconut milk.
