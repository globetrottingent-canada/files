Cuisine Guide

Philippines

The only Asian cuisine with a deep Iberian-Catholic imprint — sour, savory, and built on the philosophy of adobo.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Philippine cuisine is one of the most distinctive and layered food cultures in Southeast Asia — a tradition built on ancient Austronesian foundations, transformed by 333 years of Spanish colonial rule, briefly reshaped by American governance, and enriched by centuries of Chinese immigrant influence. The result is a cuisine unlike any other in the region: the only food culture in Asia with a substantial Iberian-Catholic heritage, expressed in dishes whose names and techniques come directly from Spain but whose ingredients, souring agents, and flavour sensibilities are entirely of the islands. The Philippine archipelago of over 7,600 islands encompasses extraordinary ecological diversity, and the regional variation in cooking is correspondingly vast.

The indigenous Austronesian foundation — the oldest layer of Philippine food — is characterised by the use of vinegar as a preservative and flavouring agent, coconut milk as a cooking medium, fermented fish and shrimp pastes for umami depth, and rice as the absolute dietary centre. This ancient layer predates any outside influence and gives Philippine cuisine its most distinctive characteristic: the sour-savoury combination that appears in adobo, sinigang, kinilaw, and the dozens of regional dishes built on the souring principles of vinegar, tamarind, kamias, and calamansi. Chinese traders and immigrants, present in the Philippines since at least the 10th century, contributed noodles, tofu, soy sauce, and the spring roll tradition that became lumpia.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Filipino cooking is bold, sour, savoury, and generous — a cuisine that does not shy from strong flavours or abundant quantities. The adobo method — braising any protein in a mixture of vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay leaf, and black pepper until the liquid reduces and the meat becomes tender and glossy — is so fundamental that it functions less as a single recipe than as a philosophical approach to cooking that can be applied to chicken, pork, seafood, or vegetables in dozens of regional variations. Every family considers its adobo definitive. Sinigang — the tamarind-soured soup of pork, prawns, or fish with vegetables — is the other great national dish, its sharp sourness as restorative as the most refined consommé.

Lechon — a whole pig slow-roasted over charcoal for four to six hours until the skin achieves an almost supernatural crackling crispness — is the great Filipino celebration food, the centrepiece of every fiesta, birthday, and major occasion. The best lechon comes from Cebu, where it is stuffed with lemongrass, garlic, and spring onions and eaten without sauce; in Manila it is traditionally eaten with liver sauce. Halo-halo — the layered dessert of shaved ice, evaporated milk, and a dozen sweet toppings including purple yam ube, red beans, coconut jelly, and leche flan — is perhaps the most theatrical dessert in Southeast Asia and the clearest expression of the Filipino love of abundance and contrast.

03

At the Table

  • Rice is eaten at every meal — sinangag fried rice for breakfast, steamed rice at lunch and dinner. The Filipino greeting 'kumain ka na ba?' — have you eaten yet? — reflects how central rice and meals are to social life.
  • Filipino food is characterised by a love of sour and salty flavours together — vinegar, calamansi, and fermented shrimp paste bagoong appear constantly, and the sour-savoury combination is the defining taste profile of the cuisine.
  • Boodle fights — military-style communal eating where food is laid directly on banana leaves and everyone eats with their hands — have become a popular Filipino social dining format, reflecting the indigenous kamayan hand-eating tradition.
  • Merienda — the afternoon snack break between lunch and dinner — is a serious institution. Sweet breads, kakanin rice cakes, and savoury snacks are eaten at 3pm by much of the country regardless of age or class.
  • Lechon — whole roasted pig slow-cooked over charcoal for hours until the skin is shatteringly crisp — is the centrepiece of every major Filipino celebration, from fiestas to birthdays to weddings.
  • Adobo is not a single dish but a cooking method — any protein braised in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, and bay leaf becomes adobo. Every family considers its own recipe definitive, and the dish varies by region, protein, and the ratio of vinegar to soy.

Did You Know?

  • The Philippines was colonised by Spain for 333 years (1565–1898), and the Spanish culinary imprint is deeper here than anywhere else in Asia. Dishes like arroz caldo, afritada, mechado, menudo, and caldereta all derive directly from Spanish cooking, Filipinized through local ingredients. The result is the only cuisine in Asia with a substantial Iberian-Catholic foundation — reflected even in the names of dishes.
  • Vinegar is the defining condiment of Filipino cooking — the Philippines produces a remarkable variety of native vinegars from coconut, sugarcane, nipa palm, and pineapple, each with its own character. Filipino vinegar culture is ancient, predating Spanish contact, and the use of souring agents to preserve and flavour food is the oldest layer of the cuisine.
  • Balut — a fertilised duck egg with a partially developed embryo, boiled and eaten from the shell — is the most internationally notorious Filipino street food, sold by vendors at night throughout the Philippines. It is a genuine delicacy to Filipinos, rich in protein and flavour, eaten with salt and vinegar. The embryo at the preferred stage is 16-18 days old.
  • Kare-kare — the oxtail and vegetables in peanut sauce stew — is believed to have been introduced to the Philippines by Indian sepoys who accompanied British forces during the brief British occupation of Manila from 1762 to 1764. The peanut-based sauce with coconut milk has no parallel in Spanish cooking, and its South Asian origin is the most plausible explanation for its presence in an otherwise Iberian-inflected cuisine.
Food & Drink Reference

The dishes, sweets, and drinks that define the everyday table.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Any protein braised in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, and bay leaf — a cooking philosophy as much as a single dish.
  2. A sharp, tamarind-soured soup of pork, prawns, or fish with vegetables, restorative and sour.
  3. A whole pig slow-roasted over charcoal for hours until the skin turns supernaturally crisp — the great celebration dish.
  4. Oxtail and vegetables in a rich peanut sauce, likely introduced by South Asian soldiers in the 1760s.
  5. A whole pork leg deep-fried until the skin is crackling crisp and the meat tender.

More Mains

  1. Thin beef slices marinated in soy sauce and calamansi, pan-fried with onions.
  2. A rich tomato-based beef stew with vegetables and liver sauce, Spanish in origin.
  3. Raw fish cured in vinegar and calamansi, the indigenous ancestor of ceviche-style dishes.
  4. A mixed vegetable stew flavored with fermented shrimp paste, a Luzon classic.
  5. A savory stew of pork simmered in blood and vinegar, deeply flavorful despite its intimidating reputation.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Shaved ice layered with evaporated milk, ube, red beans, coconut jelly, and leche flan — pure theatrical abundance.
  2. A dense, silky caramel custard, richer than the Spanish original it derives from.
  3. A soft, coconut rice cake traditionally baked in banana leaf over charcoal, a Christmas favorite.
  4. Steamed purple glutinous rice cake, a beloved Christmas-season street treat.

More Sweets

  1. A creamy dessert salad of young coconut, pandan jelly, and cream.
  2. A firm, milky coconut pudding studded with corn kernels.
  3. Sweet plantain rolled in spring roll wrapper, fried until caramelized and crisp.
  4. Flattened glutinous rice cakes coated in coconut and sugar.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. A boiled fertilized duck egg eaten from the shell with salt and vinegar, the most famous Filipino street food.
  2. Small, crisp fried spring rolls filled with seasoned ground meat.
  3. Hard-boiled quail eggs coated in orange batter and deep-fried, sold from street carts.
  4. Deep-fried fish balls on a stick, served with sweet and spicy dipping sauces.

More Snacks

  1. Grilled skewered chicken or pork intestine, a beloved late-night street snack.
  2. Warm silken tofu topped with sago pearls and caramel syrup, sold each morning by vendors.
  3. Small, soft steamed rice cakes, mildly sweet and often topped with grated coconut.
  4. An umbrella term for the many sticky rice cake sweets eaten during merienda.
