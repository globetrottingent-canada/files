Cuisine Guide

South Africa

A true rainbow-nation table — Khoisan, Bantu, Boer, Cape Malay, Indian, and British traditions united by fire and community.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

South African cuisine is one of the most genuinely diverse food cultures in the world — a true rainbow nation table reflecting the indigenous Khoisan and Bantu food traditions, the Dutch Boer settlers whose cooking became Afrikaner cuisine, the Cape Malay community descended from enslaved people brought from Southeast Asia and India by the Dutch East India Company, the Indian community brought as indentured labour to Natal by the British, and the British colonial tradition itself. No other country in Africa has synthesised so many distinct culinary traditions across such pronounced historical divisions, and the result is a food culture of extraordinary variety that is only now, thirty years after apartheid's end, beginning to be explored and celebrated as a coherent whole.

The indigenous Khoisan peoples of the Cape had a sophisticated food culture built on hunting, gathering, and coastal foraging — they contributed the knowledge of indigenous plants, the use of rooibos, and techniques for drying and preserving meat that directly influenced the biltong tradition. The Nguni and Sotho-Tswana Bantu peoples of the interior contributed the staple pap (maize porridge), fermented beverages like umqombothi sorghum beer, and the communal cooking traditions of the rural table. The Afrikaner Boer cooking tradition — potjiekos, biltong, boerewors, braai culture, and the dried rusks of the Great Trek — is the most distinctly South African culinary tradition, forged in the hardship of frontier life. The Cape Malay community produced the most spiced and technically sophisticated cooking in the country: bobotie, sosaties, and the sweet-spiced bredie stews reflect the Southeast Asian and South Asian culinary knowledge brought by enslaved people from Batavia, Ceylon, and the Malabar coast.

02

Character of the Cuisine

South African food is fire, community, and the extraordinary produce of a climatically diverse country. The braai is the central institution — not a quick weeknight meal but a social ceremony lasting hours, the fire started long before the food goes on, attended by the entire community. Boerewors — the coiled farmer's sausage of beef and pork spiced with coriander, cloves, and nutmeg — goes on first, and its sizzle and fragrance signals the beginning. The braai grill holds lamb chops, chicken pieces, sosaties, and whatever the season provides, each cooked to the braaimaster's specifications.

Biltong — the air-dried, spiced beef or game meat that is South Africa's most iconic snack — is eaten daily across all communities and generations. It is not jerky: the flavour, texture, and cultural weight are entirely different, and the coriander-vinegar cure that characterises it produces a savouriness that has no equivalent. Cape Malay cooking represents the haute cuisine of the South African table: bobotie, the minced meat dish baked with a savoury egg custard and fragrant with turmeric, apricot, and bay, is the national dish and one of the most interesting spiced meat preparations in any world cuisine. Malva pudding — the sticky, sweet, apricot jam-soaked sponge that appears at every celebration table — and the milk tart (melktert) with its silky cinnamon custard are the defining sweets of the Afrikaner tradition.

03

At the Table

  • The braai is not a barbecue — it is a social institution. A braai can last all afternoon, the fire is started hours before the food goes on, and the boerewors (farmer's sausage coiled in a spiral) goes on first as the crowd gathers. Managing the fire is a matter of honour.
  • Biltong is eaten as a daily snack across all communities — the air-dried, spiced beef or game meat is found in every petrol station, sports bar, and kitchen drawer in South Africa. It is not jerky: the texture, spicing, and curing method are distinct and superiority is not debated.
  • South African food is deeply communal and generous — ubuntu ('I am because we are') shapes the hospitality culture. A guest is fed without question and the table is expected to have more than enough.
  • The Cape Malay kitchen of the Cape Peninsula produces a spice palette unlike anything else in Africa — cinnamon, turmeric, cardamom, and tamarind in curry-style dishes that reflect the cooking traditions of the enslaved people brought from Malaysia, Indonesia, and India by the Dutch East India Company.
  • Braaibroodjie — the toasted cheese, tomato, and onion sandwich cooked directly on the braai grid — is the side dish that separates the serious braai from the casual one. Getting the cheese melted without burning the bread is a skill.
  • South African wine culture has matured rapidly — Stellenbosch, Franschhoek, and the Swartland produce world-class Chenin Blanc, Pinotage, and Cabernet Sauvignon, and the Cape Winelands are increasingly recognised internationally.

Did You Know?

  • Biltong has been made in southern Africa for hundreds of years — the indigenous Khoisan people dried meat to preserve it, and the Voortrekker Boers on the Great Trek of the 1830s depended on biltong as their primary preserved protein. The word comes from the Dutch 'bil' (rump) and 'tong' (strip). Modern biltong uses cider vinegar, coriander, salt, and pepper as its basic cure, but every family and butcher has their own variation. South Africa produces an estimated 30,000 tonnes of biltong annually.
  • Boerewors — the coiled fresh sausage of minced beef and pork spiced with coriander, cloves, and nutmeg — is legally protected in South Africa: the South African Bureau of Standards specifies that boerewors must contain at least 90% meat and no more than 30% fat, with beef as the primary component. Any product not meeting these standards cannot legally be called boerewors. It is the only South African food with a legal definition.
  • Rooibos tea — the caffeine-free herbal tea from the Cederberg mountains of the Western Cape — grows nowhere else on earth. The Aspalathus linearis shrub that produces it is endemic to a small area of the Cape Floristic Region, one of the world's most biodiverse ecosystems. Rooibos was used by the Khoisan for centuries before being commercialised in the 20th century, and South Africa holds a geographical indication for the name.
  • Bunny chow — a hollowed-out loaf of white bread filled with curry — was invented in Durban by the Indian community during apartheid. Indian workers who were forbidden from entering white restaurants had curry delivered in bread since no bowls or plates could be taken out for non-white customers. The bunny chow became so embedded in Durban street food culture that it is now eaten by all communities and considered the definitive Durban street food.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Braai & Classics

  1. The centerpiece social institution — a fire-grilled feast opened by the coiled, spiced boerewors sausage.
  2. Minced meat baked with a savory egg custard, fragrant with turmeric, apricot, and bay — South Africa's national dish.
  3. A hollowed loaf of bread filled with curry, invented in Durban's Indian community during apartheid.
  4. A slow-cooked stew of meat and vegetables, simmered for hours in a three-legged cast-iron pot over coals.
  5. Cape Malay-style spiced layered rice with meat, reflecting Southeast and South Asian heritage.

Cape Malay & More

  1. Snoek fish grilled over the braai fire, often basted with apricot jam glaze.
  2. A Xhosa dish of samp (crushed corn) and beans, slow-cooked into a hearty stew.
  3. A spicy vegetable relish served alongside pap, the staple maize porridge.
  4. A massive filled sandwich loaded with fries and sauce, a Cape Town street food icon.
  5. Marinated meat skewers with a Cape Malay-spiced glaze, often cooked on the braai.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. A sticky, sweet sponge cake soaked in apricot jam sauce, served at nearly every celebration.
  2. Braided fried dough soaked in cold sugar syrup, sticky and intensely sweet.
  3. A silky cinnamon-dusted custard tart, a defining Afrikaner sweet.
  4. A layered no-bake tart of caramel, cream, and crushed peppermint chocolate.

More Sweets

  1. A sweet, spiced vermicelli and milk dessert from the Cape Malay tradition.
  2. A baked date and nut pudding served with a warm caramel sauce.
  3. Small pastry tarts filled with apricot jam and topped with coconut meringue.
  4. Soft bread rolls grilled directly over the braai coals.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Classics

  1. Air-dried, spiced beef or game meat cured with vinegar and coriander — South Africa's most iconic snack.
  2. Grilled boerewors sausage served in a bread roll, a braai-side staple.
  3. Deep-fried dough rolls, served plain or filled with mince or jam.
  4. A toasted cheese, tomato, and onion sandwich cooked directly on the braai grid.

More Snacks

  1. A drier, cured version of boerewors, eaten as a chewy snack.
  2. Hard, twice-baked biscuits meant for dunking in tea or coffee.
  3. Maize porridge served with a spicy tomato-based relish.
  4. Small triangular fried pastries filled with spiced meat or vegetables, from the Indian community.
