Cuisine Guide

South Korea

A fermentation-forward peninsula cuisine built on kimchi, banchan, and the bold, balanced heat of gochujang and gochugaru.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Korean cuisine is one of the most distinctive and fermentation-forward food cultures in Asia — a tradition shaped by a peninsula geography of cold winters, four clear seasons, and a population historically dependent on preserving vegetables through long months of frost. The result is a cuisine defined by its banchan culture — the constellation of small fermented, pickled, braised, and seasoned side dishes that accompany every meal — and by kimchi, the fermented spiced cabbage that is simultaneously a condiment, a side dish, an ingredient, and a cultural symbol.

Chinese influence came through centuries of cultural exchange — Buddhism, Confucian food rituals, cooking techniques, and ingredients all arrived from the continent. Japanese colonization from 1910–1945 left complex culinary traces: some Japanese dishes were absorbed and Koreanized, while Korean food culture responded with fierce pride in its own traditions. The dramatic economic development of South Korea from the 1960s onwards created a modern food scene of global ambition, particularly visible in Seoul, which now ranks among the world's most exciting restaurant cities. The Korean Wave — the global spread of K-pop and Korean culture — has brought Korean food to an unprecedented global audience in the 21st century.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Korean cooking is built on fermentation, bold seasoning, and the deep satisfaction of balance. Gochugaru and gochujang bring fiery heat; doenjang delivers fermented soybean depth; sesame oil adds nutty richness; garlic is used with lavish generosity. The cuisine is not shy — flavors are direct and assertive. Yet within this boldness there is sophisticated balance: the heat of kimchi against plain rice, the richness of samgyeopsal against the brightness of perilla and ssamjang, the sweet-savory pull of bulgogi marinade.

The Korean table is democratic and communal by design — banchan are shared freely, grills are tended together, and the act of wrapping meat, rice, and condiments into a leaf parcel is inherently social. Soups and stews — jjigae — are central to every meal context, from the humble kimchi jjigae to the elaborate royal court soups. Korean fried chicken, tteokbokki, and street food culture have captured global imaginations, while the ancient traditions of temple food, royal cuisine, and regional home cooking remain deeply alive.

03

At the Table

  • Korean meals are served all at once — rice and soup in individual bowls, but all banchan side dishes placed in the center for everyone to share, replenished freely without extra charge.
  • Banchan culture is the defining feature of the Korean table — anywhere from two to a dozen small side dishes accompany every meal, from kimchi and seasoned vegetables to braised tofu and fish.
  • Koreans eat with a spoon and chopsticks together — the spoon is used for rice and soup, chopsticks for everything else. Lifting the rice bowl to your mouth is considered poor manners.
  • Jeong — the concept of affection and attachment — is deeply embedded in food culture. Cooking for someone is an act of love, and sharing food builds bonds that can last a lifetime.
  • Korean BBQ culture means that cooking at the table is social and collaborative — the grill is managed by the group, meat is wrapped in perilla or lettuce leaves with garlic, ssamjang, and kimchi.
  • Kimchi is made by every family according to its own recipe — regional styles, seasonal vegetables, and family ratios of gochugaru and fish sauce create a dish as personal as a fingerprint.

Did You Know?

  • Korea has the highest kimchi consumption per capita of any country — the average Korean eats around 18kg of kimchi per year, and the country produces approximately 1.5 million tonnes annually. Kimchi-making in late autumn — kimjang — was inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2013.
  • Gochugaru — the Korean red pepper flake used in kimchi and gochujang — was introduced from the Americas via Portuguese traders in the early 17th century. Within 200 years it had become so central to Korean cuisine that a Korea without gochugaru is now literally unimaginable.
  • South Korea has the highest number of convenience store food items of any country in the world — 7-Eleven and GS25 stores in Korea sell fresh triangle kimbap, hot oden fish cake soup, instant ramen cooked on-site, and rotating seasonal items that are taken seriously as food.
  • Makgeolli — Korea's ancient milky rice wine — is one of the oldest alcoholic drinks in East Asia, with records going back over 2,000 years. It was the drink of farmers and working people for centuries, and has seen a major craft revival in the 21st century.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. A hearty stew built on fermented kimchi, often with pork or tofu, a humble everyday staple.
  2. Rice topped with seasoned vegetables, meat, and a fried egg, mixed together with gochujang.
  3. Thin beef slices marinated in a sweet-savory soy sauce, grilled quickly over high heat.
  4. Marinated short ribs grilled over charcoal, a Korean BBQ centerpiece.
  5. Thick-cut pork belly grilled at the table and wrapped in lettuce with garlic and ssamjang.

More Mains

  1. A savory stew built on fermented soybean paste, tofu, and vegetables.
  2. Sweet potato starch noodles stir-fried with vegetables and beef in sesame oil.
  3. A spicy, bubbling stew built on soft silken tofu, often with seafood.
  4. Spicy marinated chicken stir-fried with cabbage and rice cakes on a hot griddle.
  5. A savory pancake studded with scallion and seafood, crisp at the edges.

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. Finely shaved ice topped with sweet red beans, fruit, and condensed milk.
  2. A griddled pancake filled with brown sugar, cinnamon, and nuts, a beloved winter street snack.
  3. Chewy rice cakes in countless forms, sweet or savory, central to celebrations.
  4. A traditional sweet fermented rice drink, often served as a dessert beverage.

More Sweets

  1. A deep-fried wheat cookie soaked in honey syrup, traditional at celebrations.
  2. A fish-shaped pastry filled with sweet red bean paste, a winter street food.
  3. Small glutinous rice balls coated in sweetened bean or seed powder.
  4. A category of savory pan-fried fritters, made with vegetables, seafood, or meat.

Snacks & Street Food

Street Food

  1. Chewy rice cakes simmered in a sweet-spicy gochujang sauce, Korea's most beloved street food.
  2. Rice and fillings rolled in seaweed, sliced into bite-sized pieces.
  3. Twice-fried chicken glazed in sweet, spicy, or soy-garlic sauce, a global Korean export.
  4. Fish cake skewers simmered in a savory broth, sold hot from street carts.

More Snacks

  1. A savory scallion pancake, crisp and simple, often paired with makgeolli.
  2. Rice and fillings rolled in seaweed, sliced into bite-sized pieces.
  3. Steamed, boiled, or fried dumplings filled with meat and vegetables.
  4. A category of deep-fried snacks including vegetables, seafood, and dumplings.
