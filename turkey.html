Turkey

An Ottoman synthesis at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East — kebabs, meze, and a millennium of palace refinement.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Turkish cuisine is one of the great culinary traditions of the world — a synthesis forged at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East over more than a millennium of empire. The Ottoman Empire at its height controlled territory from the Balkans to North Africa, from the Persian Gulf to Hungary, and its palace kitchens in Istanbul became one of the most sophisticated culinary institutions in history. The Topkapi Palace employed hundreds of specialist cooks organized into guilds, each responsible for a single category of food. The diversity of the empire's subject peoples — Greek, Armenian, Arab, Persian, Balkan, Kurdish, and more — flowed into the palace kitchen and from there into the broader Ottoman culinary tradition.

The nomadic Central Asian Turkic heritage provided the foundation: grilled meats, yogurt, and flatbreads are the oldest layer of the cuisine. Persian civilization contributed rice pilafs, sophisticated spice use, and confectionery. Arab culinary traditions added meze culture, stuffed vegetables, and the syrup-soaked pastry tradition. Byzantine Greek influence shaped the seafood and vegetable cooking of the Aegean and Marmara coasts. The Silk Road brought spices from the east, and the Columbian Exchange introduced tomatoes, peppers, and aubergines — now utterly inseparable from the cuisine — in the 16th and 17th centuries.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Turkish cooking is bold, deeply layered, and extraordinarily diverse — what is eaten in Istanbul bears little resemblance to the cuisine of the Black Sea coast, or southeastern Anatolia, or the Aegean. The southeastern tradition centered on Gaziantep is considered by many to be the finest regional cuisine in the country: a world of kebabs, spice pastes, pistachio-laden baklava, and slow-cooked meat dishes of remarkable refinement. Gaziantep has applied for UNESCO Creative City of Gastronomy status and is considered by Turkish food culture to occupy the same position that Lyon holds in France.

Aubergine is the king of Turkish vegetables — there are said to be over forty classic aubergine dishes, from the silky smoky purée of hunkar begendi to the olive oil-braised imam bayildi. Yogurt appears at every meal, used as a sauce, a marinade, a cooling accompaniment, and a drink. Manti — tiny handmade dumplings topped with garlicky yogurt and spiced butter — are the Turkish answer to ravioli, and like the best dishes of any great cuisine, the quality lies entirely in the care of the hands that make them. The kebab tradition encompasses dozens of distinct preparations, each specific to a region or city.

03

At the Table

  • Turkish meals begin with meze — a spread of cold and warm small dishes that can constitute an entire meal in themselves, accompanied by raki and conversation that lasts for hours.
  • Bread is sacred at the Turkish table — ekmek arrives before everything else and is replenished throughout. Throwing away bread is considered deeply disrespectful; stale bread is always repurposed.
  • Tea is Turkey's true national drink — çay, brewed black in a double teapot and served in tulip-shaped glasses with sugar cubes on the side, is drunk all day from morning to night.
  • The Turkish breakfast is legendary — a vast spread of cheeses, olives, tomatoes, cucumber, eggs, honey, clotted cream, pastries, and multiple jams eaten slowly over hours on weekends.
  • Raki protocol is specific — the anise spirit is always diluted with cold water, turning milky white, and always drunk alongside food, never alone. Drinking raki without meze is considered improper.
  • Hospitality is a matter of national pride — a guest must be offered tea immediately upon arrival and leaving without eating something is genuinely difficult to accomplish in a Turkish household.

Did You Know?

  • Turkey is the world's largest producer of hazelnuts, supplying roughly 70–75% of global production from the Black Sea coastal region. Turkish hazelnuts underpin the global chocolate and confectionery industry — Nutella, Ferrero Rocher, and virtually every major hazelnut chocolate product depends on Turkish supply.
  • Istanbul is the only city in the world built on two continents — and its food reflects this. The European side leans toward Ottoman palace cuisine, meyhane culture, and Balkan-influenced dishes; the Asian side has its own distinct neighborhood food traditions. The Bosphorus divides them, and a ferry ride between the two costs less than a glass of tea.
  • Yogurt was introduced to Europe from Turkey — the word itself is Turkish, and the technique of fermenting milk with live cultures was brought west along trade and migration routes. Turkish yogurt culture is ancient, with references to fermented milk dating back over 8,000 years in the region.
  • The döner kebab as served in Europe — meat shaved from a vertical rotating spit into flatbread — was largely popularized by Turkish immigrant workers in Germany in the 1970s. Kadir Nurman in Berlin is often credited with creating the German-style döner sandwich in 1972. It is now one of the most consumed fast foods in Europe.

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Hand-minced spiced lamb skewers grilled over charcoal, named for the southeastern city of Adana.
  2. Sliced döner meat over bread, drenched in tomato sauce, yogurt, and browned butter.
  3. Whole aubergine braised in olive oil, stuffed with onion and tomato, served cold or warm.
  4. Aubergine split and filled with spiced minced meat, baked until tender.
  5. Spiced grilled meatballs, found in dozens of regional variations across the country.

More Mains

  1. Tiny handmade dumplings topped with garlicky yogurt and spiced butter, the Turkish answer to ravioli.
  2. A boat-shaped baked flatbread topped with cheese, meat, or egg.
  3. A thin, crispy flatbread topped with spiced minced meat, often rolled and eaten by hand.
  4. A silky, smoky roasted aubergine purée, traditionally served under stewed meat.
  5. Meat and vegetables slow-cooked in a sealed clay pot, dramatically cracked open at the table.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Pastries & Sweets

  1. Layered phyllo pastry filled with chopped pistachios and soaked in sweet syrup, especially prized from Gaziantep.
  2. A crisp shredded pastry filled with melting cheese and soaked in syrup, served warm.
  3. A creamy baked rice pudding, often finished with a lightly caramelized top.
  4. A milk pudding with a caramelized, slightly burnt base, a distinctive Ottoman dessert.

More Sweets

  1. A soft, powdered-sugar-dusted jelly confection, flavored with rosewater, pistachio, or citrus.
  2. A dense sweet made from sesame paste or semolina, often served at both celebrations and wakes.
  3. A grain, fruit, and nut pudding made for a specific religious occasion, symbolizing abundance.
  4. A dense, chewy ice cream made with mastic and salep, famous for its elastic texture.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Meze & Pastry

  1. A shared spread of cold and warm small dishes that opens a Turkish meal, paired with raki.
  2. Flaky layered pastry filled with cheese, spinach, or meat, baked or fried.
  3. A crisp, sesame-crusted bread ring, sold from street carts across the country.
  4. Eggs scrambled with tomato, green pepper, and spices, a beloved breakfast dish.

Soups & Sides

  1. Vegetables or grape leaves stuffed with rice or spiced meat.
  2. A cooling dip of yogurt, cucumber, and mint, served as a meze or side.
  3. Thin pastry rolled around cheese filling and fried until crisp, named for its cigarette shape.
  4. A smooth, comforting red lentil soup, a staple starter across the country.
