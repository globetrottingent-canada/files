The Midwest

Chicago, Kansas City, Cincinnati, Wisconsin, and the Great Lakes — an immigrant-built heartland cuisine of brats, cheese curds, and deep dish, dismissed as plain by the coasts and quietly extraordinary.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

The American Midwest is the most underestimated food region in the United States — a vast agricultural heartland that feeds much of the world while its own food culture is dismissed as plain, heavy, or unremarkable by the coasts. This dismissal misses the point. The Midwest's food story is one of extraordinary immigrant diversity — German, Scandinavian, Polish, Czech, Slovak, Hungarian, and Slavic communities who settled the prairies and Great Lakes region in the 19th and early 20th centuries and brought brewing traditions, sausage-making knowledge, cheese-making skills, and baking cultures that shaped the American table profoundly. Chicago, the great Midwestern metropolis, was the meat-packing capital of the world and the city that industrialised the American food supply.

German immigrants left the deepest culinary mark — bratwurst, the beer hall tradition, rye bread, pickle culture, and the cheese-making knowledge that made Wisconsin America's dairy state all derive from German and Central European communities in Wisconsin, Minnesota, Iowa, and Ohio. Scandinavian immigrants brought lefse, kringle, and lutefisk to Minnesota and the Dakotas. Polish and Bohemian communities contributed kielbasa, pierogi, and kolache to Chicago, Detroit, and Cleveland. The Great Migration of African Americans from the South to Chicago and Detroit brought soul food traditions northward — fried chicken, greens, sweet potato pie — establishing Chicago's South Side as a northern extension of the Southern tradition.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Midwestern cooking is built on feeding people well, feeding them enough, and not making a fuss about it. Chicago has contributed some of America's most distinctive regional foods: the Chicago-style hot dog (never, under any circumstances, ketchup) and the Italian beef sandwich, dipped in cooking juices and topped with sweet or hot peppers — both fiercely local institutions.

Kansas City straddles the Missouri-Kansas border and claims its own distinct BBQ tradition — heavy on pork ribs and burnt ends, defined by a thick, sweet, tomato-and-molasses sauce applied generously rather than served on the side. Wisconsin's dairy culture has produced the cheese curd as the state's most distinctive food. Detroit-style pizza, baked in a rectangular steel pan with crispy caramelised cheese edges, has recently been recognised nationally as one of America's great regional pizza styles. And Cincinnati chili — cinnamon-spiced meat sauce over spaghetti — is the most polarising and fascinating regional dish in American food.

03

At the Table

  • The Midwestern table is honest, abundant, and unpretentious — portion sizes are large, prices are fair, and the expectation is that you leave the table full.
  • Friday night fish fry is a Catholic immigrant tradition across Wisconsin, Michigan, and the Great Lakes region — battered lake perch, walleye, or cod with coleslaw and tartar sauce, eaten every Friday from October to April.
  • The church potluck and the county fair are the two great institutions of Midwestern food culture — one a community gathering where every family brings a dish, the other a celebration of agricultural abundance and deep-fried excess.
  • Beer and bratwurst are inseparable in Wisconsin — brats simmered in beer and onions before a Green Bay Packers tailgate is a ritual with near-religious significance.
  • Chicago eating is serious and competitive — the city has fierce opinions about its hot dog (no ketchup, ever), its deep dish (not eaten daily — locals favor thin crust), and its Italian beef (dipped, with giardiniera).
  • Kansas City BBQ is its own tradition — thick, sweet, tomato-and-molasses sauce applied generously to ribs and burnt ends, the charred brisket tips elevated from scraps to the most sought-after item on the menu.

Did You Know?

  • Cincinnati chili is the most unusual regional chili in America — no beans, seasoned with cinnamon, allspice, chocolate, and Worcestershire, and served over spaghetti (two-way), with cheddar (three-way), beans (four-way), or onions (five-way). Invented by Macedonian immigrant Athanas Kiradjieff in 1922, it's consumed at roughly two million pounds a year.
  • Chicago-style deep dish pizza was invented at Pizzeria Uno in 1943 by Ike Sewell and Ric Riccardo — deep buttery crust, cheese directly on the dough, tomato sauce on top, and a long bake time that Chicagoans consider their own and New Yorkers refuse to call pizza.
  • The Midwest produces roughly 75% of the country's corn and soybean crop — Iowa alone produces more corn than the entire country of Mexico — yet this agricultural abundance has paradoxically given the region a reputation for plain food.
  • Wisconsin produces more cheese than any other US state — roughly 3 billion pounds a year across 600-plus varieties. The cheese curd, eaten fresh and squeaky within hours of production, is a state institution, and fried cheese curds are the essential Wisconsin bar snack.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Chicago & Kansas City

  1. A thick, buttery crust holding cheese directly on the dough with tomato sauce layered on top, baked low and slow in a deep steel pan — invented at Pizzeria Uno in 1943.
  2. The caramelized, deeply smoked tip pieces of brisket, once considered scraps and now the most sought-after item on a KC BBQ plate.
  3. A sweet-spiced meat sauce seasoned with cinnamon, allspice, and chocolate, ladled over spaghetti and layered with cheese, beans, or onion to order.
  4. Thinly sliced seasoned beef piled on a long Italian roll, dipped in its own cooking juices and topped with sweet or hot peppers.
  5. Battered lake perch, walleye, or cod served with coleslaw, rye bread, and tartar sauce — a Catholic immigrant tradition eaten every Friday from fall through spring.

Regional Classics

  1. Baked in a rectangular steel pan with a thick, airy crust and cheese pushed to the edges, where it caramelizes into a crisp lacy border.
  2. Bratwurst simmered in beer and onions before grilling — the centerpiece of Wisconsin tailgates and backyard cookouts.
  3. A yeasted bread pocket baked around a filling of seasoned beef, onion, and cabbage — a Nebraska staple with German-Russian immigrant roots.
  4. A thin pork cutlet, breaded and fried until it hangs well past the edges of its bun, served simply with pickles, onion, and mustard.
  5. A mild, flaky freshwater fish native to the Great Lakes, typically breaded and pan-fried or featured in the region's Friday fish fry.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. A double-crust pie filled with tart Michigan cherries, the state being the country's leading tart cherry producer.
  2. Light choux pastry shells filled with sweetened whipped cream, a Wisconsin State Fair icon sold by the tens of thousands each summer.
  3. A flaky, layered oval pastry filled with fruit or nut fillings and iced, brought by Danish immigrants and now a Racine, Wisconsin specialty.
  4. A peanut butter fudge ball dipped partway in chocolate, named for its resemblance to the nut of Ohio's buckeye tree.

Immigrant Pastries

  1. A dense, spiced steamed pudding made from wild persimmon pulp, an Indiana autumn tradition tied to the fruit's harvest.
  2. A soft butter cookie rolled in cinnamon sugar before baking, giving it a signature crackled top.
  3. A soft yeasted pastry with a well of fruit or sweet cheese filling, brought by Czech immigrant communities.
  4. A soft, thin potato-based flatbread rolled with butter and sugar, central to Norwegian-American holiday tables in Minnesota.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Chicago Classics

  1. Fresh squeaky Wisconsin cheese curds battered and deep-fried until crispy outside and molten within.
  2. A Vienna Beef frankfurter in a poppy seed bun topped with mustard, onion, relish, tomato, pickle, sport peppers, and celery salt — never ketchup.
  3. A hot dog coated in cornmeal batter and deep-fried on a stick, a staple of Midwestern county fairs.
  4. A whole dill pickle spear, battered and deep-fried, sold on a stick as county fair novelty food.

Fair & Bar Food

  1. Thin-sliced griddled beef and melted cheese in a long roll, a popular fair-circuit sandwich across the Midwest.
  2. Soft, salted baked dough twists reflecting the region's deep German baking heritage, sold at fairs, bars, and ballparks.
  3. Roasted, salted mixed nuts served in bowls at Midwestern taverns alongside beer.
  4. Whitefish, potatoes, and onions boiled together outdoors over an open fire, finished with a dramatic boil-over of fish oil — a Door County, Wisconsin tradition.
