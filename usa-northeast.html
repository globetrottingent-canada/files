The Northeast

New England, New York, Philadelphia, and the Mid-Atlantic — the oldest European-settled table in America, and the gateway through which immigrant cooking became the American table.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

The American Northeast is the oldest European-settled part of the United States and the entry point for the great waves of immigration that created American food culture as the world knows it. New England's culinary tradition is the oldest of any American region with European roots — a Colonial English and indigenous Wampanoag synthesis built on seafood, corn, beans, game, and the extraordinary produce of a short but intense growing season. The Pilgrims who arrived in 1620 survived their first winter largely because indigenous peoples taught them to cultivate corn and harvest local seafood. Thanksgiving commemorates this exchange, however imperfectly.

New York City is the gastronomic epicentre of American immigration food — the Ellis Island gateway through which millions of Europeans passed between 1892 and 1954, each wave leaving culinary traces that became American institutions. Ashkenazi Jewish immigrants from Eastern Europe created the bagel, the knish, the pastrami sandwich, and the deli counter. Italian immigrants from Naples and Sicily created New York pizza and the hero sandwich. Irish immigrants brought their own traditions, and later waves of Puerto Rican, Dominican, Chinese, and Korean immigrants added layers that make New York's food scene genuinely the most diverse in the world.

02

Character of the Cuisine

New England cooking is honest, seasonal, and built on the exceptional quality of its seafood and produce. New England clam chowder — cream-based, with potatoes and surf clams — is the definitive American chowder, and the cream-versus-tomato debate with Manhattan chowder reflects real cultural difference between Puritan New England and the immigrant pluralism of New York. The lobster roll — cold lobster meat dressed simply with mayonnaise in a top-split, butter-toasted bun — is the quintessential New England summer food.

New York's greatest food contributions are its immigrant inventions: the hand-folded pizza slice, the boiled-and-baked bagel, the pastrami on rye stacked obscenely high, and the dense, creamy New York cheesecake. Buffalo wings — invented at the Anchor Bar in Buffalo in 1964 — are one of America's most successful food inventions. Philadelphia contributes the cheesesteak, the soft pretzel, and the water ice, maintaining a fierce local food identity quite distinct from New York.

03

At the Table

  • New York is the world's greatest immigrant food city — every cuisine on earth is available within the five boroughs, and the city's most beloved foods (bagel, pizza slice, hot dog, deli sandwich) are all immigrant inventions thoroughly Americanized.
  • The New York pizza slice is its own food category — thin, foldable, sold by the slice from a coal or wood-fired brick oven, eaten standing on the street.
  • New England seafood is seasonal and serious — the lobster shack, the clam bake, the raw bar, and the chowder house are institutions tied to the short summer season.
  • The Jewish deli is a New York institution of enormous cultural weight — pastrami and corned beef on rye, matzo ball soup, latkes, and the particular atmosphere of the deli counter.
  • Brunch in New York and Philadelphia is a serious cultural institution — eggs Benedict, avocado toast, the Bloody Mary, and the bottomless mimosa, a format that has spread globally from its Northeast origins.
  • Philadelphia has its own fiercely local food identity — the cheesesteak is consumed with enormous local pride and strong opinions about whether Whiz, provolone, or American belongs on it.

Did You Know?

  • New York-style pizza was created by Gennaro Lombardi, an Italian immigrant who opened America's first pizzeria on Spring Street in Manhattan in 1905. The coal-fired oven and the practice of selling by the slice were American adaptations of Neapolitan pizza. New York water — very soft, low in minerals — is genuinely believed by pizza makers to affect the dough.
  • The American bagel originated in the Jewish communities of Eastern Europe, brought to New York by Ashkenazi immigrants in the late 19th century. For decades it was hand-rolled and boiled in malted water, a process controlled by the Bagel Bakers Local 338 union until automated machines arrived in the 1960s.
  • New England clam chowder and Manhattan clam chowder are genuinely different preparations whose rivalry reflects a real historical divide — New England is cream-based, Manhattan is tomato-based. A 1939 Maine state bill proposed making it illegal to add tomatoes to clam chowder, though it never passed.
  • The Philly cheesesteak was invented in 1930 by Pat Olivieri, a hot dog vendor in South Philadelphia who grilled chopped beef on his cart and served it in a hoagie roll. The debate between Pat's King of Steaks and Geno's Steaks, across the street from each other, has run for decades.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

New England

  1. A rich, cream-based soup of potatoes and surf clams — the definitive American chowder, distinct from tomato-based Manhattan style.
  2. A thin, foldable slice from a coal or wood-fired oven, eaten standing on the street — a distinct American descendant of Neapolitan pizza.
  3. Cold lobster meat dressed simply with mayonnaise in a top-split, butter-toasted hot dog bun — the quintessential New England summer food.
  4. Thin-sliced grilled beef and melted cheese (Whiz, provolone, or American) piled into a long roll — a fiercely defended Philadelphia institution.
  5. Clams, lobster, corn, and potatoes traditionally steamed together over hot stones and seaweed at the shore — a New England summer ritual.

New York & Mid-Atlantic

  1. Smoked, peppery cured beef stacked high on rye bread with mustard — a cornerstone of the New York Jewish deli tradition.
  2. Fried chicken wings tossed in cayenne hot sauce, served with celery and blue cheese dressing — invented at Buffalo's Anchor Bar in 1964.
  3. White beans slow-cooked with molasses and salt pork, a Colonial-era dish that gave Boston its nickname 'Beantown.'
  4. A whole steamed lobster served with drawn butter, traditionally eaten at a picnic table by the water.
  5. Fresh raw oysters served chilled on the half shell, a raw bar staple along the Northeast coast.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. A dense, rich cheesecake on a graham cracker base, baked until firm and famously creamy.
  2. Two layers of sponge cake filled with custard cream and topped with a chocolate glaze — technically a cake, not a pie.
  3. Two soft cake rounds sandwiching a sweet cream filling, a Maine and Pennsylvania Dutch favorite.
  4. Vermont's take on soft-serve ice cream, swirled with real maple syrup instead of standard vanilla.

More Sweets

  1. A double-crust pie filled with small, intensely flavored wild Maine blueberries.
  2. A soft butter cookie rolled in cinnamon sugar, giving it a signature crackled top.
  3. A slow-baked cornmeal pudding sweetened with molasses, a Colonial New England dessert dating to the earliest settlers.
  4. A cake doughnut made with fresh apple cider and warm spices, sold at Northeastern orchards each autumn.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Classics

  1. A dense, chewy boiled-and-baked bagel topped with cream cheese and smoked salmon (lox) — a New York Jewish deli classic.
  2. A steamed or grilled hot dog from a street cart, classically topped with spiced onion sauce and mustard.
  3. A large, chewy salted pretzel sold from street carts across New York and Philadelphia.
  4. Strips of clam meat, breaded and deep-fried until crisp — a New England seafood shack staple.

More Snacks

  1. A thick pastry filled with mashed potato, meat, or kasha, fried or baked — a Jewish deli and street food staple in New York.
  2. Chicken broth with a light, fluffy dumpling made from matzo meal — a Jewish deli comfort food.
  3. Lump crab meat bound lightly with breadcrumbs and egg, pan-fried until golden — a Mid-Atlantic seafood signature.
  4. Minced quahog clam meat mixed with breadcrumbs and seasoning, baked in the shell — a Rhode Island specialty.
