The South

BBQ, soul food, Cajun, Creole, and Low Country — the deepest, most historically layered food culture in America, forged at the intersection of Indigenous, West African, and European traditions.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

The American South has the deepest, most historically layered, and most culturally significant food culture in the United States — a tradition built at the intersection of three great culinary heritages: the indigenous peoples of the Southeast who cultivated corn, beans, and squash and introduced European colonists to smoking and hominy-making; the West African enslaved people who brought okra, black-eyed peas, rice cultivation, and the cooking knowledge that would define Southern food for centuries; and the European colonial settlers — English, French, Spanish, and Scottish — who were utterly dependent on African culinary labour and knowledge to make their traditions work in the New World.

The specific sub-traditions within Southern food are genuinely distinct. Cajun cooking is the rural French Acadian peasant tradition of the Louisiana bayou — brought by French settlers expelled from Nova Scotia in 1755, built on pork fat, the holy trinity of onion, celery, and bell pepper, and the extraordinary protein resources of crawfish, alligator, catfish, and frog. Creole cooking is the urban tradition of New Orleans — French, Spanish, African, Haitian, Sicilian, and Native American influences layered over three centuries into something entirely its own. Carolina Low Country cooking is built on the rice culture established by West African enslaved people from Sierra Leone and Senegal.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Southern cooking is defined by slow time, bold flavour, and the generous spirit of feeding people well. BBQ is the region's most iconic contribution to world food — cooking tough cuts of meat low and slow over wood smoke until collagen converts to gelatin. The regional variations are fiercely distinct: Texas privileges beef brisket over post oak; the Carolinas use whole hog or pork shoulder with a sharp vinegar-based sauce; Memphis applies dry rubs to ribs; Kansas City bastes everything in thick sweet tomato-based sauce.

Soul food — the cooking of African Americans, particularly in the rural South — is built on making extraordinary food from limited means: braised collard greens with smoked ham hocks, black-eyed peas, cornbread baked in a cast-iron skillet, and fried chicken in seasoned flour. New Orleans stands alone as America's most interesting food city, where Creole and Cajun traditions produce gumbo, jambalaya, crawfish étouffée, beignets, and the Sazerac cocktail in a context of genuine culinary depth.

03

At the Table

  • BBQ is a religion with denominations — Texas low-and-slow beef brisket over oak, Carolina whole-hog pork over hickory with vinegar sauce, Memphis dry-rubbed ribs, and Kansas City sauced ribs are genuinely distinct traditions whose partisans hold strong views.
  • Soul food and Southern food are related but not the same — soul food emerged specifically from African American cooking traditions, particularly after the Great Migration carried it north to Chicago, Detroit, and Harlem.
  • Sunday dinner is a Southern institution — fried chicken, biscuits, collard greens, cornbread, and sweet tea, eaten after church with extended family.
  • Sweet tea is brewed hot with sugar dissolved while hot, producing a sweetness that cannot be replicated by adding sugar to cold tea. Ordering unsweetened iced tea in rural Mississippi requires courage.
  • The po' boy and the muffuletta are New Orleans institutions — the po' boy a long sandwich of fried seafood or roast beef, the muffuletta a round Sicilian loaf stuffed with Italian meats and olive salad.
  • Cajun and Creole are neighbours but distinct — Cajun is rural, spicier, and lard-based; Creole is urban, more refined, butter-based, and tomato-forward.

05

Did You Know?

  • American BBQ is the most technically sophisticated form of outdoor cooking in the world — developed primarily by enslaved African Americans assigned the labour-intensive task of slow-cooking whole animals over pits for plantation events. The techniques and regional styles that define American BBQ today are products of African culinary knowledge applied over more than three centuries.
  • Gumbo — the emblematic dish of Louisiana Creole cooking — is a direct product of culinary creolisation: the word derives from the Bantu word for okra (ki ngombo), the dish is thickened with okra or filé powder (ground sassafras leaves used by the Choctaw), and the trinity of onion, celery, and bell pepper replaces the French mirepoix.
  • Tabasco sauce, produced on Avery Island, Louisiana since 1868, is found in over 195 countries. It's made from just three ingredients — tabasco peppers, salt, and vinegar — aged in repurposed Jack Daniel's barrels, and the McIlhenny family has produced it on the same island for over 150 years.
  • The Southern biscuit descends from the African technique of making quick bread with fat worked into flour, adapted through plantation cooking. The best are made with soft Southern wheat flour (White Lily is the brand of choice), lower in protein than Northern flours, producing a more tender crumb.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

BBQ & Soul Food

  1. Beef brisket smoked low and slow over post oak until the fat renders and the crust forms a dark, peppery bark — Texas's signature BBQ.
  2. A roux-based stew thickened with okra or filé powder, built on the trinity of onion, celery, and bell pepper — Louisiana's emblematic dish.
  3. Chicken pieces soaked, seasoned, and fried in well-seasoned flour until deeply crisp — a centerpiece of Southern Sunday dinner.
  4. Sautéed shrimp served over creamy, slow-cooked corn grits, a Low Country breakfast dish turned all-day favorite.
  5. Rice cooked down with meat, sausage, and vegetables in a single pot, seasoned with the Creole spice palette.

Cajun & Creole

  1. Whole hog or pork shoulder smoked over hickory and pulled, dressed in a sharp vinegar-based sauce rather than a sweet one.
  2. Crawfish tails smothered in a roux-based sauce with the holy trinity, served over rice — a Louisiana Cajun classic.
  3. Shrimp, corn, sausage, and potatoes boiled together in seasoned water and served heaped on newspaper-lined tables.
  4. A tenderized beef steak breaded and fried like fried chicken, smothered in peppery cream gravy.
  5. Fried chicken brushed with a fiery cayenne-oil paste after frying, served on white bread with pickles.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Pies & Puddings

  1. A single-crust pie of toasted pecans set in a rich corn syrup and egg filling.
  2. Layers of vanilla custard, sliced bananas, and vanilla wafers, chilled until the wafers soften into cake-like layers.
  3. Sweetened baked peaches under a biscuit-like or pie-crust topping, a Southern summer staple.
  4. Cubed bread baked in a rich egg custard and finished with a warm bourbon sauce — a New Orleans classic.

More Sweets

  1. A tart custard pie made with the juice of small Florida Key limes, set in a graham cracker crust.
  2. A spiced custard pie of mashed sweet potato, closely related to pumpkin pie but distinctly Southern.
  3. A creamy brown-sugar candy studded with pecans, set into flat disks — a New Orleans confectionery signature.
  4. Square, pillowy fried dough squares buried under powdered sugar — a New Orleans café classic best eaten with chicory coffee.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Classics

  1. Small deep-fried cornmeal fritters, traditionally served alongside fried fish or BBQ.
  2. Halved hard-boiled eggs with their yolks mashed with mayonnaise and mustard, then piped back into the whites.
  3. A spread of sharp cheddar, mayonnaise, and diced pimento peppers, often called 'the caviar of the South.'
  4. Raw peanuts boiled for hours in heavily salted water until soft — a roadside snack across the Deep South.

More Snacks

  1. Sliced okra coated in cornmeal batter and fried until crisp, cutting the vegetable's characteristic sliminess.
  2. A Cajun sausage of pork, rice, and seasonings, often squeezed straight from the casing at gas stations across Acadiana.
  3. Fried pork skin and fat cooked until deeply crisp, a Cajun and Southern snack sold by the bag.
  4. Flaky buttermilk biscuits smothered in peppery sausage cream gravy, a Southern breakfast staple.
