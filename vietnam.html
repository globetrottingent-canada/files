Cuisine Guide

Vietnam

Herb-forward, five-flavor cooking shaped by Chinese, Cham, Khmer, and French layers along a 1,650-kilometre coast.

01

Culinary Roots & Influences

Vietnamese cuisine is one of the most vibrant, fresh, and herb-forward food cultures in Southeast Asia — a tradition shaped by over a thousand years of Chinese dominance, three centuries of internal division, nearly a century of French colonialism, and the extraordinary biodiversity of a country that stretches 1,650 kilometres from subtropical north to tropical south. The Han dynasty's long occupation of northern Vietnam left deep marks: chopsticks, the wok, soy-based sauces, tofu, and noodles all arrived from China, though Vietnam absorbed and transformed them into something distinctly its own. The Cham kingdom of central Vietnam and Khmer influence in the south added further layers.

French colonial rule from 1858 to 1954 introduced baguettes, pâté, coffee, condensed milk, and long bone broth — all absorbed with extraordinary creativity. The bánh mì and phở both show unmistakable French influence at their foundations, yet both are now so thoroughly Vietnamese that their European roots are almost invisible. Vietnam's long north-south geography also means the cuisine splits into three distinct regional traditions: the subtle, soy-and-vinegar cooking of Hanoi and the north; the fiercely spiced, shrimp-paste-rich imperial cuisine of Huế; and the sweeter, coconut-milk-abundant, herb-generous cooking of the south.

02

Character of the Cuisine

Vietnamese cooking is defined by balance, freshness, and the brilliant use of contrasting textures and temperatures in a single dish. The five flavours — sour, bitter, sweet, spicy, and salty — are deliberately calibrated, often all present at once. Fresh herbs are not optional garnish but essential structural components: a bowl of phở is incomplete without its tableside plate of bean sprouts, basil, mint, and lime. The lightness of Vietnamese food sets it apart from richer neighbours — very little dairy, restrained oil, and an emphasis on grilling, steaming, and poaching over deep-frying.

Fish sauce — nước mắm — is the foundational seasoning, used with a confidence that transforms every dish it touches. Nước chấm, the dipping sauce of diluted fish sauce with lime, sugar, garlic, and chili, accompanies almost everything and varies by household. Rice appears in every form: steamed as cơm, ground into noodles as bún and bánh phở, pressed into crepes as bánh cuốn, and puffed as bánh tráng. Street food culture is extraordinary — an endless stream of vendors and stalls, each serving a single speciality perfected over decades.

03

At the Table

  • Meals are eaten communally from shared dishes placed at the centre — rice is served individually but everything else is shared, filling the table at once.
  • Fresh herbs are a structural component, not a garnish — a plate of mint, perilla, bean sprouts, and basil arrives alongside most dishes and is eaten freely throughout the meal.
  • Phở is a morning dish — eaten for breakfast or early lunch, not dinner. The best broth is made overnight and served through the morning.
  • Fish sauce is the salt of Vietnam — nước mắm, made from fermented anchovies, seasons virtually everything, alongside its dipping-sauce cousin nước chấm.
  • The bánh mì is a legacy of French colonialism so thoroughly Vietnamized that its baguette is now its own distinct thing — lighter, airier, crispier than its French ancestor.
  • Northern, central, and southern Vietnamese food are genuinely distinct — Hanoi's subtle cooking contrasts sharply with Huế's intensely spiced imperial cuisine and the south's sweeter, herb-abundant style.

05

Did You Know?

  • Phở — Vietnam's national soup of clear beef or chicken broth, rice noodles, and fresh herbs — is a relatively recent invention. It emerged in northern Vietnam around the early 20th century, most likely in Nam Định province, and shows clear French influence in its use of beef bones for broth. Before French colonialism, cattle were working animals, not food.
  • Vietnam is the world's second-largest coffee producer after Brazil, with a coffee culture entirely distinct from the European model. Robusta beans from the Central Highlands are brewed through a phin drip filter, served over condensed milk as cà phê sữa đá, or blended with egg yolk in Hanoi's famous cà phê trứng.
  • Nước mắm is produced along the Vietnamese coast, particularly on Phú Quốc island, using only anchovies and salt fermented for twelve to eighteen months in wooden barrels. The finest Phú Quốc fish sauce, with nitrogen content above 40°N, is a protected geographical-indication luxury product.
  • The bánh mì sandwich is one of the most successful culinary fusions in history — a French colonial baguette filled with pâté, pickled daikon and carrot, cucumber, coriander, chili, and various proteins. What began as colonial street food is now ranked among the world's greatest sandwiches.

06

Mains & Iconic Dishes

Classics

  1. Vietnam's iconic soup: clear, long-simmered beef broth over rice noodles with herbs, bean sprouts, and lime, traditionally eaten in the morning.
  2. A French colonial baguette, lighter and crispier than its ancestor, filled with pâté, pickled vegetables, herbs, chili, and a choice of meat.
  3. An intensely spiced beef and pork noodle soup from the imperial city of Huế, seasoned with lemongrass, chili oil, and shrimp paste.
  4. Southern-style broken rice served with grilled pork chop, a fried egg, pickled vegetables, and fish sauce — a beloved everyday lunch.
  5. A turmeric-tinted, coconut-milk rice crepe filled with pork, shrimp, and bean sprouts, folded and eaten wrapped in lettuce and herbs.

More Mains

  1. Hanoi's classic: char-grilled pork patties and slices served over cold rice vermicelli with herbs and a sweet-sour dipping sauce.
  2. Fish braised in a clay pot with caramelized sugar and fish sauce, producing a deeply savory, slightly sweet, dark sauce.
  3. Translucent rice-paper rolls of shrimp, pork, rice noodles, and herbs, served fresh and unfried with peanut or fish sauce dip.
  4. A Da Nang specialty of turmeric-dyed noodles in a light, intensely flavored broth with shrimp, pork, herbs, and toasted rice crackers.
  5. Cubed beef seared hot and fast in a wok — the name refers to the tossing motion — served over rice or salad with a peppery lime dip.
07

Puddings & Sweets

Classics

  1. A layered dessert of mung bean, red bean, and pandan jelly over coconut milk and crushed ice — a beloved street-side treat.
  2. A French-influenced egg custard with caramel sauce, often served with condensed milk or strong coffee.
  3. Deep-fried glutinous rice balls coated in sesame seeds with a sweet mung bean filling.
  4. Sweetened glutinous rice served with fresh ripe mango and a drizzle of coconut cream.

More Sweets

  1. Chewy glutinous rice balls filled with palm sugar, boiled and served in a light ginger syrup.
  2. Coconut ice cream served inside a hollowed, frozen coconut shell — a favorite hot-weather treat.
  3. A baked, airy sponge cake with a distinctive honeycomb texture from fermented rice flour and coconut milk.
  4. A warm dessert soup of banana, sago pearls, and coconut milk, often topped with roasted peanuts.
08

Snacks & Street Food

Street Classics

  1. Translucent rice-paper rolls of shrimp, pork, rice noodles, and herbs, served fresh and unfried with peanut or fish sauce dip.
  2. Delicate steamed rice-flour sheets filled with minced pork and mushroom, topped with fried shallots and dipping sauce.
  3. A tomato-based noodle soup with a rustic crab and tofu patty, tamarind-soured broth, and fresh herbs.
  4. Nicknamed 'Vietnamese pizza' — rice paper grilled over charcoal with egg, dried shrimp, scallion, and chili.

More Street Food

  1. Crispy fried spring rolls packed with pork, crab meat, mushroom, and vermicelli, wrapped in lettuce and herbs to eat.
  2. Bite-sized crispy rice-flour pancakes cooked in a special mold, topped with shrimp and served with herbs and dipping sauce.
  3. A hollow, puffed, deep-fried donut coated in sesame seeds — a common breakfast or afternoon street snack.
  4. A fertilized duck egg with a developing embryo, boiled and eaten from the shell with herbs, salt, and pepper.
